# Bible Study: Colossians 3:5-11

## Putting Off the Old Self: From Death to Renewal

A study of Colossians 3:5-11 using the methodology from *Hermeneutics and Exegesis for Laypeople* by Clayton Chancey.

---

## Passage Summary

In Colossians 3:5-11, Paul commands believers to decisively put to death the sins of their former pagan life — both the internal sins of desire (immorality, impurity, passion, evil desire, greed) and the external sins of speech and hostility (anger, wrath, malice, slander, abusive speech, lying). These commands are grounded in what has already happened: the believer has stripped off the old self and put on the new self, who is being progressively renewed by God according to His image. The passage climaxes with the declaration that in this new humanity, all human divisions — ethnic, religious, cultural, socioeconomic — are abolished, because "Christ is all, and in all."

## Key Findings

### The Indicative-Imperative Dynamic
Paul does not simply command moral improvement. His ethical imperatives ("put to death," "put aside") are grounded in theological indicatives ("you have died," "you have been raised," "you laid aside the old self," "you have put on the new self"). What God has already done in Christ is the foundation and motivation for what the believer must now do.

### Two Categories of Sin
The passage contains two vice lists targeting different dimensions of sin:
- **Verse 5**: Sins of desire and appetite (immorality, impurity, passion, evil desire, greed)
- **Verses 8-9**: Sins of speech and social hostility (anger, wrath, malice, slander, abusive speech, lying)

The progression moves from the internal root to the external expression, from sins against God to sins against neighbor.

### Greed as Idolatry
Paul identifies greed (πλεονεξία) as idolatry — not merely similar to idolatry, but identical with it. Greed elevates a created thing to the place of God. This reveals that sin is fundamentally misplaced worship.

### Sanctification as Image-Restoration
The new self is "being renewed to a true knowledge according to the image of the One who created him" (v. 10). This echoes Genesis 1:26-27. Sanctification is not arbitrary rule-following; it is the progressive restoration of God's original design for humanity. God is making believers more fully human, not less.

### Christ Is All, and in All
The passage climaxes with the most comprehensive declaration of Christ's supremacy in the ethical section: "Christ is all, and in all" (v. 11). In the new humanity, all human distinctions — ethnic (Greek/Jew), religious (circumcised/uncircumcised), cultural (barbarian/Scythian), socioeconomic (slave/free) — are rendered irrelevant. Christ alone determines identity, value, and standing.

### Against the Colossian Heresy
This passage implicitly refutes the false teaching in Colossae. The false teachers offered human-made rules, ascetic practices, and mystical experiences as the path to spiritual maturity (2:16-23). Paul counters: real transformation comes from union with Christ, not from self-made religion. The "severe treatment of the body" has "no value against fleshly indulgence" (2:23); only dying and rising with Christ produces genuine change.

## Methodology

This study follows the Pyramid of Interpretation:

1. **Doctrine of Inspiration** — Affirming Verbal Plenary Inspiration: this text is God-breathed and bears God's authority, while also being genuinely authored by Paul.
2. **Hermeneutics** — Using the Literal Historical Grammatical hermeneutic: a commonsense reading accounting for historical setting and linguistic choices to determine Paul's originally intended meaning.
3. **Exegesis** — Extracting meaning through literary context (Colossians as a whole, the parallel in Ephesians), historical context (Paul's imprisonment, the Colossian situation), and grammatical context (Greek word studies, conjunction analysis).
4. **Interpretation** — Arriving at authorial intent before deriving theological significance and personal application.
