# Colossians 3:5-11 — Exegetical Resources and Notes

This document records observations, questions, and findings from the use of exegetical tools, following the methodology in Chapter 7 of *Hermeneutics and Exegesis for Laypeople*.

---

## Key Greek Words and Phrases

### Verse 5

- **Νεκρώσατε** (*nekrōsate*) — "Put to death." Aorist active imperative of νεκρόω. The aorist tense conveys urgency and decisiveness: this is not a gradual, ongoing process but a decisive act. The same root appears in Romans 4:19 (Abraham's body "as good as dead") — it means to render something lifeless, powerless, inactive. Paul does not say "manage" or "control" — he says "kill."

- **τὰ μέλη τὰ ἐπὶ τῆς γῆς** (*ta melē ta epi tēs gēs*) — "the members which are on earth." Literally "the members upon the earth." Paul is not speaking of literal body parts but using the body as a metaphor for sinful behaviors and desires rooted in earthly existence. The apposition that follows (immorality, impurity, etc.) defines what these "members" are.

- **πορνείαν** (*porneian*) — "Immorality/sexual immorality." A broad term encompassing all sexual activity outside the covenant of marriage. Derived from πόρνη (prostitute). In the Greco-Roman world, visiting prostitutes and engaging in extramarital sexual activity was culturally normative, especially for men.

- **ἀκαθαρσίαν** (*akatharsian*) — "Impurity/uncleanness." Broader than πορνεία; includes moral filthiness and defilement of any kind. In the Old Testament, ceremonial uncleanness excluded a person from worship. Paul uses the concept morally.

- **πάθος** (*pathos*) — "Passion." In the New Testament, this term almost always carries a negative connotation: uncontrolled, degrading passion (cf. Rom 1:26, "degrading passions"; 1 Thess 4:5, "lustful passion").

- **ἐπιθυμίαν κακήν** (*epithymian kakēn*) — "Evil desire." ἐπιθυμία by itself is neutral (it simply means "strong desire"), but qualified by κακήν it becomes "evil desire." The progression from πορνεία to ἐπιθυμίαν κακήν moves from specific outward acts to the internal root.

- **πλεονεξίαν, ἥτις ἐστὶν εἰδωλολατρία** (*pleonexian, hētis estin eidōlolatria*) — "Greed, which is idolatry." πλεονεξία literally means "the desire to have more." Paul's identification of greed as idolatry is theologically significant: greed elevates a created thing (wealth, possessions, status) to the place of God. The relative pronoun ἥτις ("which is of such a nature that") indicates that this equivalence is inherent to greed's nature — it is not merely similar to idolatry; it *is* idolatry.

### Verse 6

- **δι' ἅ** (*di' ha*) — "Because of which things." The preposition διά with the accusative indicates cause. God's wrath comes *because of* these specific sins.

- **ἡ ὀργὴ τοῦ θεοῦ** (*hē orgē tou theou*) — "The wrath of God." Not irrational rage but the settled, just response of a holy God to sin. The present tense verb ἔρχεται ("is coming / will come") may point to both present manifestations of divine displeasure (cf. Rom 1:18, 24, 26, 28) and the eschatological day of judgment.

### Verse 7

- **περιεπατήσατέ** (*periepatēsate*) — "You walked." The aorist tense looks back on their former way of life as a completed chapter. "Walking" is a common biblical metaphor for one's manner of life.

- **ἐζῆτε** (*ezēte*) — "You were living." The imperfect tense describes their former continuous state. They were habitually living in these sins. The contrast between the aorist "walked" and the imperfect "were living" captures both the specific acts and the ongoing condition of their pre-Christian life.

### Verse 8

- **νυνὶ δέ** (*nyni de*) — "But now." A strong adversative marker. This phrase regularly marks the turning point in Paul's letters between the believer's past condition and present reality (cf. Rom 3:21; 6:22; 7:6; Eph 2:13). It is the hinge of the passage.

- **ἀπόθεσθε** (*apothesthe*) — "Put aside / put off." Aorist middle imperative of ἀποτίθημι. The verb evokes the image of removing clothing — stripping off dirty garments. This clothing metaphor runs throughout the passage (cf. "laid aside" in v. 9, "put on" in v. 10) and connects to the broader "put off the old / put on the new" imagery.

- **ὀργήν, θυμόν, κακίαν, βλασφημίαν, αἰσχρολογίαν** — The second vice list:
  - **ὀργήν** (*orgēn*) — "Anger." A settled, deep-seated hostility. (The same root as God's "wrath" in v. 6, though human anger is not equivalent to divine wrath.)
  - **θυμόν** (*thymon*) — "Wrath." A more explosive, passionate outburst of anger. Distinguished from ὀργή by its intensity and impulsiveness.
  - **κακίαν** (*kakian*) — "Malice." The general disposition of ill will; wickedness directed toward others.
  - **βλασφημίαν** (*blasphēmian*) — "Slander." Speech that damages another's reputation. Against God, this is "blasphemy"; against humans, it is "slander."
  - **αἰσχρολογίαν** (*aischrologian*) — "Abusive speech / filthy language." Speech that is shameful, obscene, or degrading. Only used here in the New Testament.

### Verse 9

- **μὴ ψεύδεσθε εἰς ἀλλήλους** (*mē pseudesthe eis allēlous*) — "Do not lie to one another." Present middle imperative with μή — a command to stop an ongoing practice or to not begin one. Lying destroys the trust that community requires.

- **ἀπεκδυσάμενοι** (*apekdysamenoi*) — "Having laid aside / having stripped off." Aorist middle participle of ἀπεκδύομαι — a rare, emphatic compound verb meaning to completely strip off, as one would remove a garment entirely. The aorist tense points to a completed action: at conversion, the old self was decisively removed. This same verb appears in 2:15, where Christ "disarmed" (literally "stripped off") the rulers and authorities. The old self is stripped off just as Christ stripped the powers.

### Verse 10

- **ἐνδυσάμενοι τὸν νέον** (*endysamenoi ton neon*) — "Having put on the new self." The counterpart to ἀπεκδυσάμενοι. The clothing metaphor continues: the old garment is removed and a new one is put on. The aorist tense again points to a completed act at conversion.

- **τὸν ἀνακαινούμενον** (*ton anakainoumenon*) — "Who is being renewed." Present passive participle. While the initial putting on happened decisively at conversion (aorist), the renewal is **ongoing** (present tense) and **accomplished by God** (passive voice — a "divine passive"). Sanctification is God's continuing work in the believer.

- **εἰς ἐπίγνωσιν** (*eis epignōsin*) — "To a true/full knowledge." ἐπίγνωσις is an intensified form of γνῶσις (knowledge), indicating deep, experiential, full knowledge. This is significant given the Colossian context: the false teachers claimed special, secret knowledge. Paul says the real knowledge comes through renewal in Christ.

- **κατ' εἰκόνα τοῦ κτίσαντος αὐτόν** (*kat' eikona tou ktisantos auton*) — "According to the image of the One who created him." A clear echo of Genesis 1:26-27 (LXX: κατ' εἰκόνα). The "One who created" is God. Sanctification is image-restoration — a new creation (cf. 2 Cor 5:17).

### Verse 11

- **ὅπου** (*hopou*) — "Where." Introduces the sphere in which this renewal takes place — the new humanity in Christ.

- **οὐκ ἔνι** (*ouk eni*) — "There is no" or "there cannot be." A strong negation. These distinctions do not merely fade — they are rendered irrelevant in Christ.

- **Ἕλλην καὶ Ἰουδαῖος, περιτομὴ καὶ ἀκροβυστία, βάρβαρος, Σκύθης, δοῦλος, ἐλεύθερος** — Paul lists four pairs/categories of human division:
  - **Greek and Jew** — ethnic/cultural division
  - **Circumcised and uncircumcised** — religious/ritual division
  - **Barbarian, Scythian** — cultural/civilizational division (Scythians were considered the most uncivilized of barbarians by Greeks and Romans)
  - **Slave and freeman** — socioeconomic division

- **ἀλλὰ [τὰ] πάντα καὶ ἐν πᾶσιν Χριστός** (*alla [ta] panta kai en pasin Christos*) — "But Christ is all, and in all." The strongest possible statement of Christ's supremacy and sufficiency. Christ is everything that matters (τὰ πάντα) and is present in every believer (ἐν πᾶσιν). This is the climax of the passage and indeed of the entire ethical section.

## Questions Arising from Study

1. **What is the relationship between the two vice lists?** The first (v. 5) focuses on desire/appetite; the second (v. 8) focuses on anger/speech. Is there an intentional progression — from sins against self and God to sins against neighbor?

2. **Why does Paul single out greed as idolatry?** Other sins in the list could also be called idolatrous. Is Paul responding to a specific problem in Colossae, or is greed uniquely revelatory of sin's nature as misplaced worship?

3. **What is the relationship between "put to death" (v. 5) and "put aside" (v. 8)?** Are these synonymous commands, or does the stronger language of v. 5 ("put to death") indicate a more radical action than "put aside"? One possibility: desires must be killed at the root; behaviors must be removed like soiled garments.

4. **How does the "divine passive" of v. 10 ("being renewed") relate to the active imperatives of vv. 5 and 8?** If God is the one doing the renewing, what is the believer's role? Answer: both are true simultaneously. God renews; the believer cooperates by actively putting to death sin and putting on the new self. This is the classic Reformed understanding of sanctification as "synergistic" in practice (God works, we work) but "monergistic" in power (God alone provides the ability).

5. **What is the significance of listing "Scythian" alongside "barbarian"?** Scythians were nomadic warriors from the Black Sea region known for extreme brutality. They represented the absolute bottom of the Greco-Roman cultural hierarchy. By including them, Paul is saying: even the most despised, uncivilized person is fully welcome in Christ. No one is too far gone.

## Parallel Passage Comparison: Colossians 3:5-11 and Ephesians 4:17-32

These passages were likely written at the same time and share extensive thematic and verbal parallels.

| Theme | Colossians 3 | Ephesians 4 |
|---|---|---|
| Former way of life | "in them you also once walked" (3:7) | "walk no longer just as the Gentiles also walk" (4:17) |
| Impurity/greed | "immorality, impurity... greed" (3:5) | "have given themselves over to sensuality for the practice of every kind of impurity with greediness" (4:19) |
| Put off the old self | "you laid aside the old self" (3:9) | "lay aside the old self" (4:22) |
| Put on the new self | "have put on the new self" (3:10) | "put on the new self" (4:24) |
| Renewed in God's image | "being renewed... according to the image of the One who created him" (3:10) | "created in righteousness and holiness of the truth" (4:24) |
| Vice list: anger/wrath | "anger, wrath, malice" (3:8) | "Let all bitterness and wrath and anger... be put away from you" (4:31) |
| Vice list: speech | "slander, abusive speech... do not lie" (3:8-9) | "laying aside falsehood, speak truth" (4:25); "Let no unwholesome word proceed from your mouth" (4:29) |
| Forgiveness/grace | (In 3:13: "just as the Lord forgave you") | "Be kind to one another... forgiving each other, just as God in Christ also has forgiven you" (4:32) |

The parallels confirm that Paul was addressing the same ethical framework in both letters, adapted to the specific needs of each congregation. Colossians emphasizes the sufficiency of Christ (against the Colossian heresy), while Ephesians emphasizes the unity of the church (the "mystery" of Jew-Gentile inclusion).
