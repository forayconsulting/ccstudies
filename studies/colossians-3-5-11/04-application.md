# Colossians 3:5-11 — Application

This file develops the personal and corporate application of Colossians 3:5-11, building on the exegetical work in the previous study files. Following the methodology, application is reserved until after exegesis. What follows is grounded in authorial intent, theological significance, and the bridging questions of Question 4 ("How does this truth apply to me?").

---

## Bridging the Gap: From Their World to Ours

### My Relationship to Paul

Paul writes as an apostle — one directly commissioned by the risen Christ. I do not share his office, but his inspired letters carry the authority of God's Word and are binding on all believers (2 Tim 3:16-17). His commands in this passage are not suggestions from a respected teacher; they are imperatives delivered under divine authority.

### My Relationship to the Colossians

Like the Colossians, I am a Gentile believer living in a culture that normalizes the sins Paul lists. Like them, I face teachings that would supplement the sufficiency of Christ — whether through political ideology, emotional experience, moral performance, or cultural conformity. My spiritual situation is remarkably parallel to theirs.

### Differences Between Our Worlds

- The Colossians were first-generation converts out of paganism. Many had personally participated in idolatrous worship and its attendant immorality. My cultural background is different in specifics but not in kind.
- The "Colossian heresy" (angel worship, dietary legalism, ascetic mysticism) is not prevalent today, but its underlying pattern is: the temptation to add something to Christ.
- The social categories of v. 11 (Greek/Jew, barbarian/Scythian, slave/free) were the fault lines of the ancient world. Today's divisions — racial, political, economic, generational — are different in specifics but identical in nature.

### My Relationship to God

No different from theirs. As a New Testament believer, I have been justified by faith in Christ, indwelt by the Holy Spirit, and am being sanctified through union with Christ. The same indicative-imperative dynamic applies to me directly. The commands of this passage are mine to obey in the same way and for the same reasons.

## Application from the Text's Three Movements

The outline identified three movements in this passage: (1) put to death the sins of desire (vv. 5-7), (2) put aside the sins of speech (vv. 8-9a), and (3) the basis — the old self removed, the new self renewed (vv. 9b-11). Application flows from each.

---

### Movement 1: Put to Death the Sins of Desire (vv. 5-7)

**The command**: "Consider the members of your earthly body as dead to immorality, impurity, passion, evil desire, and greed, which amounts to idolatry."

**The motivation**: God's wrath is coming because of these sins (v. 6), and the Colossians themselves once lived this way (v. 7).

#### Personal Application

1. **The standard is death, not management.** The verb "put to death" (nekrosate) is an aorist imperative — a decisive, once-for-all action. Paul does not say "reduce," "monitor," or "be careful about." He says kill. This means I cannot negotiate with sinful desires or try to keep them at a manageable level. The goal is not moderation of sin but elimination of it. When I notice myself rationalizing, bargaining with, or minimizing a sinful desire, I am failing to obey this command.

2. **The list moves from external act to internal root.** Paul begins with sexual immorality (the outward act) and moves inward through impurity, passion, and evil desire to greed (the root disposition). This progression reveals that sin is not merely a behavior problem — it is a worship problem. The practical implication: dealing with sinful behavior at the surface level is insufficient. I must trace sinful actions back to the desires and affections that produce them. If I struggle with a recurring sinful behavior, I should ask: what desire or affection is driving this? What am I worshipping instead of God?

3. **Greed is idolatry — not metaphorically, but actually.** Paul's identification of greed as idolatry (v. 5) means that the desire to have more — more money, more status, more comfort, more security, more approval — is not merely a character flaw. It is the elevation of a created thing to the place of God. This strikes at the heart of a consumer culture. I must examine where I am placing my hope, security, and satisfaction. If the answer is anything other than Christ, I am committing the sin Paul equates with idolatry.

4. **The motivation is both future and past.** God's coming wrath (v. 6) is not a scare tactic; it is a reality that should create urgency. And the reminder that "you also once walked" in these sins (v. 7) is a call to remember what I was delivered from. Both of these motivations should produce gratitude and resolve — gratitude that I have been rescued, and resolve not to return to the life I was saved out of.

#### Questions for Self-Examination

- Where am I managing sin rather than killing it?
- What desires am I treating as needs that are actually expressions of greed?
- Am I placing my security, identity, or satisfaction in anything other than Christ?
- Do I take the reality of God's coming judgment seriously, or have I become comfortable with sin?

---

### Movement 2: Put Aside the Sins of Speech (vv. 8-9a)

**The command**: "Put them all aside: anger, wrath, malice, slander, and abusive speech from your mouth. Do not lie to one another."

**The pivot**: "But now" (nyni de) — a sharp contrast between who the Colossians were and who they are now.

#### Personal Application

1. **Relational sins are as serious as the sins of desire.** Paul does not present the second vice list as a lesser category. The command to "put aside" these sins follows the command to "put to death" with equal urgency. I must not treat anger, malice, slander, and abusive speech as personality traits or temperamental quirks. They are sins that Paul commands me to strip off.

2. **The list reveals a progression from disposition to expression.** Anger (orge) is a settled hostility; wrath (thymos) is an explosive outburst; malice (kakia) is the general disposition of ill will; slander (blasphemia) is speech that damages another's reputation; abusive speech (aischrologia) is language that is shameful or degrading. The progression moves from the internal state (anger) to its verbal expression (slander, abusive speech). This means I must address the root (anger) and not merely police the symptoms (what I say).

3. **Lying destroys community.** Paul singles out lying as a separate imperative: "Do not lie to one another" (v. 9a). Lying is the fundamental relational sin because it destroys trust, and trust is the foundation of community. This command reaches beyond outright deception to include half-truths, selective disclosure intended to mislead, and the creation of false impressions. In my relationships — with family, friends, fellow believers — I must be a person whose word can be trusted.

4. **These sins are incompatible with who I am now.** The "but now" of v. 8 is not merely temporal ("now, at this point in time"); it is a declaration of identity ("now that you are in Christ"). The sins of speech are incompatible with the new self. When I engage in anger, slander, or lying, I am acting as the old self that has already been stripped off. I am wearing clothes that no longer fit.

#### Questions for Self-Examination

- Is there a pattern of anger or hostility in my life that I have excused as "just how I am"?
- Do I use my words to build up or to tear down? When I speak about others, would they be hurt by what I say?
- Am I honest in my relationships, or do I create false impressions to protect my reputation or avoid discomfort?
- When I sin with my words, do I trace it back to the underlying anger or malice?

---

### Movement 3: The Basis — The Old Self Removed, the New Self Renewed (vv. 9b-11)

**The basis**: "Since you laid aside the old self with its evil practices, and have put on the new self who is being renewed to a true knowledge according to the image of the One who created him — a renewal in which there is no distinction between Greek and Jew, circumcised and uncircumcised, barbarian, Scythian, slave and freeman, but Christ is all, and in all."

#### Personal Application

1. **My identity has already changed.** The aorist tenses of "laid aside" and "put on" indicate completed action. At conversion, the old self was stripped off and the new self was put on. This is not something I need to achieve; it is something I need to believe and live from. The commands of vv. 5 and 8 are not the means of becoming a new person — they are the outworking of already being one. When I struggle with sin, the answer is not to try harder in my own strength but to remember and act on what is already true about me in Christ.

2. **Sanctification is God's work in progress — and I must cooperate.** The present passive participle "being renewed" (anakainoumenon) tells me two things: the renewal is ongoing (present tense), and it is being done to me by God (passive voice). I am not the primary agent of my own transformation — God is. But the imperatives of vv. 5 and 8 tell me I am not passive either. Both truths must be held together. I depend on God for the power to change, and I actively obey His commands. This rules out both self-reliant moralism ("I'll fix myself") and passive quietism ("God will fix me without my effort").

3. **I am being restored to the image of God.** Sanctification is not arbitrary rule-following. It is image-restoration — becoming more fully what God designed me to be in Genesis 1:26-27. Every act of obedience, every sin put to death, every virtue put on moves me closer to the original design. This reframes holiness: it is not the suppression of my humanity but the restoration of it. The most human thing I can do is reflect the image of my Creator.

4. **Christ abolishes all human divisions in His community.** Verse 11 declares that in the new humanity, ethnic, religious, cultural, and socioeconomic distinctions cannot determine a person's standing, value, or identity. "Christ is all, and in all." This has direct implications for how I view and treat other believers:
   - I must not allow racial, political, economic, or cultural differences to create division or hierarchy in my relationships with other Christians.
   - I must not view any category of person as less welcome, less valuable, or less capable of maturity in Christ.
   - I must resist the tribalism and identity politics that the surrounding culture promotes, because in Christ, these categories are rendered irrelevant to standing and worth.
   - The unity of v. 11 is not achieved by human effort or social programs but by the reality that "Christ is all, and in all." Christ is the basis for unity, and any attempt to build unity apart from Him will fail.

5. **Christ is sufficient — nothing needs to be added to Him.** The climactic declaration "Christ is all, and in all" means that I need nothing beyond Christ for salvation, sanctification, identity, or community. This directly confronts the temptation to supplement Christ with:
   - Political alignment as a marker of genuine faith
   - Emotional experiences as evidence of spiritual maturity
   - Cultural conformity as a condition of belonging
   - Moral performance as the basis of standing before God

   None of these are what Paul directs me to. Christ alone is all, and in all.

#### Questions for Self-Examination

- Am I living from my new identity in Christ, or am I still trying to earn transformation through self-effort?
- Do I believe that God is actively renewing me, and am I cooperating with Him through obedience?
- Do I see growth in holiness as becoming more truly human, or as losing something?
- Are there people or groups I view as less-than in the body of Christ because of their background, culture, or social status?
- Is Christ truly "all" to me, or am I supplementing Him with something else for my identity, security, or worth?

## Corporate Application

The passage is addressed to a church, not merely to individuals. Its application therefore extends to the life of the believing community.

1. **The church must be a community where the old-self behaviors are not tolerated.** The sins of vv. 5 and 8 — especially the relational sins of anger, malice, slander, and lying — destroy fellowship. A church that allows gossip, slander, factions, and dishonesty to persist unchecked is a church that has not obeyed this passage. This does not mean a church must be perfect, but it does mean these sins should be named, addressed, and put off through mutual accountability and pastoral care.

2. **The church must actively embody the unity of v. 11.** A congregation that is divided along racial, economic, political, or cultural lines — or one that is homogeneous by design rather than by circumstance — should examine whether "Christ is all, and in all" is a functional reality in its life. The new humanity Paul describes is not merely diverse; it is unified *in Christ*, with all other identity markers subordinated to Him.

3. **The church must ground its pursuit of holiness in the gospel, not in human rules.** The Colossian heresy offered self-made religion and ascetic discipline as the path to spiritual maturity (2:23). Churches today face the same temptation: to substitute behavioral conformity for gospel-driven transformation. The power to put to death sin and put on the new self comes from union with Christ, not from stricter rules, cultural expectations, or performance metrics.

---

## Summary

The application of Colossians 3:5-11 can be stated simply:

**Because I have died with Christ and been raised to new life in Him, I must decisively put to death every sin of desire and put aside every sin of speech and hostility — not through self-effort but through the reality of my new identity. God is actively renewing me in His image, and the community He is creating has no room for the old self's sins or the world's divisions. Christ is all I need, and He is in all who belong to Him.**
