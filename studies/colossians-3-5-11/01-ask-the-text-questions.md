# Colossians 3:5-11 — Ask the Text Questions

---

## (1) What did this text mean to the human author?

### (1A) Who were they?

The human author is the Apostle Paul (Col 1:1), writing along with Timothy. Paul was a Pharisee educated under Gamaliel (Acts 22:3), a former persecutor of the church who was dramatically converted on the road to Damascus (Acts 9). By the time he wrote Colossians, Paul was an experienced missionary and church planter who had spent decades preaching to Gentiles throughout the Roman Empire. He was a Roman citizen by birth (Acts 22:28) and a Hebrew of Hebrews (Phil 3:5).

Paul's unique background — steeped in both Jewish Torah scholarship and Greco-Roman culture — equipped him to address the specific blend of Jewish legalism and pagan philosophy that threatened the Colossian church. His authority as an apostle grounded his commands: when Paul tells the Colossians to "put to death" certain behaviors, he speaks as one commissioned by Christ Himself.

### (1B) When did they write this?

Paul wrote Colossians during his first Roman imprisonment, approximately AD 60-62 (Col 4:3, 10, 18). This places the letter in the early days of Nero's reign, a period of relative tolerance toward Christians before the persecutions of AD 64. The Roman Empire was at the height of its power and moral decadence. Greco-Roman religion and philosophy permeated every aspect of public life. Emperor worship was spreading, and mystery religions were popular. The moral environment of the Roman world normalized the very vices Paul lists in verses 5 and 8 — sexual immorality, greed, anger, and slander were common features of pagan society.

Paul wrote Colossians around the same time as Ephesians, Philemon, and Philippians (the "Prison Epistles"). The close relationship between Colossians and Ephesians is especially notable: they share substantial parallel content, including the "put off / put on" ethical framework found in Colossians 3 and Ephesians 4:17-32.

### (1C) Where did they live?

Paul was writing from Rome, where he was under house arrest (Acts 28:16, 30-31). He had freedom to receive visitors and preach, but he was chained to a Roman guard.

The recipients lived in Colossae, a small city in the Lycus Valley of Phrygia (modern-day western Turkey), about 100 miles east of Ephesus. Colossae was situated along a major trade route and was near the cities of Laodicea and Hierapolis (Col 4:13). By Paul's time, Colossae had declined in importance compared to its neighbors. The population was a mixture of native Phrygians, Greeks, and a significant Jewish community that had been settled there by Antiochus III around 200 BC.

This geographical and demographic context matters: the Colossian church was a mixed congregation of Jewish and Gentile believers living in a pluralistic environment where multiple religious and philosophical traditions intersected. This explains why Paul addresses both Jewish-flavored legalism (2:16-17 — food laws, festivals, sabbaths) and Greek-flavored mysticism (2:18 — angel worship, visions) in this letter.

### (1D) What were they addressing?

Paul had never personally visited Colossae (Col 2:1). The church was founded by Epaphras, one of Paul's associates (Col 1:7; 4:12-13). Epaphras had traveled to Rome to report on the church's condition, and his report prompted this letter.

The central problem was the "Colossian heresy" — a false teaching that blended elements of Jewish legalism, Greek philosophy, and mystical practices. This false teaching:

- Emphasized human tradition and philosophy over Christ (2:8)
- Imposed dietary restrictions and observance of festivals and sabbaths (2:16)
- Promoted the worship of angels and claimed special visions (2:18)
- Advocated severe treatment of the body as a path to spirituality (2:20-23)

Paul's response was comprehensive: Christ is supreme over all creation and all spiritual powers (1:15-20; 2:9-10), and believers are complete in Him (2:10). The practical section beginning in 3:1 flows from this theological foundation. In 3:5-11, Paul is addressing what genuine transformation looks like. The false teachers offered human-made rules for dealing with the flesh (2:23 — "these are matters which have... the appearance of wisdom in self-made religion and self-abasement and severe treatment of the body, but are of no value against fleshly indulgence"). Paul counters with the real solution: dying with Christ and being raised to new life in Him.

In verses 5-11 specifically, Paul is telling the Colossians that their new identity in Christ (established in 3:1-4) demands a decisive break from their former pagan lifestyle. The two vice lists (vv. 5, 8) represent the behaviors that must be "put to death" and "put aside" — not through ascetic self-discipline (the false teachers' approach), but through the reality of their union with Christ.

### (1E) What was in their Bible?

Paul had access to the entire Old Testament and likely many New Testament writings that preceded his prison epistles. He had his own earlier letters (Romans, 1-2 Corinthians, Galatians, 1-2 Thessalonians), likely the epistle of James, and possibly the Gospel of Mark and an early form of Luke's Gospel (given his close relationship with Luke, who was with him during this imprisonment — Col 4:14).

The Colossian believers, being largely Gentile, may not have had extensive personal familiarity with the Old Testament Scriptures, but they would have received teaching from Epaphras grounded in the Old Testament. Paul's allusions in this passage draw on Old Testament themes:

- **"Put to death"** (v. 5) — echoes the language of covenant faithfulness; Israel was called to destroy what was devoted to destruction (Deut 13:15; Josh 7).
- **"Greed, which amounts to idolatry"** (v. 5) — echoes the first and second commandments (Exod 20:3-4) and prophetic denunciations of covetousness as a form of idol worship.
- **"The image of the One who created him"** (v. 10) — a direct allusion to Genesis 1:26-27, the creation of humanity in the image of God. Paul frames sanctification as a restoration of the image that was marred by the fall.
- **"No distinction between Greek and Jew"** (v. 11) — draws on the prophetic vision of all nations being brought into covenant relationship with God (Isa 49:6; 56:6-8).

## (2) What does this text teach me about God?

### (A) What is God saying or doing in this text?

Through Paul's apostolic instruction, God is commanding believers to align their behavior with their new identity in Christ. God is not merely giving moral advice; He is declaring that transformation is both required and possible because of what He has already accomplished. The indicative (what God has done — vv. 1-4, 9b-10) grounds the imperative (what believers must do — vv. 5, 8-9a).

God is also warning of coming judgment: "the wrath of God will come upon the sons of disobedience" (v. 6). This is a declaration of divine justice — God will not tolerate sin indefinitely.

And God is announcing a new creation: He is renewing believers "according to the image of the One who created him" (v. 10). God is actively at work restoring humanity to the original design of Genesis 1.

### (B) Why is God saying or doing this?

God communicates these truths because:

1. **Holiness matters to God.** God's character demands that His people reflect His nature. The vices listed in vv. 5 and 8 are contrary to who God is.
2. **The Colossians needed to understand that real transformation comes from Christ, not human rules.** The false teachers offered a counterfeit holiness based on asceticism and ritual (2:20-23). God, through Paul, reveals that true holiness flows from union with Christ — dying with Him and being raised with Him.
3. **God's redemptive purpose includes restoring the image of God in humanity.** Verse 10 reveals that sanctification is not arbitrary moral cleanup — it is the progressive renewal of God's image in the believer. God is undoing the effects of the fall.
4. **God is building a new community that transcends human divisions.** Verse 11 declares that in this renewal, ethnic, religious, cultural, and socioeconomic barriers are abolished. God is creating a unified people in Christ.

### (C) How is God saying or doing this?

God communicates through **special revelation** — the inspired writing of the Apostle Paul. This is not a direct theophany or miraculous event; rather, God is working through the human author's pastoral instruction, carried by the letter-bearer Tychicus (Col 4:7-8) to be read aloud to the congregation.

God is acting through **providential means**: using Paul's imprisonment, Epaphras's report, and the letter's delivery to accomplish His purposes. The Holy Spirit superintended Paul's writing so that the words bear divine authority (2 Tim 3:16-17).

God is also acting **regeneratively and progressively** in the lives of believers. Verse 10 uses a present passive participle — "being renewed" (ἀνακαινούμενον) — indicating that God is actively and ongoingly renewing the believer. Sanctification is God's work in progress.

### (D) Which of God's attributes are on display?

- **Holiness** — God's moral purity demands the eradication of sin in His people's lives (vv. 5-8).
- **Wrath/Justice** — God will judge sin; His wrath will come upon the disobedient (v. 6).
- **Sovereignty** — God has the authority to command radical moral transformation, and He has the power to accomplish it.
- **Creativity** — God is the Creator ("the image of the One who created him," v. 10), and sanctification is an act of re-creation.
- **Impartiality** — In the new humanity God is creating, there is no partiality based on ethnicity, culture, or social status (v. 11).
- **Sufficiency** — "Christ is all, and in all" (v. 11). God, in Christ, is fully sufficient for salvation, sanctification, and the creation of a new community.

## (3) How should this text contribute to my theology?

### (A) How does this text advance the metanarrative of Scripture?

This passage is a critical link in the redemption story. The metanarrative of Scripture moves from **Creation** (Genesis 1-2) through **Fall** (Genesis 3) to **Redemption** (the work of Christ) and toward **Restoration** (the new heavens and new earth).

Colossians 3:5-11 stands at the intersection of Redemption and Restoration:

- **Creation**: Verse 10 explicitly references the creation of humanity in God's image (Gen 1:26-27). The "new self" is described as being renewed "according to the image of the One who created him." Sanctification is a return to original design.
- **Fall**: The vice lists in vv. 5 and 8 represent the distortion of human nature after the fall. Sexual immorality, greed, anger, malice — these are the fruits of humanity's rebellion against God.
- **Redemption**: The passage is grounded in the believer's death and resurrection with Christ (vv. 1-4, stated via the inferential "therefore" in v. 5). Because Christ has died and risen, believers have a new identity that makes the old life incompatible.
- **Restoration**: Verse 11 anticipates the eschatological reality where all human divisions are abolished and Christ is supreme. This new community — the church — is a foretaste of the coming restoration of all things.

### (B) How does this text point to Christ?

Christ is the center of this passage, even though much of the text addresses human behavior:

- **Union with Christ** — The "therefore" in v. 5 connects back to 3:1-4. Believers have died with Christ and been raised with Him. Their ability to "put to death" sin flows from Christ's death on their behalf.
- **Christ as the Image of God** — The "new self" is being renewed according to the image of God (v. 10). Colossians 1:15 has already established that Christ Himself is "the image of the invisible God." Believers are being conformed to Christ.
- **Christ is all, and in all** (v. 11) — This climactic declaration affirms Christ's absolute supremacy and sufficiency. He is the sole basis for identity, unity, and worth in the new community. He is not merely a teacher or example — He is everything.
- **Contrast with the Colossian heresy** — The false teachers offered supplementary spiritual experiences (angel worship, visions, asceticism). Paul declares that Christ alone is sufficient. No addition is needed.

### (D) Which areas of systematic theology relate?

- **Hamartiology** (the study of sin) — The two vice lists provide a taxonomy of sin: sins of desire and appetite (v. 5) and sins of speech and social interaction (vv. 8-9). Greed is identified as idolatry (v. 5), revealing sin's essential nature as misplaced worship.

- **Soteriology** (the study of salvation) — The passage assumes the reality of justification (the believer has already "died" and been "raised" with Christ) and addresses sanctification (the progressive outworking of that new identity). The indicative-imperative structure ("you have put on the new self" → "therefore put to death") is foundational to understanding how salvation works in daily life.

- **Christology** (the study of Christ) — Christ as the image of God (v. 10; cf. 1:15), the supremacy and sufficiency of Christ ("Christ is all, and in all," v. 11), and union with Christ as the basis for ethical transformation.

- **Anthropology** (the study of man) — The "old self" and "new self" language raises questions about human nature, the effects of the fall, and the renewal of the image of God. What does it mean to be human? This text says it means to bear God's image — and that sin distorts that image while Christ restores it.

- **Ecclesiology** (the study of the church) — Verse 11 defines the church as a community that transcends all human categories. The church is not merely a voluntary association; it is a new humanity where "Christ is all, and in all."

- **Eschatology** (the study of the end) — The wrath of God "will come" (v. 6, future tense) upon the disobedient, pointing to final judgment. The renewal of the image of God (v. 10) points toward the ultimate glorification of believers (cf. 3:4).

## (4) How does this truth apply to me?

### (A) What is my relationship to the human author?

Paul is an apostle of Jesus Christ — one uniquely commissioned by the risen Lord. I am not an apostle, nor do I hold apostolic authority. However, as a believer in Christ, I am a beneficiary of Paul's apostolic ministry. His inspired letters carry the authority of God's Word and are binding on all Christians in every era (2 Tim 3:16-17). Paul writes as my spiritual forefather in the faith.

### (B) What is my relationship to the original audience?

Like the Colossians, I am a Gentile believer who has been brought into the people of God through faith in Christ. Like them, I live in a culture saturated with sexual immorality, greed, anger, and division. Like them, I face false teachings that would supplement or replace the sufficiency of Christ with human philosophy, religious ritual, or spiritual experiences. My spiritual situation is remarkably similar to theirs.

### (C) What are the differences between my world and their world?

- The Colossians were first-generation believers converting out of paganism. Many of them had personally participated in idolatrous worship and the attendant immorality. While I live in a post-Christian culture increasingly hostile to biblical values, my cultural background is different from theirs.
- The specific "Colossian heresy" (angel worship, dietary legalism, ascetic mysticism) is not prevalent in my context, but its underlying pattern is: the temptation to add something to Christ, whether that be political ideology, emotional experience, moral performance, or cultural conformity.
- The social categories Paul lists in v. 11 (Greek/Jew, circumcised/uncircumcised, barbarian, Scythian, slave/free) were the dominant fault lines of the ancient world. Today's divisions — racial, political, economic, national — are different in specifics but identical in kind. The principle of v. 11 speaks directly to the tribalism and identity politics of any era.

### (D) Is my relationship to God different than theirs?

No. As a New Testament believer, my relationship to God is the same as the Colossians'. I have been justified by faith in Christ, indwelt by the Holy Spirit, and am being sanctified through union with Christ. The same indicative-imperative dynamic applies to me: because I have died with Christ and been raised with Him, I must put to death the deeds of the flesh and put on the new self. The commands of this passage apply to me directly.

### Summary Application

1. **I must decisively put to death sinful desires** — not merely manage them, suppress them, or negotiate with them. The verb "put to death" (νεκρώσατε) is an aorist imperative: a decisive, once-for-all action. This includes not only obvious sins like sexual immorality but also the subtle idolatry of greed.

2. **I must put aside relational sins** — anger, wrath, malice, slander, abusive speech, and lying. These sins destroy community and contradict the new humanity God is creating.

3. **I must recognize that genuine transformation flows from my identity in Christ**, not from self-made rules, cultural conformity, or ascetic discipline. The power to change comes from union with Christ, not from willpower.

4. **I must view other believers — regardless of ethnicity, culture, or social status — as equals in Christ.** "Christ is all, and in all" means that no human category can determine a person's standing in God's new community.

5. **I must understand sanctification as image-restoration.** God is renewing me "according to the image of the One who created" me. My growth in holiness is not arbitrary rule-following; it is a return to original design — becoming more fully human as God intended.
