# Step 1: Ask the Text Questions — Colossians 3:1-4

## Text (NASB 1995)

> *(1) Therefore, if you have been raised with Christ, keep seeking the things that are above, where Christ is, seated at the right hand of God. (2) Set your minds on the things that are above, not on the things that are on earth. (3) For you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God. (4) When Christ, who is our life, is revealed, then you too will be revealed with Him in glory.*

---

## (1) What did this text mean to the human author?

### (1A) Who were they?

The human author is **Paul the Apostle** (Col 1:1), writing alongside Timothy. Paul was a Pharisee trained under Gamaliel (Acts 22:3), a Roman citizen from Tarsus, and the apostle to the Gentiles (Rom 11:13). By the time of writing, Paul had conducted multiple missionary journeys and planted churches across Asia Minor and Greece. He had not personally visited the Colossian church — it was founded by **Epaphras**, a co-laborer who had evangelized the Lycus Valley (Col 1:7; 4:12-13).

Paul's deep theological training in both Judaism and the Old Testament Scriptures, combined with his direct apostolic commission from the risen Christ (Gal 1:1, 11-12), equipped him to address the theological confusion at Colossae with precision. His language in 3:1-4 reflects someone intimately acquainted with both Jewish theology (resurrection, the right hand of God) and the lived experience of union with Christ.

### (1B) When did they write this?

Colossians was written during Paul's **first Roman imprisonment**, approximately **AD 60-62** (Acts 28:16-31). Paul was under house arrest, chained to a Roman guard, yet free to receive visitors and send letters. This period also produced Ephesians, Philemon, and Philippians — the "Prison Epistles."

Historically, the Roman Empire was at its zenith under Nero (emperor AD 54-68). Nero's early reign was relatively stable, though persecution of Christians would intensify later. The church was roughly 30 years old. The apostles were still alive and active. Christianity was spreading rapidly through the Gentile world but was also encountering increasing theological challenges from both Jewish legalism and Greco-Roman philosophy.

The Lycus Valley, where Colossae was located, had experienced a devastating earthquake around AD 60-61 (recorded by Tacitus). This may have heightened the Colossians' sense of vulnerability and their susceptibility to false teaching promising spiritual security through special knowledge or ritual practices.

### (1C) Where did they live?

Paul wrote from **Rome**, but the recipients lived in **Colossae**, a city in the Lycus Valley of Phrygia (modern-day western Turkey). Colossae sat along a major trade route and was neighbored by Laodicea and Hierapolis (Col 4:13). Once a prominent city, Colossae had declined in significance by the first century, overshadowed by neighboring Laodicea.

The Lycus Valley was a melting pot of religious influences: Phrygian folk religion, Greco-Roman paganism, a significant Jewish diaspora community, and mystery religions. This diverse religious environment is critical for understanding the false teaching Paul combats in chapter 2 — a syncretistic blend that mixed Jewish observances (dietary laws, festivals, Sabbath — Col 2:16) with ascetic practices (Col 2:21-23) and what appears to be early forms of angel worship or mystical visionary experiences (Col 2:18).

### (1D) What were they addressing?

Paul wrote to combat a specific false teaching threatening the Colossian church, often called the **"Colossian heresy."** While we do not have the heretics' documents, we can reconstruct their teaching from Paul's response in chapter 2:

- **Jewish legalism:** Insistence on dietary laws, festivals, new moons, Sabbaths (2:16)
- **Asceticism:** "Do not handle, do not taste, do not touch" (2:21) — severe treatment of the body as a path to spiritual maturity (2:23)
- **Mystical practices:** "Worship of the angels," "visions he has seen," being "inflated without cause by his fleshly mind" (2:18)
- **Philosophy:** "Empty deception according to the tradition of men, according to the elementary principles of the world" (2:8)

These false teachers were essentially adding requirements to the gospel. They taught that faith in Christ alone was insufficient — believers needed additional spiritual knowledge, ritual observances, and ascetic disciplines to achieve true spiritual fullness.

Paul's response throughout Colossians is to assert the **absolute sufficiency of Christ**: "In Him all the fullness of Deity dwells in bodily form, and in Him you have been made complete" (2:9-10). Colossians 3:1-4 is the **turning point** of the letter. Having demolished the false teaching in chapter 2, Paul now pivots to the positive implications: since believers are complete in Christ, their lives should be oriented toward Him.

The word **"Therefore"** (οὖν, *oun*) at the start of 3:1 is an inferential conjunction that draws a conclusion from everything Paul has argued in chapters 1-2. It signals: *Given all that is true about Christ and your union with Him, here is how you should now live.*

### (1E) What was in their Bible?

Paul had access to the complete **Old Testament canon** (the Law, the Prophets, and the Writings). As a trained Pharisee, he was deeply versed in these Scriptures. By AD 60-62, Paul had also written many of his own epistles (Romans, 1-2 Corinthians, Galatians, 1-2 Thessalonians), and the early church was beginning to circulate and recognize apostolic letters as authoritative (Col 4:16; 2 Pet 3:15-16).

The Colossian believers — many of them Gentile converts — would have been familiar with the Old Testament through synagogue readings and apostolic teaching. Paul's language in 3:1-4 draws on Old Testament imagery:

- **"Raised with Christ"** — Echoes the resurrection hope found in passages like Psalm 16:10, Isaiah 26:19, Ezekiel 37 (the valley of dry bones), and Daniel 12:2
- **"Seated at the right hand of God"** — A direct allusion to **Psalm 110:1**, the most-quoted Old Testament verse in the New Testament: "The LORD says to my Lord: 'Sit at My right hand until I make Your enemies a footstool for Your feet.'"
- **"Hidden with Christ in God"** — Echoes the concept of God as a hiding place and refuge (Ps 32:7; 91:1; 119:114)
- **"Revealed in glory"** — Draws on prophetic expectations of divine revelation and glory (Isa 40:5; Dan 7:13-14)

## (2) What does this text teach me about God?

### (A) What is God saying or doing in this text?

God, through Paul, is declaring the **believer's new identity and position in Christ**. God is communicating several truths simultaneously:

1. **God raised Christ** — and by extension, He has raised believers with Christ (v1). The passive voice ("you have been raised") indicates God is the agent of this resurrection.
2. **God seated Christ at His right hand** — the position of supreme authority and honor (v1). This is God the Father's vindication of Christ's completed work.
3. **God holds the believer's life secure** — "your life is hidden with Christ in God" (v3). The believer's true life is tucked away in the safest place in the universe: in Christ, who is Himself in God. This is a double enclosure of security.
4. **God will reveal Christ — and believers with Him — in glory** (v4). God has a future plan of revelation that encompasses both Christ and His people.

### (B) Why is God saying or doing this?

God communicates these truths for several purposes:

1. **To reorient the Colossians away from the false teaching.** The heretics were directing believers downward — toward earthly rituals, human traditions, and self-imposed regulations. God redirects them upward — toward the exalted Christ.
2. **To ground Christian living in Christian identity.** The imperatives in vv1-2 ("keep seeking," "set your minds") are rooted in the indicatives of vv3-4 ("you have died," "your life is hidden," "you will be revealed"). God establishes what is true *before* commanding what to do.
3. **To provide assurance.** The Colossians were being told they needed "more" — more knowledge, more rituals, more discipline. God assures them through Paul that their life is already secure, hidden in the most protected place possible.
4. **To set their hope on future glory.** The present is not the final chapter. God points them forward to the day when what is now hidden will be fully revealed.

### (C) How is God saying or doing this?

God communicates these truths **providentially through the apostle Paul**, using the medium of an inspired letter. This is not direct speech, dream, or angelic visitation — it is the ordinary means of apostolic instruction, carried by **Tychicus** (Col 4:7-8) to be read aloud in the Colossian assembly.

The mode is **declarative and imperative** — Paul states theological realities and issues commands based on those realities. The authority behind the words is apostolic (Col 1:1), which Paul understood as a direct commission from Christ Himself.

### (D) Which of God's attributes are on display?

- **Sovereignty** — God raised Christ, seated Him at His right hand, and will reveal Him in glory. God is in complete control of redemptive history.
- **Power** — The same power that raised Christ from the dead has raised believers to new life (cf. Eph 1:19-20).
- **Faithfulness** — God's plan moves from past action (raising, seating) through present reality (hidden life) to future consummation (revelation in glory). God finishes what He starts.
- **Love and Grace** — Believers are included in God's plan not by merit but by union with Christ. Their life is "hidden with Christ in God" — an intimate, secure relationship.
- **Wisdom** — God's strategy for combating false teaching is not a point-by-point rebuttal of every error but a grand declaration of the supremacy and sufficiency of Christ.

## (3) How should this text contribute to my theology?

### (A) How does this text advance the metanarrative of Scripture?

Colossians 3:1-4 sits at a pivotal point in the Bible's overarching story of redemption:

- **Creation → Fall → Redemption → Consummation.** This text speaks to both Redemption (what Christ has accomplished) and Consummation (what is yet to come).
- The passage reveals that believers are already participants in the new creation through their union with the risen Christ, even as they await its full manifestation. The "already/not yet" tension of the kingdom is present: believers *have been* raised (past), their life *is* hidden (present), and they *will be* revealed in glory (future).
- This advances the metanarrative by showing that God's redemptive plan is not merely forensic (legal declaration) but participatory — believers are drawn into the life of Christ Himself.

### (B) How does this text point to Christ?

This text is **saturated with Christology**:

- **Christ has been raised** — His resurrection is the foundation of everything that follows. Without the resurrection, there is no "raised with Christ."
- **Christ is seated at the right hand of God** — His exaltation fulfills Psalm 110:1 and demonstrates His completed work and present reign.
- **Christ is where the "things above" are** — The believer's upward orientation is not abstract or mystical; it is personal. "Things above" are defined by Christ's location and presence.
- **The believer's life is hidden with *Christ*** — Union with Christ is the dominant theme. The Christian life is not independent; it is derivative of and dependent upon Christ.
- **"Christ, who is our life"** (v4) — One of the most remarkable Christological statements in Scripture. Christ is not merely the giver of life or the way to life — He *is* the believer's life. This is identification at the deepest level.
- **Christ will be revealed** — His second coming is the event toward which all of history moves. Believers' glorification is contingent upon and simultaneous with His revelation.

### (C) What other passages clarify and/or illustrate this text?

- **Romans 6:1-11** — Paul's fullest treatment of death and resurrection with Christ. "Consider yourselves to be dead to sin, but alive to God in Christ Jesus" (Rom 6:11). The theological foundation for "you have died" and "you have been raised."
- **Ephesians 2:4-6** — "Even when we were dead in our transgressions, [God] made us alive together with Christ... and raised us up with Him, and seated us with Him in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus." Nearly identical theology stated in parallel.
- **Galatians 2:20** — "I have been crucified with Christ; and it is no longer I who live, but Christ lives in me." The personal dimension of "Christ, who is our life."
- **Philippians 3:20-21** — "Our citizenship is in heaven, from which also we eagerly wait for a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ; who will transform the body of our humble state into conformity with the body of His glory." Parallel to the future revelation in v4.
- **1 John 3:2** — "We know that when He appears, we will be like Him, because we will see Him just as He is." Direct parallel to being "revealed with Him in glory."
- **Psalm 110:1** — "The LORD says to my Lord: 'Sit at My right hand until I make Your enemies a footstool for Your feet.'" The Old Testament foundation for Christ's session at God's right hand.
- **Romans 8:17-19** — "If children, heirs also... if indeed we suffer with Him so that we may also be glorified with Him... the anxious longing of the creation waits eagerly for the revealing of the sons of God." Parallel to future revelation and glorification.
- **Colossians 2:9-15, 20-23** — The immediate preceding context. Paul has just argued that believers died with Christ to the elementary principles of the world (2:20) and that fullness is found in Christ alone (2:9-10). The "therefore" of 3:1 draws the conclusion.

### (D) Which areas of systematic theology relate?

- **Christology** — Christ's resurrection, exaltation, session at God's right hand, identification as "our life," and future revelation/second coming.
- **Soteriology** — Union with Christ; positional sanctification (believers have died and been raised with Christ); the "already/not yet" structure of salvation; the security of the believer ("hidden with Christ in God").
- **Eschatology** — The future revelation of Christ; the glorification of believers at Christ's appearing; the "not yet" dimension of salvation.
- **Anthropology/Hamartiology** — The believer's old self has died (v3); the contrast between "things above" and "things on earth" (v2) implies the ongoing tension between the flesh and the new identity.
- **Theology Proper** — God as the one who raises, seats, hides, and reveals. The Father's relationship to the Son (Christ seated at *God's* right hand). The Trinitarian structure implicit in "with Christ in God."

## (4) How does this truth apply to me?

### (A) What is my relationship to the human author?

I am not a direct recipient of this letter as the Colossians were. Paul did not know me, nor did he write with my specific situation in mind. However, as an apostle commissioned by Christ to instruct the church, Paul's authoritative teaching applies to all believers across all ages (cf. 1 Cor 14:37; 2 Pet 3:15-16). I stand as a beneficiary of Paul's apostolic ministry.

### (B) What is my relationship to the original audience?

The Colossians were first-century Gentile believers in a small Phrygian city, facing a specific syncretistic heresy. I share their status as a Gentile believer, their union with Christ, and their position of having "died" and been "raised" with Him. The theological realities Paul describes — union with Christ, the believer's hidden life, future glorification — are not culturally bound but are grounded in the universal work of Christ.

### (C) What are the differences between my world and their world?

- I am not facing the specific "Colossian heresy" — the particular blend of Jewish legalism, asceticism, and angel mysticism. However, the *principle* of the heresy — adding requirements beyond Christ for spiritual fullness — is perennial. Modern equivalents include legalistic systems, prosperity theology, moralistic religion, and spiritual experiences marketed as essential for "higher" Christianity.
- The Colossians lived under Roman imperial rule with limited religious freedom. I live in a context of religious liberty but face different pressures: secularism, materialism, and distraction.
- The Colossians received this letter read aloud in a gathered assembly. I have personal access to the text in my own language.

### (D) Is my relationship to God different than theirs?

No — not fundamentally. Both the Colossian believers and I are New Covenant believers, united to Christ by faith, indwelt by the Holy Spirit, and awaiting Christ's return. The same realities Paul describes apply to me: I have died with Christ, I have been raised with Christ, my life is hidden with Christ in God, and when Christ is revealed, I too will be revealed with Him in glory.

**Therefore, the imperatives apply directly:** I am to keep seeking the things above. I am to set my mind on things above, not on things on the earth. I am to live in light of my true identity and future hope, refusing to be drawn back into earthly systems of self-improvement, legalistic performance, or mystical experience as substitutes for the sufficiency of Christ.
