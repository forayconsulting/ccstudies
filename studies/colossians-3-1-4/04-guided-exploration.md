# A Guided Exploration of Colossians 3:1-4

> *"Therefore, if you have been raised with Christ, keep seeking the things that are above, where Christ is, seated at the right hand of God. Set your minds on the things that are above, not on the things that are on earth. For you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God. When Christ, who is our life, is revealed, then you too will be revealed with Him in glory."*

These four verses sit at a turning point in the book of Colossians. They are the hinge that connects Paul's theology to his ethics, his argument to his application. And like any good hinge, they swing in two directions at once: backward into everything Paul has been arguing, and forward into everything he's about to command. If we pull them off the hinge and treat them as a standalone devotional thought, we lose the force of what Paul is doing here.

So let's not do that. Let's dig in.

## Starting with Context

One of the first things you'll notice in Colossians 3:1 is the word **"Therefore."** If you've been paying attention to conjunctions (and after our work in chapter 6, I hope you have!), that word should stop you in your tracks. "Therefore" is an inferential conjunction. It means Paul is drawing a conclusion. He's saying, *"Given everything I've just told you, here is what follows."*

That raises an obvious question: what has Paul just told us?

The short answer is: a lot. In chapters 1 and 2, Paul has made an extended case for the supremacy and sufficiency of Jesus Christ. He's told the Colossians that Christ is the image of the invisible God, that all the fullness of Deity dwells in Him bodily, and that in Christ they have been made complete (1:15; 2:9-10). He has also taken aim at a specific false teaching circulating in the Colossian church. This teaching was a dangerous cocktail: part Jewish legalism (dietary laws, festivals, Sabbath observances), part asceticism ("do not handle, do not taste, do not touch"), and part mysticism (angel worship and visionary experiences). Whatever its exact shape, the heresy essentially told believers that Christ wasn't enough. You needed Christ *plus* something else: extra knowledge, extra rituals, extra discipline.

Paul's response to all of this is devastating. In 2:20-23, he lands the final blow: *"If you have died with Christ to the elementary principles of the world, why, as if you were living in the world, do you submit yourselves to decrees, such as, 'Do not handle, do not taste, do not touch!'... These are matters which have, to be sure, the appearance of wisdom in self-made religion and self-abasement and severe treatment of the body, but are of no value against fleshly indulgence."*

And then, without missing a beat: *"Therefore..."*

Do you see what's happening? Colossians 3:1 is not the beginning of a new thought. It is the culmination of an old one. The commands in our passage are the positive side of a coin whose negative side Paul has already shown us. Chapter 2 says, "Stop submitting to earthly regulations." Chapter 3 says, "Start seeking heavenly realities." Same argument, two directions.

If we skip the context and jump straight to "set your minds on things above," we might walk away with a vague sense that Christians should think happy thoughts. But that's not Paul's point at all. He's making a specific argument to a specific church facing a specific problem. And we need to understand that argument before we can understand these verses.

## Breaking Down the Sentences

Colossians is a letter, so our unit of thought is the sentence. Let's walk through each one.

### Verse 1

> *"Therefore, if you have been raised with Christ, keep seeking the things that are above, where Christ is, seated at the right hand of God."*

This sentence has two halves. The first half states a reality: "you have been raised with Christ." The second half issues a command: "keep seeking the things that are above." The structure is important. Paul does not say, "Seek things above so that you can be raised with Christ." He says, "Since you have been raised, therefore seek." The imperative flows from the indicative. What you do is grounded in who you are.

Now, a word about that little word "if." In English, "if" usually introduces uncertainty. "If it rains, we'll cancel the picnic." But the Greek construction here is what grammarians call a first-class condition. It assumes the condition is true. A better translation might be "since" or "given that." Paul is not wondering whether the Colossians have been raised with Christ. He's affirming it as the foundation for his command.

And notice how Paul defines "things above." He doesn't leave it abstract. He immediately adds, "where Christ is, seated at the right hand of God." The upward orientation Paul calls for is not mystical or vague. It is personal. "Things above" are defined by a Person, not a place. They are defined by Christ's presence and His position of supreme authority at the Father's right hand. That phrase, "seated at the right hand of God," is an allusion to Psalm 110:1, the most frequently quoted Old Testament verse in the entire New Testament. It speaks of completed work and present reign. Christ is not still laboring; He is seated. He is not subordinate; He is at the right hand of power.

### Verse 2

> *"Set your minds on the things that are above, not on the things that are on earth."*

This verse arrives without a conjunction. In Greek grammar, that's called asyndeton, and it creates emphasis. Paul doesn't ease into this command; he delivers it sharply. It's a second imperative stacked right on top of the first.

But it's not a mere repetition. In verse 1, the command was "keep seeking" (the Greek word *zeteo*, meaning to actively pursue). Here, the command is "set your minds on" (the Greek word *phroneo*, meaning to adopt a certain mindset, to orient your entire disposition). If "seeking" describes outward pursuit, "setting the mind" describes inward orientation. Together, the two commands are comprehensive. Paul is calling for a total reorientation of life, both in what we pursue and in how we think.

The contrast is equally pointed: "not on the things that are on earth." In context, "things on earth" does not mean the material world in general. Paul is not a Platonist, and he's not encouraging some kind of spiritual escapism. Remember, he's just finished dismantling the Colossian heresy. "Things on earth" refers to the earthly religious system the false teachers promoted: human traditions, self-imposed regulations, ascetic disciplines that promise spiritual advancement but deliver nothing (2:22-23). Those are the "earthly things" Paul wants them to stop fixating on.

### Verse 3

> *"For you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God."*

Here we get the reason for the commands. The word "For" (*gar* in Greek) is a causal conjunction. It tells us *why* we should seek and think about things above. And the answer is striking: because we have died.

Paul stated this same reality back in 2:20: "If you have died with Christ to the elementary principles of the world..." The death he's talking about is not physical. It is the believer's co-crucifixion with Christ (see Romans 6:6 and Galatians 2:20). At the cross, the old self was terminated. The believer's former identity, bound up in worldly systems and self-effort, has come to an end. That is past tense, completed action. It's done.

But Paul doesn't stop there. Death is only half the picture. He adds, "and your life is hidden with Christ in God." The Greek verb for "hidden" (*krypto*, from which we get the English word "cryptic") is in the perfect tense, indicating a past action with ongoing results. The believer's life was hidden at the moment of conversion and remains hidden now. And look at where it is hidden: *with Christ in God*. That is a double enclosure. Your life is in Christ, and Christ is in God. It's hard to imagine a safer location in the universe.

This is not hiddenness as obscurity or neglect. This is hiddenness as security. The world cannot see your true identity. The world looks at a Christian and sees an ordinary person with an ordinary life. But the reality is concealed, tucked away in the most protected place imaginable. And that hiddenness is temporary, as the next verse makes clear.

### Verse 4

> *"When Christ, who is our life, is revealed, then you too will be revealed with Him in glory."*

This final sentence resolves the tension created in verse 3. If the believer's life is hidden, it will not always be so. The word "revealed" (*phaneroo*) stands in deliberate contrast to "hidden" (*krypto*). What is now concealed will one day be made visible. What the world cannot see now, it will see then.

The temporal conjunction "when" (*hotan*) points to a future event: the return of Christ. And the structure of the sentence ties the believer's destiny to Christ's appearance. When *He* is revealed, *then* you will be revealed with Him. The believer's glorification is not an independent event. It is inseparable from Christ's own revelation.

But perhaps the most remarkable element of this verse is the parenthetical phrase tucked into the middle: **"who is our life."** Not "who gives us life." Not "who is the way to life." Christ *is* our life. This is identification at the deepest possible level. The Christian life is not a self-improvement project assisted by Jesus. It is Christ Himself, living in and through the believer. Paul makes the same point in Galatians 2:20: "It is no longer I who live, but Christ lives in me." And in Philippians 1:21: "For to me, to live is Christ."

If Christ is our life, then every system that would supplement Christ with additional requirements for spiritual vitality is not just unnecessary; it is an insult to the sufficiency of the One who is Himself our life.

## Seeing the Structure

Now that we've worked through each sentence, let's step back and look at the passage as a whole. When we synthesize our summaries, two natural groupings emerge.

Verses 1 and 2 are **imperative**. They tell us what to do: seek things above, set your mind on things above.

Verses 3 and 4 are **indicative**. They tell us what is true: you have died, your life is hidden, Christ is your life, you will be revealed in glory.

The structure, then, is this:

>     (3:1-4) The Believer's Heavenly Orientation
>
>         (3:1-2) Command: Seek What Is Above
>
>             (3:1) Keep seeking the things above, where the risen Christ reigns
>
>             (3:2) Set your mind on things above, not on earthly things
>
>         (3:3-4) Ground: Your Life Is in Christ
>
>             (3:3) You died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God
>
>             (3:4) When Christ is revealed, you will be revealed with Him in glory

Within each pair, there is also internal movement. Verse 1 addresses action (seeking), while verse 2 addresses mindset (thinking). That's an intensification, moving from the external to the internal. And verse 3 describes past and present realities (you died; your life is hidden), while verse 4 describes the future hope (you will be revealed). That's a temporal progression, moving from what has happened and is happening to what will happen.

There's even a chiastic shape if you look closely. The outer frame (verses 1 and 4) speaks of the believer's union with Christ: raised with Him in the past, revealed with Him in the future. The inner frame (verses 2 and 3) addresses the present: what to do and why. Christ brackets everything.

## Letting Scripture Interpret Scripture

One of our most powerful exegetical principles is this: let Scripture interpret Scripture before turning to external resources. So let's see how the rest of the Bible illuminates these four verses.

**Romans 6:1-11** provides the fullest treatment of the theology underlying verse 3. In that passage, Paul explains that believers have been "buried with [Christ] through baptism into death, so that as Christ was raised from the dead through the glory of the Father, so we too might walk in newness of life" (6:4). The logic is the same as Colossians 3: because you have participated in Christ's death and resurrection, live accordingly.

**Ephesians 2:4-6** runs almost parallel to our passage: "Even when we were dead in our transgressions, [God] made us alive together with Christ... and raised us up with Him, and seated us with Him in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus." The theology is nearly identical, and these two letters were likely written and delivered around the same time.

**1 John 3:1-2** is perhaps the closest parallel to the hiddenness and future revelation of verse 4: "The world does not know us, because it did not know Him. Beloved, now we are children of God, and it has not appeared as yet what we will be. We know that when He appears, we will be like Him, because we will see Him just as He is." The same pattern holds: hidden identity now, glorious revelation later.

And **Philippians 3:20-21** gives us the embodied dimension: "Our citizenship is in heaven, from which also we eagerly wait for a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ; who will transform the body of our humble state into conformity with the body of His glory." The "revelation" Paul talks about in Colossians 3:4 is not merely spiritual. It will include the transformation of our very bodies.

These cross-references confirm what we've already observed through careful reading. But they also expand our understanding. The concept of dying and rising with Christ is not unique to Colossians. It is a major theme in Paul's theology, appearing across multiple letters written to different churches. And the hope of future glorification is not uniquely Pauline, either. John, Peter, and the author of Hebrews all affirm it. When multiple biblical authors converge on the same truth, we can have great confidence in our interpretation.

## What This Teaches Us About God

As we study these verses, several of God's attributes come into view.

We see His **sovereignty**. God raised Christ, seated Him at His right hand, and will reveal Him in glory at the appointed time. Every major event in this passage is initiated by God. He is not reacting to the circumstances; He is orchestrating the whole story.

We see His **power**. The same power that raised Christ from the dead has raised believers to new life. As Paul puts it in Ephesians 1:19-20, the power available to believers is "in accordance with the working of the strength of His might which He brought about in Christ, when He raised Him from the dead."

We see His **faithfulness**. God's redemptive plan moves from past (He raised Christ and the believer with Him), through present (the believer's life is hidden and secure), to future (revelation in glory). God finishes what He starts. What He has begun in the believer, He will bring to completion (Philippians 1:6).

And we see His **wisdom**. God's strategy for combating the Colossian heresy is not a point-by-point rebuttal of every claim the false teachers made. Instead, it is a grand, sweeping declaration of the supremacy and sufficiency of Christ. God does not dignify every error with a direct response. Sometimes, the best way to dispel darkness is simply to turn on the light.

## Theological Significance

Let's take a step further and consider how this passage contributes to our broader theology.

In the area of **Christology**, we learn that Christ has been raised, exalted, and seated at the right hand of God. He is identified as "our life," one of the most profound Christological statements in Scripture. And He will be revealed in glory at the end of the age. This passage alone touches on the resurrection, ascension, present reign, and second coming of Christ.

In **Soteriology**, we encounter the concept of union with Christ. Believers have not merely received benefits from Christ at a distance. They have been united to Him in His death and resurrection. Their old life has ended. Their new life is bound up in His. This is what theologians sometimes call "positional sanctification": the believer's status before God has been permanently and decisively changed.

And in **Eschatology**, we find the "already/not yet" tension that characterizes the New Testament's view of the last days. Believers have *already* been raised with Christ (past). Their life *is* hidden with Christ in God (present). But they have *not yet* been revealed in glory (future). They live between the "already" of salvation accomplished and the "not yet" of salvation consummated.

## Bringing It Home

So how does this apply to us?

We are not first-century Colossian believers. We are not facing the exact false teaching Paul was addressing. We don't have traveling teachers pressuring us to observe Jewish festivals, worship angels, and pursue ascetic self-denial as a path to spiritual maturity. Our situation is different.

But the *principle* behind the Colossian heresy is timeless. The lie that Christ is not enough takes many forms. In our day, it might look like moralistic religion: "You need to try harder, do more, perform better." It might look like experiential spirituality: "You need a second blessing, a deeper experience, a special encounter." It might look like intellectual elitism: "You need this system, this teacher, this theological framework." Whatever the specific form, the underlying message is always the same: Christ alone is insufficient.

Paul's answer is simple and devastating. Christ is not merely the starting point of the spiritual life. He is the whole thing. He is seated at the right hand of God. Your life is hidden in Him. He *is* your life. And when He is revealed in glory, you will be revealed with Him.

That is why you seek things above. Not because earthly life doesn't matter. Not because the material world is evil. Not because you're supposed to float through life with your head in the clouds. You seek things above because the Person who defines your identity, secures your present, and guarantees your future is there. Your life is where He is. And one day, the whole world will see what has been hidden all along.

Until then, we seek. We set our minds. And we trust the One who holds our life.
