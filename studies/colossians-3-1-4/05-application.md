# Applying Colossians 3:1-4

> *"Therefore, if you have been raised with Christ, keep seeking the things that are above, where Christ is, seated at the right hand of God. Set your minds on the things that are above, not on the things that are on earth. For you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God. When Christ, who is our life, is revealed, then you too will be revealed with Him in glory."*

We've spent a good deal of time in this passage. We've asked questions of the text. We've outlined its structure. We've consulted exegetical resources. And we've synthesized those findings into a guided exploration that traced Paul's argument from context to conclusion. Now it's time to ask the question every faithful student of Scripture eventually comes to: *How does this truth apply to me?*

But we need to be careful here. Application is the part of Bible study where most of us are tempted to skip ahead. It's the part that feels the most "useful," the most immediately relevant. And that temptation, ironically, is exactly what makes it dangerous. When we rush to application without doing the hard work of exegesis, we risk building our lives on a meaning the text was never intended to carry. As we discussed in chapter 3, an application-driven reading is the hallmark of the Moral/Allegorical hermeneutic, and while application is important, it must come *last*, not first.

So let's slow down and walk through the application questions carefully. We've earned the right to ask them. We've done the exegetical work. Now let's see how these truths land in our own lives.

## What Is My Relationship to the Human Author?

Paul wrote this letter roughly two thousand years ago, from a Roman prison cell, to a church he had never personally visited. I was not in the room when Tychicus unrolled the scroll and read these words aloud. Paul did not know my name, my struggles, or my circumstances.

And yet, Paul was an apostle. His commission came directly from the risen Christ (Galatians 1:1). His letters were recognized as authoritative Scripture even during the lifetimes of the other apostles (2 Peter 3:15-16). When Paul writes under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit, he is not merely offering his personal opinion. He is delivering the Word of God.

This means that while Paul did not write *to* me, he wrote *for* me, in the sense that all Scripture is "profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, for training in righteousness" (2 Timothy 3:16). I am not Paul's direct audience, but I am a legitimate recipient of the truth he was commissioned to communicate.

That distinction matters. It keeps me from reading my own circumstances into the text as though Paul had me in mind. But it also prevents me from dismissing the passage as irrelevant simply because I didn't live in first-century Phrygia. Paul's authoritative teaching about union with Christ, heavenly orientation, and future glory applies to every believer in every era. I receive these words not as private mail opened by a stranger but as Scripture preserved for the church.

## What Is My Relationship to the Original Audience?

The Colossian believers were predominantly Gentile converts living in a small, declining city in the Lycus Valley of Asia Minor. They had come to faith through the ministry of Epaphras (Colossians 1:7), not through Paul directly. They gathered in homes. They lived under Roman authority. They navigated a religious landscape saturated with pagan temples, Jewish synagogues, mystery religions, and folk superstition.

In some ways, my situation is quite different. I am not a recent convert from paganism. I do not live under the political authority of a totalitarian empire. I have access to the complete canon of Scripture in my own language, along with centuries of Christian commentary, theology, and tradition that the Colossians did not possess.

But in the ways that matter most, we share the same standing before God. Like the Colossian believers, I am a Gentile who has been brought into the family of God through faith in Jesus Christ. Like them, I have "been raised with Christ." Like them, my life is "hidden with Christ in God." And like them, I await the day when Christ is revealed and I am revealed with Him in glory.

The theological realities Paul describes in these four verses are not culturally conditioned. They are not tied to a specific time, place, or ethnic group. They are grounded in the universal work of Christ on behalf of all who believe. When Paul says "you have died," he is describing something that is true of every Christian, in every century, on every continent.

So the original audience and I share the same Savior, the same union with Christ, the same hidden life, and the same future hope. That shared identity means the commands Paul issues apply to me just as directly as they applied to his first readers.

## What Are the Differences Between My World and Their World?

This is where careful thinking is essential. The Colossians were facing a very particular threat. False teachers had entered the church promoting a system of spiritual advancement that required supplements to Christ: Jewish dietary laws, festival observances, ascetic self-denial, angel veneration, and mystical visions (Colossians 2:16-23). The message was clear: *Christ is a good start, but He is not enough. You need more.*

I do not face that exact heresy. Nobody in my life is insisting that I observe the Jewish calendar or worship angels or practice ritual self-deprivation as a condition of spiritual growth. The specific features of the Colossian false teaching are foreign to my experience.

But the *principle* behind the Colossian heresy is not foreign at all. In fact, it is remarkably alive.

Consider how many voices in the modern Christian landscape essentially say the same thing the Colossian false teachers said, just dressed up in different clothing.

**Moralistic religion** tells me that my standing with God depends on my behavior. If I read my Bible enough, pray enough, volunteer enough, and avoid enough sins, then I am right with God. If I fall short, I need to try harder. The emphasis falls not on Christ's finished work but on my ongoing performance. This is the Colossian heresy in modern dress: Christ plus my moral effort equals spiritual fullness.

**Experiential spirituality** tells me that I need a special encounter with God beyond simple faith in Christ. Maybe it's a second blessing, a dramatic emotional experience, a spiritual gift, or a mystical encounter. The message is that ordinary Christians who trust Christ and study His Word are operating at a lower tier. You need the experience to reach the next level. This, too, is the Colossian heresy repackaged: Christ plus a special experience equals true spiritual maturity.

**Intellectual elitism** tells me that I need the right theological system, the right teacher, or the right tradition to truly understand God. Without access to this particular framework of knowledge, I am spiritually deficient. Sound familiar? It should. The Colossian heretics traded in "philosophy and empty deception" (2:8), and they judged other believers for not conforming to their system.

**Prosperity theology** tells me that God's primary concern is my comfort, health, and financial success. If I have enough faith, sow enough financial seeds, and claim enough promises, God will bless me materially. When suffering comes, it is a sign of insufficient faith. This approach replaces heavenly orientation with earthly fixation, which is precisely the opposite of what Paul commands in verse 2.

**Cultural conformity** is subtler but no less real. The pressure to conform to the values, priorities, and aspirations of the surrounding culture can slowly erode a believer's heavenly mindset. Career ambition, social status, political identity, lifestyle upgrades: none of these are inherently sinful, but when they become the primary objects of our seeking and thinking, they become the "things on earth" that compete with the "things above."

In each case, the underlying message is the same one Paul confronted in Colossae: *Christ is not enough.* You need Christ *plus* something else to be truly spiritual, truly blessed, truly complete.

Paul's answer, then and now, is the same. You have been raised with Christ. Your life is hidden with Christ in God. Christ *is* your life. What exactly are you looking to add to that?

## Is My Relationship to God Different Than Theirs?

No. And this is the most important point of all.

The Colossian believers and I stand on the same side of the cross. We are both New Covenant believers. We are both justified by grace through faith in Jesus Christ. We are both indwelt by the Holy Spirit. We are both united to the risen Christ, participants in His death and resurrection. We both await His return.

Some passages of Scripture address people whose relationship to God is fundamentally different from ours. When we read about the Mosaic Law, for instance, we recognize that we are not under that covenant. When we read God's promises to Abraham regarding the land, we recognize that those promises were made to Abraham and his descendants, not to the church. The question "Is my relationship to God different than theirs?" helps us navigate those distinctions responsibly.

But in Colossians 3:1-4, no such distinction exists. The realities Paul describes belong to every person who has trusted Christ. The imperatives Paul issues are binding on every believer. There is no dispensational, covenantal, or cultural barrier between the original audience and us when it comes to the truths in this passage.

This means the application is direct and unqualified:

**Keep seeking the things above.** This is a present-tense, ongoing command. It is not a one-time decision but a daily orientation. Seeking "things above" means actively pursuing the priorities, purposes, and perspective of the kingdom of God as they are defined by Christ's presence and authority. It means asking, in the ordinary decisions of life, "Am I directing my energy toward what is temporal or toward what is eternal?"

**Set your mind on things above, not on things on earth.** This moves from external pursuit to internal disposition. It addresses the way we think, the framework through which we process our experiences. Setting our mind on things above means training our default thought patterns to evaluate life from a heavenly vantage point rather than an earthly one. When anxiety presses in, where does my mind go? When ambition stirs, what is it reaching for? When disappointment arrives, what does my thinking anchor to?

This does not mean pretending the material world does not exist or that earthly responsibilities are unimportant. Paul himself will spend the rest of Colossians 3 and 4 discussing very earthly topics: relationships, family, work, speech, and daily conduct. "Setting your mind on things above" does not mean disengaging from ordinary life. It means engaging ordinary life from a different center of gravity.

**Rest in the security of your hidden life.** Verse 3 is not a command; it is a statement of reality. But it carries profound applicational weight. If my life is hidden with Christ in God, then my identity, my security, and my worth are not determined by anything the world can see, measure, or take away. My job does not define me. My failures do not disqualify me. My reputation does not sustain me. My life is hidden in the safest place in the universe.

This is deeply freeing. It means I do not have to perform for acceptance. I do not have to achieve for significance. I do not have to protect my identity from the opinions of others. The world cannot see who I truly am, and that is by design. My true self is concealed in Christ, and the One who holds my life is seated at the right hand of God.

**Live in light of the future revelation.** Verse 4 tells me that what is hidden will not always be hidden. When Christ returns, everything will be revealed. The quiet faithfulness that nobody noticed. The hidden struggles that nobody saw. The private worship that nobody heard. All of it will be brought into the light of glory.

This hope changes how I endure suffering. It changes how I respond to injustice. It changes how I think about obscurity and insignificance. The world may not see the reality of my life in Christ, but one day, it will. And that day will make everything else look like a footnote.

## Putting It All Together

The application of Colossians 3:1-4, properly derived from the exegetical work we have done, comes down to this:

Because I have been raised with Christ, I am to orient my entire life around Him. My pursuit, my thinking, my identity, and my hope are all anchored in the risen, reigning, returning Christ. I refuse to supplement His sufficiency with any system, experience, performance, or philosophy that claims to offer what He has already provided. I rest in the security of a life hidden in Him. And I look forward to the day when the whole world will see what God has been doing in secret all along.

That is not a vague spiritual sentiment. It is a concrete call to reorder the priorities, perspectives, and pursuits of my daily life around a Person who is alive, exalted, and coming back.

The hard work of exegesis has brought us here. And here is where the text meets the road.

## Discussion Questions

- In what areas of your life are you most tempted to seek "things on earth" rather than "things above"? What does that seeking look like practically?
- Can you identify any "Christ plus" messages in your own spiritual environment? What are they adding to the sufficiency of Christ?
- How does the concept of your life being "hidden with Christ in God" change the way you think about your identity, your security, or your worth?
- What would it look like, concretely, to "set your mind on things above" in your daily routine this week?
- How does the promise of future revelation in verse 4 affect the way you handle seasons of suffering, obscurity, or discouragement?
