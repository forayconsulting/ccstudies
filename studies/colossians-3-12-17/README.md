# Bible Study: Colossians 3:12-17

## Putting On the New Self: From Virtue to Worship to Total-Life Consecration

A study of Colossians 3:12-17 using the methodology from *Hermeneutics and Exegesis for Laypeople* by Clayton Chancey.

---

## Passage Summary

In Colossians 3:12-17, Paul commands believers — as God's chosen, holy, and beloved people — to clothe themselves with Christlike virtues: compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, and patience. These virtues are to be expressed in relational practices of mutual forbearance and forgiveness, grounded in Christ's own forgiveness. Above all, love is to be put on as the binding virtue that holds the community together in completeness. Paul then directs the church to let the peace of Christ arbitrate their hearts, to let the word of Christ dwell richly among them through teaching, admonishing, and singing, and to do everything — in word and deed — in the name of the Lord Jesus, with thanksgiving to God the Father.

This passage is the positive counterpart to 3:5-11. Where 3:5-11 described what must be stripped off (the old self's vices), 3:12-17 describes what must be put on (the new self's virtues). Together, they form a complete picture of the transformed life in Christ.

## Key Findings

### The Clothing Metaphor Completed
Colossians 3:5-17 uses a sustained clothing metaphor. Believers have "stripped off" the old self (3:9, ἀπεκδυσάμενοι) and "put on" the new self (3:10, ἐνδυσάμενοι). In 3:12-17, Paul now commands them to dress the new self in specific virtues — compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, patience — with love as the outer garment that holds everything together (v. 14). The metaphor is deliberate: just as one intentionally selects and puts on clothing each day, believers must intentionally clothe themselves in Christlike character.

### Identity Grounds Imperative
Paul does not begin with commands but with identity: "as those who have been chosen of God, holy and beloved" (v. 12). This triad — chosen, holy, beloved — was originally Israel's covenant identity (Deut 7:6-8). Now it describes the church. The ethical imperatives flow from this identity: you are to act this way because of who God has made you. Obedience is a response to grace, not a condition for it.

### Love as the Bond of Perfection
Love is not merely the most important virtue in the list — it is placed "beyond all these things" (ἐπὶ πᾶσιν, v. 14) as the overcoat that binds the other virtues together. Paul calls love "the perfect bond of unity" (σύνδεσμος τῆς τελειότητος) — the ligament that holds the body together in completeness. Without love, the other virtues are fragmented and incomplete.

### Christ Governs Every Dimension
The passage is saturated with Christ:
- The **peace of Christ** rules/arbitrates in believers' hearts (v. 15)
- The **word of Christ** dwells richly in the community (v. 16)
- Everything is done in the **name of the Lord Jesus** (v. 17)

Christ governs the inner life (peace), the corporate life (word), and the total life (name). He is not merely the occasion for transformation — He is the active, ongoing Lord of every dimension of the community's existence.

### Thanksgiving as the Defining Posture
Thanksgiving appears three times in three consecutive verses (vv. 15, 16, 17), making it the most repeated theme in the passage. This is not accidental. Thanksgiving is the antithesis of the greed (= idolatry) condemned in 3:5. Greed says "I deserve more"; thanksgiving says "God has given everything." The new self is defined not by acquisitiveness but by gratitude. A thankless Christian is a contradiction in terms.

### The Word of Christ and the Spirit's Filling
Colossians 3:16 ("Let the word of Christ richly dwell within you") and Ephesians 5:18 ("Be filled with the Spirit") produce nearly identical community outcomes: teaching, singing psalms/hymns/spiritual songs, and giving thanks. These are not competing instructions but complementary descriptions of the same reality — the Spirit works through the word, and the word is made effective by the Spirit. A community saturated with Christ's word is a Spirit-filled community.

## Methodology

This study follows the Pyramid of Interpretation:

1. **Doctrine of Inspiration** — Affirming Verbal Plenary Inspiration: this text is God-breathed and bears God's authority, while also being genuinely authored by Paul.
2. **Hermeneutics** — Using the Literal Historical Grammatical hermeneutic: a commonsense reading accounting for historical setting and linguistic choices to determine Paul's originally intended meaning.
3. **Exegesis** — Extracting meaning through literary context (Colossians as a whole, the parallel in Ephesians), historical context (Paul's imprisonment, the Colossian situation), and grammatical context (Greek word studies, conjunction analysis).
4. **Interpretation** — Arriving at authorial intent before deriving theological significance and personal application.
