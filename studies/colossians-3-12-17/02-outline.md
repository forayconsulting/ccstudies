# Colossians 3:12-17 — Outline

## Genre Identification

Colossians is an **epistle** (personal letter). The quintessential unit of thought in epistolary literature is a **sentence**. Each complete sentence is a unit to be summarized and synthesized.

---

## Book-Level Outline of Colossians

Before outlining 3:12-17, we need to understand where this passage sits within the whole letter.

    (1:1-2) Greeting

    (1:3-2:5) Thanksgiving and the Supremacy of Christ

        (1:3-8) Thanksgiving for the Colossians' Faith
        (1:9-14) Prayer for Spiritual Growth
        (1:15-20) The Supremacy of Christ over All Creation
        (1:21-23) Reconciliation through Christ
        (1:24-2:5) Paul's Ministry of the Mystery of Christ

    (2:6-23) Warning against False Teaching

        (2:6-7) Continue in Christ as You Received Him
        (2:8-15) The Sufficiency of Christ vs. Human Philosophy
        (2:16-23) Freedom from Human Regulations

    (3:1-4:6) The New Life in Christ

        (3:1-4) Seek the Things Above
        (3:5-11) Put to Death the Old Self
        (3:12-17) Put On the New Self
        (3:18-4:1) Household Instructions
        (4:2-6) Exhortation to Prayer and Wisdom

    (4:7-18) Final Greetings and Instructions

Our passage (3:12-17) falls within the practical section of the letter ("The New Life in Christ"), directly after the negative commands to remove vice (3:5-11) and directly before the household instructions (3:18-4:1). The "So" (Οὖν) of 3:12 connects the positive virtues to the entire preceding argument: because of who Christ is (1:15-20), because believers are complete in Him (2:10), because they have died and been raised with Him (3:1-4), and because they have put off the old self (3:9-10) — therefore, put on the new self's virtues.

## Sentence-Level Outline of Colossians 3:12-17

### Step 1: Divide into Sentence Units

| Unit | Reference | Text (NASB) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 3:12 | "So, as those who have been chosen of God, holy and beloved, put on a heart of compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness and patience;" |
| 2 | 3:13 | "bearing with one another, and forgiving each other, whoever has a complaint against anyone; just as the Lord forgave you, so also should you." |
| 3 | 3:14 | "Beyond all these things put on love, which is the perfect bond of unity." |
| 4 | 3:15 | "Let the peace of Christ rule in your hearts, to which indeed you were called in one body; and be thankful." |
| 5 | 3:16 | "Let the word of Christ richly dwell within you, with all wisdom teaching and admonishing one another with psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, singing with thankfulness in your hearts to God." |
| 6 | 3:17 | "Whatever you do in word or deed, do all in the name of the Lord Jesus, giving thanks through Him to God the Father." |

Note: Verse 13 is grammatically subordinate to verse 12 (the participles "bearing with" and "forgiving" modify the imperative "put on"), but it contains a distinct thought — the practice of forbearance and forgiveness — and merits separate treatment. Verse 14 introduces a new imperative ("put on love") that crowns the virtue list, warranting its own unit.

### Step 2: Summarize Each Unit

    (3:12)  As God's chosen, holy, beloved people, put on compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, and patience
    (3:13)  Bear with and forgive one another, just as the Lord forgave you
    (3:14)  Above all, put on love — the perfect bond of unity
    (3:15)  Let the peace of Christ rule in your hearts; be thankful
    (3:16)  Let the word of Christ dwell richly; teach, admonish, and sing with thanksgiving
    (3:17)  Do everything in word and deed in the name of the Lord Jesus, giving thanks to God the Father

### Step 3: Group by Commonality (Synthesis)

Three movements emerge:

1. **Vv. 12-14**: The virtues to put on — from the five foundational virtues (v. 12), through their relational expression in forgiveness (v. 13), to the crowning virtue of love (v. 14).
2. **Vv. 15-16**: The corporate life of the new community — peace ruling hearts, the word of Christ dwelling richly, mutual teaching and worship.
3. **V. 17**: The comprehensive application — all of life under Christ's lordship, with thanksgiving.

### Step 4: Title Each Movement

    (3:12-14) Clothe Yourselves with Christlike Virtues, Crowned by Love
    (3:15-16) Let Christ's Peace and Word Govern the Community
    (3:17)    Do Everything in the Name of the Lord Jesus

### Step 5: Synthesize into a Single Header

    (3:12-17) Putting On the New Self: From Virtue to Worship to Total-Life Consecration

## Key Conjunctions and Logical Movement

| Verse | Conjunction | Type | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3:12 | **So** (Οὖν) | Inferential | Draws the conclusion from everything preceding — the theological indicatives of 3:1-4 and the negative commands of 3:5-11. "Because you have been raised with Christ and have put off the old self, therefore put on..." |
| 3:13 | **bearing with...and forgiving** (ἀνεχόμενοι...καὶ χαριζόμενοι) | Coordinating (participial) | Two participles that elaborate on the virtues of v. 12, showing how compassion, kindness, and patience express themselves in relationships. The participles are adverbial, describing the manner in which the virtues are practiced. |
| 3:13b | **just as...so also** (καθὼς...οὕτως καί) | Comparative/Inferential | Establishes Christ's forgiveness as the ground and model for human forgiveness. The comparison is not merely illustrative — it is foundational. Because the Lord forgave you, therefore you must forgive. |
| 3:14 | **Beyond all these things** (ἐπὶ πᾶσιν δὲ τούτοις) | Transitional/Emphatic | Elevates love above the preceding virtues. The preposition ἐπί ("over, upon, beyond") with the dative indicates that love is placed on top of and over all the other virtues. δέ functions as a mild transitional conjunction, moving to the climax. |
| 3:14b | **which is** (ὅ ἐστιν) | Subordinating (relative) | Identifies love as "the perfect bond of unity" — a relative clause defining love's function. Love is the binding agent that holds all virtues and the community together. |
| 3:15 | **and** (καί) | Coordinating/Transitional | Links the virtue section (vv. 12-14) to the corporate-life section (vv. 15-16). The shift from "put on" virtues to "let" the peace of Christ rule marks a new phase of the argument. |
| 3:15b | **to which indeed** (εἰς ἣν καί) | Subordinating (relative) | Provides the basis for letting peace rule: you were called to this peace in one body. The calling is corporate — "in one body" — reinforcing the communal dimension. |
| 3:16 | (no explicit conjunction — asyndeton) | Transitional | The absence of a conjunction between vv. 15 and 16 creates a close connection: the peace of Christ (v. 15) and the word of Christ (v. 16) are twin realities governing the community's life. They function in parallel. |
| 3:17 | **Whatever** (πᾶν ὅ τι ἐάν) | Comprehensive/Transitional | Introduces the most sweeping command of the passage. The indefinite relative clause ("whatever you do") encompasses every possible word and deed. This is the culmination: all of life falls under Christ's lordship. |
| 3:17b | **giving thanks** (εὐχαριστοῦντες) | Coordinating (participial) | A participle of attendant circumstance: thanksgiving accompanies every act done in Christ's name. Gratitude is not an afterthought but the constant posture of the new self. |

## Observations from the Outline

1. **The passage moves from individual virtue to corporate life to total-life consecration.** There is a deliberate widening of scope: personal character (v. 12) → relational practice (v. 13) → the crowning virtue (v. 14) → corporate governance by Christ's peace and word (vv. 15-16) → everything without exception (v. 17). Paul's vision for the new self is comprehensive.

2. **Love is the pinnacle of the virtue list.** Verse 14 does not merely add love to the list — it places love "beyond" (ἐπί) all the others. Love is the σύνδεσμος τῆς τελειότητος — the bond that brings completeness, perfection, and unity. Without love, the individual virtues remain disconnected.

3. **Thanksgiving is the recurring refrain.** Gratitude appears three times: "be thankful" (v. 15), "with thankfulness" (v. 16), "giving thanks" (v. 17). Thanksgiving is not a single command but the pervasive atmosphere of the new life. It functions as the antithesis of the greed (= idolatry) condemned in 3:5 — greed says "I need more"; thanksgiving says "God has given enough."

4. **Christ governs every dimension.** The peace of *Christ* rules (v. 15). The word of *Christ* dwells (v. 16). Everything is done in the name of the Lord *Jesus* (v. 17). Christ is not merely the occasion for transformation — He is the active, ongoing Lord of the community's inner life, corporate life, and total life.

5. **This passage is the positive counterpart to 3:5-11.** The structure is intentional:
   - 3:5-11: Put off / put to death → vice lists → grounded in having put off the old self
   - 3:12-17: Put on → virtue list → grounded in being chosen, holy, beloved

   The two passages form a complete picture: the old self's garments are stripped off (3:5-11), and the new self's garments are put on (3:12-17). Neither passage is complete without the other.
