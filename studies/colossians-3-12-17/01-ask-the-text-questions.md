# Colossians 3:12-17 — Ask the Text Questions

---

## (1) What did this text mean to the human author?

### (1A) Who were they?

The human author is the Apostle Paul (Col 1:1), writing along with Timothy. Paul was a Pharisee educated under Gamaliel (Acts 22:3), a former persecutor of the church dramatically converted on the road to Damascus (Acts 9). By the time he wrote Colossians, Paul was a seasoned missionary and church planter who had spent decades preaching to Gentiles across the Roman Empire. He was a Roman citizen by birth (Acts 22:28) and a Hebrew of Hebrews (Phil 3:5).

Paul's dual background — deeply formed in Jewish Torah scholarship and fluent in Greco-Roman culture — uniquely equipped him to address the Colossian church. His commands in 3:12-17 carry apostolic authority: when Paul tells believers to "put on" compassion, kindness, and love, he writes as one commissioned by Christ Himself. The warmth and pastoral tenderness of this passage — addressing the Colossians as "chosen of God, holy and beloved" — reflects Paul's deep care for a church he had never personally visited but loved through Epaphras's ministry.

### (1B) When did they write this?

Paul wrote Colossians during his first Roman imprisonment, approximately AD 60-62 (Col 4:3, 10, 18). This places the letter in the early years of Nero's reign, before the persecutions of AD 64. The Roman Empire was at the height of its power, and Greco-Roman society was marked by rigid social stratification, patron-client relationships, and a morality often hostile to the virtues Paul commands. Compassion was generally viewed as weakness in Roman culture; humility was not a virtue but a mark of low status. Paul's virtue list in v. 12 would have been counter-cultural.

Paul wrote Colossians around the same time as Ephesians, Philemon, and Philippians (the "Prison Epistles"). The close relationship between Colossians and Ephesians is especially notable: the virtue list and communal instructions of Colossians 3:12-17 have extensive parallels in Ephesians 4:1-6, 4:32-5:2, and 5:15-21.

### (1C) Where did they live?

Paul was writing from Rome under house arrest (Acts 28:16, 30-31). He had freedom to receive visitors and preach, but was chained to a Roman guard.

The recipients lived in Colossae, a small city in the Lycus Valley of Phrygia (modern-day western Turkey), about 100 miles east of Ephesus. Colossae sat along a major trade route near Laodicea and Hierapolis (Col 4:13). The population was a mixture of native Phrygians, Greeks, and a significant Jewish community settled there by Antiochus III around 200 BC.

This mixed demographic context is critical for 3:12-17. The Colossian church was a diverse congregation of Jewish and Gentile believers from different ethnic, cultural, and socioeconomic backgrounds. Paul's commands to "bear with one another," "forgive each other," and let the "peace of Christ rule" address the real relational friction that existed in this pluralistic community. The virtues of v. 12 are not abstract ideals — they are the specific relational qualities needed for a multi-ethnic church to survive and thrive.

### (1D) What were they addressing?

Paul had never personally visited Colossae (Col 2:1). The church was founded by Epaphras (Col 1:7; 4:12-13), who traveled to Rome to report on the church's condition. His report prompted this letter.

The central problem was the "Colossian heresy" — a false teaching blending Jewish legalism, Greek philosophy, and mystical practices. This false teaching emphasized human tradition over Christ (2:8), imposed dietary restrictions and festival observance (2:16), promoted angel worship and claimed special visions (2:18), and advocated severe bodily treatment as a path to spirituality (2:20-23).

In 3:12-17, Paul provides the positive counterpart to 3:5-11. Having told the Colossians what to "put off" (vices) and "put to death" (sins of desire), he now tells them what to "put on" — the virtues that characterize the new self. This is critical to Paul's argument against the false teaching: the Colossian heresy offered external rules and self-made religion as the path to holiness (2:23). Paul counters with a vision of genuine community transformation rooted in Christ — not in ascetic discipline, but in Christlike character expressed in loving relationships, mutual forgiveness, corporate worship, and a life wholly oriented toward the Lord Jesus.

The passage also addresses a practical need: this diverse church needed concrete instruction on how to live together. The virtues of v. 12 (compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, patience) are specifically relational. The commands in vv. 13-14 (bearing with, forgiving, loving) are explicitly communal. The instructions in vv. 15-16 (peace ruling, the word dwelling, teaching and admonishing one another) are corporate practices. Paul is building a community, not just improving individuals.

### (1E) What was in their Bible?

Paul had access to the entire Old Testament and likely many New Testament writings that preceded his prison epistles — his own earlier letters (Romans, 1-2 Corinthians, Galatians, 1-2 Thessalonians), likely the epistle of James, and possibly the Gospel of Mark and an early form of Luke's Gospel (given Luke's presence during this imprisonment — Col 4:14).

The Colossian believers, being largely Gentile, may not have had extensive personal familiarity with the Old Testament, but Epaphras's teaching would have grounded them in its themes. Paul's language in this passage draws on rich Old Testament background:

- **"Chosen of God, holy and beloved"** (v. 12) — Language drawn directly from Israel's identity. God "chose" Israel (Deut 7:6-7; Isa 43:20), declared them "holy" (set apart — Exod 19:6; Lev 20:26), and called them "beloved" (Deut 33:12; Isa 5:1; Hos 11:1). Paul transfers this covenant language to the church, affirming that Gentile believers share in Israel's identity as God's treasured people.
- **"Compassion"** (σπλάγχνα, v. 12) — Echoes God's own compassion for Israel. The Lord is described as "compassionate and gracious" (Exod 34:6; Ps 103:8; 145:8). Believers are to mirror God's character.
- **"Just as the Lord forgave you"** (v. 13) — Echoes God's pattern of forgiveness throughout the Old Testament (Exod 34:6-7; Num 14:18-19; Neh 9:17; Ps 103:3, 10-12). The standard for forgiving others is God's own forgiveness.
- **"Psalms and hymns and spiritual songs"** (v. 16) — The Psalter was Israel's worship book. Paul's instruction to use "psalms" connects the Colossian church's worship directly to the Old Testament liturgical tradition while expanding it with "hymns and spiritual songs" appropriate to the new covenant era.
- **"In the name of the Lord"** (v. 17) — A phrase rooted in the Old Testament practice of acting under the authority and for the honor of God's name (1 Sam 17:45; Ps 118:26; Mic 4:5).

## (2) What does this text teach me about God?

### (A) What is God saying or doing in this text?

Through Paul's apostolic instruction, God is commanding believers to clothe themselves with Christlike virtues and to build a community marked by forgiveness, love, peace, and worship. This is not moral advice but divine imperative — God is defining what the new humanity looks like in practice.

God is also revealing His own character as the standard for the believer's conduct. The command to forgive is grounded in God's forgiveness: "just as the Lord forgave you, so also should you" (v. 13). God's prior action is the model and motivation for human behavior.

God is calling believers into a worshiping community where "the word of Christ" dwells richly (v. 16) — God wants His truth to permeate the life of the church through teaching, admonishing, and singing. The goal is a community saturated with Christ.

And God is claiming total lordship over every aspect of life: "Whatever you do in word or deed, do all in the name of the Lord Jesus" (v. 17). No area of life falls outside Christ's authority.

### (B) Why is God saying or doing this?

1. **God is completing the picture of the new self.** Colossians 3:5-11 described what must be removed (the old self's vices); 3:12-17 describes what must replace them (the new self's virtues). God does not leave believers in a moral vacuum — He fills the space left by sin with Christlike character.

2. **God is forming a community, not just reforming individuals.** The virtues listed are specifically relational (compassion, patience, forgiveness, love). God's purpose is not merely personal holiness but corporate witness — a people who display Christ to the world through how they treat one another.

3. **God is countering the Colossian heresy with the real thing.** The false teachers offered external rules, ascetic practices, and mystical experiences as the path to spiritual maturity (2:16-23). God, through Paul, shows that authentic spiritual life looks like love, forgiveness, peace, gratitude, and the indwelling word of Christ — not self-made religion.

4. **God is establishing worship as the heart of the Christian life.** Verses 15-17 move from individual virtue to corporate worship to total-life consecration. God desires a people whose entire existence — their hearts, their community, their words, their deeds — is oriented toward Christ in thanksgiving.

### (C) How is God saying or doing this?

God communicates through **special revelation** — the inspired writing of the Apostle Paul. This is pastoral instruction carried by Tychicus (Col 4:7-8) to be read aloud to the congregation. God uses Paul's imprisonment, Epaphras's report, and the letter's delivery to accomplish His purposes through **providential means**.

The Holy Spirit superintended Paul's writing so that the words bear divine authority (2 Tim 3:16-17). The warmth and tenderness of the passage ("chosen of God, holy and beloved") reflects both Paul's pastoral heart and God's love for His people — a clear expression of dual authorship.

God is also working through the corporate life of the church itself. Verse 16 reveals a community where believers teach and admonish "one another" — God uses His people as instruments of His truth. The word of Christ dwells not merely in individual hearts but "within you" (plural) — in the gathered community.

### (D) Which of God's attributes are on display?

- **Love** — God's love is the foundation of the passage. The believers are "beloved" (v. 12), and love is "the perfect bond of unity" (v. 14). God's character as love (1 John 4:8) is the source and model for the love believers are commanded to practice.
- **Grace/Mercy** — "Just as the Lord forgave you" (v. 13). God's forgiveness — unearned, undeserved, and complete — is both the model and the motivation for believers to forgive one another. God's grace precedes and enables human obedience.
- **Holiness** — The Colossians are called "holy" (v. 12), set apart by God for His purposes. God's holiness is communicated to His people through their calling and is to be expressed in their character.
- **Sovereignty** — God "chose" the Colossians (v. 12). Their identity as God's people is rooted in His sovereign election, not in their merit. God's sovereignty also underlies the comprehensive claim of v. 17: all of life belongs to the Lord.
- **Peace** — "The peace of Christ" (v. 15) is to rule in believers' hearts. This reflects Christ's role as peacemaker (Col 1:20 — "having made peace through the blood of His cross") and God's nature as the God of peace (Phil 4:9; 1 Thess 5:23).
- **Wisdom** — "With all wisdom teaching and admonishing one another" (v. 16). God is a God of wisdom (Rom 11:33), and His wisdom is to characterize the community's instruction. This also counters the false "wisdom" of the Colossian heresy (2:23).

## (3) How should this text contribute to my theology?

### (A) How does this text advance the metanarrative of Scripture?

Colossians 3:12-17 advances the metanarrative of Scripture at the intersection of Redemption and Restoration:

- **Creation**: The virtues Paul commands reflect the character of God in whose image humanity was originally made (Gen 1:26-27). Compassion, kindness, love, and peace are not arbitrary moral standards — they are attributes of God Himself. Putting on these virtues is part of the image-restoration described in 3:10.

- **Fall**: The relational sins addressed in 3:5-11 (anger, wrath, malice, slander) are the fruit of the fall, which shattered both the divine-human relationship and human-to-human relationships (Gen 3-4). The virtues of 3:12-17 are the reversal of the fall's relational destruction.

- **Redemption**: The passage is grounded in God's redemptive work. The Colossians can forgive because the Lord forgave them (v. 13). The peace of Christ rules because Christ made peace through His blood (1:20). The new community Paul describes is the direct result of Christ's death and resurrection.

- **Restoration**: This passage paints a picture of the coming new creation in microcosm. A community bound by love, ruled by peace, saturated with Christ's word, and doing everything in His name — this is an anticipation of the eschatological reality where God will be all in all (1 Cor 15:28). The church, as described here, is a foretaste of the restored creation.

### (B) How does this text point to Christ?

Christ permeates this entire passage:

- **Christ as the model for virtue** — The virtues Paul commands (compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, patience) are the character traits of Christ Himself. Philippians 2:5-8 describes Christ's humility; the Gospels portray His compassion (Matt 9:36), gentleness (Matt 11:29), and patience. Believers are to "put on" what Christ already is.

- **Christ as the forgiver** — "Just as the Lord forgave you" (v. 13). Christ's forgiveness, accomplished through the cross (Col 1:14; 2:13-14), is both the ground and the standard for human forgiveness. The believer does not generate forgiveness from within — it flows from having been forgiven by Christ.

- **Christ as the bond of peace** — "The peace of Christ" (v. 15) recalls 1:20, where Christ "made peace through the blood of His cross." His atoning death reconciled believers to God and to one another. This peace now "rules" (βραβευέτω — acts as umpire/arbitrator) in the community.

- **Christ as the indwelling word** — "The word of Christ" (v. 16) is to dwell richly in the community. Christ's teaching, His gospel, His truth — this is the content that fills the church. Christ is not merely a historical figure to be remembered but a living reality whose word continues to shape His people.

- **Christ as Lord of all** — "In the name of the Lord Jesus" (v. 17). Every word and deed is to be done under Christ's authority and for His glory. Christ's lordship is total — encompassing not just worship but every dimension of human existence.

### (D) Which areas of systematic theology relate?

- **Soteriology** (the study of salvation) — The passage assumes the reality of election ("chosen of God," v. 12) and justification ("the Lord forgave you," v. 13) and addresses sanctification — the progressive growth of the believer in Christlike character. The indicative-imperative pattern continues: because God has chosen, sanctified, and forgiven, therefore put on, forgive, and love.

- **Christology** (the study of Christ) — Christ is central: His forgiveness is the model for ours (v. 13), His peace rules the community (v. 15), His word fills the church (v. 16), and His name governs all of life (v. 17). The passage presupposes Christ's deity, lordship, and ongoing active presence in the church.

- **Ecclesiology** (the study of the church) — This is one of the richest ecclesiological texts in the New Testament. The church is described as a community of the chosen, holy, and beloved (v. 12); a forgiving community (v. 13); a community bound by love (v. 14); a body called to peace (v. 15); a teaching, admonishing, worshiping community (v. 16); and a community that does everything in the name of the Lord (v. 17). The corporate dimension is unmistakable — these are not instructions for isolated individuals but for a body.

- **Pneumatology** (the study of the Holy Spirit) — While the Spirit is not explicitly named in this passage, the parallel in Ephesians 5:18-21 attributes the same communal dynamics (singing, thanksgiving, mutual submission) to being "filled with the Spirit." What Colossians attributes to the "word of Christ" dwelling richly, Ephesians attributes to the Spirit's filling — suggesting these are complementary descriptions of the same reality.

- **Theology Proper** (the study of God) — God is the one who chose, sanctified, and loved the Colossians (v. 12). He is the God of peace (v. 15), the God who forgives (v. 13), and the Father to whom thanks is given through Christ (v. 17). The passage reveals God as a relational being who forms a relational people.

- **Doxology/Worship** — Verses 15-17 describe the worship life of the church. Thanksgiving is mentioned three times (vv. 15, 16, 17), making gratitude a defining mark of the new community. Worship involves psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs (v. 16) — the church is a singing community. And all of life is worship: "whatever you do in word or deed" (v. 17).

## (4) How does this truth apply to me?

### (A) What is my relationship to the human author?

Paul is an apostle of Jesus Christ — uniquely commissioned by the risen Lord. I am not an apostle, nor do I hold apostolic authority. However, as a believer in Christ, I am a beneficiary of Paul's apostolic ministry. His inspired letters carry the authority of God's Word and are binding on all Christians in every era (2 Tim 3:16-17). The tender address of v. 12 — "chosen of God, holy and beloved" — applies to me as it applied to the Colossians, because it describes the identity of all who are in Christ.

### (B) What is my relationship to the original audience?

Like the Colossians, I am a believer who has been brought into the people of God through faith in Christ. Like them, I am part of a diverse church community that requires compassion, patience, forgiveness, and love to function. Like them, I face the temptation to substitute external religious practices for genuine Christlike character. The communal virtues Paul commands are as necessary in my church as they were in Colossae.

### (C) What are the differences between my world and their world?

- The Colossians were first-generation believers in a pre-Christian, pluralistic culture. Many had recently converted from paganism and were still learning what a Christian community looked like. Most modern Western Christians have inherited centuries of Christian cultural tradition, which can lead to nominalism — going through the motions without genuine transformation.
- Roman culture despised the virtues Paul commends. Humility was weakness; compassion was sentimentality; patience was passivity. While contemporary Western culture gives lip service to some of these virtues, the underlying values of self-promotion, entitlement, and outrage culture are equally hostile to the Christlike character Paul describes.
- The specific false teaching in Colossae (angel worship, dietary legalism, ascetic mysticism) is not common today, but the underlying pattern is ever-present: the temptation to add something to Christ — performance-based religion, political ideology, emotional experience, moral self-improvement programs — rather than allowing Christ's word to dwell richly and His lordship to govern all of life.
- The communal context has shifted. The Colossian church likely met in a home (cf. Philemon 2), in close relational proximity. Modern church culture can be more anonymous and individualistic, making the "one another" commands of vv. 13, 16 harder to practice but no less necessary.

### (D) Is my relationship to God different than theirs?

No. As a New Testament believer, my relationship to God is the same as the Colossians'. I have been chosen by God, declared holy, and loved (v. 12). I have been forgiven by the Lord (v. 13). I have been called into one body (v. 15). The same indicative-imperative dynamic applies to me: because God has done these things, I must respond with obedience — putting on compassion, forgiving as I have been forgiven, letting Christ's peace rule, letting His word dwell richly, and doing everything in His name. These commands apply to me directly and without qualification.

### Summary Application

1. **I must clothe myself with Christlike virtues — deliberately and daily.** The command to "put on" (ἐνδύσασθε) is a decisive act, like getting dressed. Compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, and patience are not optional personality traits — they are the required wardrobe of God's chosen people. I must actively cultivate these in my relationships.

2. **I must forgive as I have been forgiven.** The standard for forgiveness is not fairness but grace: "just as the Lord forgave you." This means forgiving completely, freely, and without keeping a record. Any complaint I hold against another believer must be released in light of the immeasurably greater debt Christ has forgiven in me.

3. **I must prioritize love as the binding virtue.** Love is not one virtue among many — it is "the perfect bond of unity" (v. 14) that holds all other virtues together. Without love, the other virtues become mere performance. Love is the integrating center of the Christian life.

4. **I must let the peace of Christ arbitrate my decisions and relationships.** When conflict, anxiety, or confusion arise in the community or in my heart, Christ's peace should function as the ruling authority — the umpire who settles disputes. This requires cultivating an inner disposition of trust in Christ's sovereignty.

5. **I must be saturated with the word of Christ.** The word of Christ is to dwell "richly" — not superficially, not occasionally, but abundantly and deeply. This happens through personal study, corporate teaching, mutual admonishment, and worship. A community that is thin on Scripture will be thin on spiritual health.

6. **I must do everything — all words and all deeds — in the name of the Lord Jesus.** This is the most comprehensive command in the passage. There is no sacred/secular divide. Every conversation, every task, every relationship falls under Christ's lordship. The test for any word or action is: can this be done in Jesus' name, for His glory, with thanksgiving?
