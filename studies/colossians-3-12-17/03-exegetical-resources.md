# Colossians 3:12-17 — Exegetical Resources and Notes

This document records observations, questions, and findings from the use of exegetical tools, following the methodology in Chapter 7 of *Hermeneutics and Exegesis for Laypeople*.

---

## Key Greek Words and Phrases

### Verse 12

- **Οὖν** (*oun*) — "So / Therefore." An inferential conjunction drawing the practical conclusion from the preceding argument. This single word connects the positive virtue commands to everything Paul has said: Christ's supremacy (1:15-20), the believer's completeness in Him (2:10), the rejection of human regulations (2:16-23), the heavenly orientation (3:1-4), and the removal of the old self (3:5-11). The "therefore" of 3:12 carries the weight of the entire letter.

- **ἐκλεκτοὶ τοῦ θεοῦ, ἅγιοι καὶ ἠγαπημένοι** (*eklektoi tou theou, hagioi kai ēgapēmenoi*) — "Chosen of God, holy and beloved." Three identity markers that ground the commands that follow:
  - **ἐκλεκτοί** ("chosen/elect") — A term applied to Israel in the Old Testament (Isa 43:20; 45:4; 65:9) now applied to the church. God's sovereign choice is the foundation of identity.
  - **ἅγιοι** ("holy ones / saints") — Set apart by God for His purposes. Not a moral achievement but a positional reality.
  - **ἠγαπημένοι** ("beloved") — A perfect passive participle: "having been loved and continuing to be loved." God's love is the ongoing reality in which believers live. The same term is used of Christ Himself (Matt 3:17; Col 1:13, "the Son of His love").

  This triad echoes Israel's covenant identity (Deut 7:6-8) and is now extended to the church. Paul grounds ethical imperatives in theological identity: you are to act this way because of who God has made you.

- **ἐνδύσασθε** (*endysasthe*) — "Put on / clothe yourselves." Aorist middle imperative of ἐνδύω. The clothing metaphor that began with "put off" (ἀπόθεσθε, 3:8) and "stripped off" (ἀπεκδυσάμενοι, 3:9) now reaches its positive counterpart. The aorist tense conveys decisive action: put these on now. The middle voice suggests personal involvement — you yourself must do the putting on. The same verb is used in Romans 13:14 ("put on the Lord Jesus Christ") and Galatians 3:27 ("clothed yourselves with Christ"), linking the metaphor to union with Christ.

- **σπλάγχνα οἰκτιρμοῦ** (*splanchna oiktirmou*) — "A heart of compassion" (literally, "bowels of mercy/compassion"). σπλάγχνα refers to the internal organs — the gut, the viscera — which in ancient thought were the seat of deep emotion. This is not superficial sympathy but gut-level compassion, the kind that moves a person to action. The same term describes Christ's compassion (Matt 9:36; Mark 1:41 — ἐσπλαγχνίσθη, "He was moved with compassion") and God's mercy (Luke 1:78 — σπλάγχνα ἐλέους, "the tender mercy of our God").

- **χρηστότητα** (*chrēstotēta*) — "Kindness." Active goodness expressed toward others. Used of God's kindness in Romans 2:4 ("the kindness of God leads you to repentance") and Romans 11:22 ("the kindness and severity of God"). A fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:22).

- **ταπεινοφροσύνην** (*tapeinophrosynēn*) — "Humility." Literally "lowliness of mind." In Greco-Roman culture, this was a negative quality — servile, weak, degrading. Christianity transformed it into a virtue, modeled by Christ Himself (Phil 2:3-8). Notably, the same word appears in Col 2:18, 23, where it describes the false teachers' false "humility" (self-abasement). Paul reclaims the term: true humility is Christlike lowliness, not self-made ascetic performance.

- **πραΰτητα** (*prautēta*) — "Gentleness / meekness." Not weakness but controlled strength. The word describes a powerful horse under the rider's control. Jesus describes Himself as "gentle" (πραΰς) in Matthew 11:29. Listed as a fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:23).

- **μακροθυμίαν** (*makrothymian*) — "Patience / longsuffering." Literally "long-tempered" — the opposite of being quick to anger. Used of God's patience in Romans 2:4, 9:22 and 2 Peter 3:9. This virtue is specifically relational: it is patience with people, not merely patience with circumstances.

### Verse 13

- **ἀνεχόμενοι ἀλλήλων** (*anechomenoi allēlōn*) — "Bearing with one another." Present middle participle of ἀνέχομαι — to endure, tolerate, put up with. The present tense indicates an ongoing practice. This is the realistic recognition that life in community involves friction, irritation, and disappointment. The command is not to pretend these don't exist but to bear with them.

- **χαριζόμενοι ἑαυτοῖς** (*charizomenoi heautois*) — "Forgiving each other." From χαρίζομαι — a word rooted in χάρις (grace). To forgive is literally to "grace" someone — to extend unmerited favor. The same verb describes God's forgiveness in 2:13 ("having forgiven us all our transgressions") and Ephesians 4:32.

- **μομφήν** (*momphēn*) — "Complaint / grievance." Only here in the New Testament. Refers to a legitimate grievance, not a petty annoyance. Paul acknowledges that real offenses occur in the community — and commands forgiveness anyway.

- **καθὼς καὶ ὁ κύριος ἐχαρίσατο ὑμῖν** (*kathōs kai ho kyrios echarisato hymin*) — "Just as the Lord forgave you." The aorist tense of ἐχαρίσατο points to a specific, decisive act of forgiveness — Christ's forgiveness at the cross. "The Lord" (ὁ κύριος) is Christ. His forgiveness is not merely the model ("forgive like this") but the ground ("because He forgave you, therefore you must forgive"). The Ephesians parallel makes this even more explicit: "forgiving each other, just as God in Christ also has forgiven you" (Eph 4:32).

### Verse 14

- **ἐπὶ πᾶσιν δὲ τούτοις** (*epi pasin de toutois*) — "Beyond all these things" or "Over all these things." The preposition ἐπί with the dative indicates that love is placed on top of, over, and beyond the other virtues. Love is the outer garment — the overcoat that goes over all the other clothing. This is not merely one more virtue added to the list; it is the virtue that crowns and completes them all.

- **τὴν ἀγάπην** (*tēn agapēn*) — "Love." The definite article ("the love") may indicate that Paul is referring to a specific, well-known quality — the love that defines Christianity, the love modeled by Christ (John 13:34-35; 1 Cor 13). ἀγάπη is self-giving, other-oriented love that seeks the good of the beloved regardless of cost.

- **ὅ ἐστιν σύνδεσμος τῆς τελειότητος** (*ho estin syndesmos tēs teleiotētos*) — "Which is the perfect bond of unity." This phrase is exegetically dense:
  - **σύνδεσμος** ("bond, ligament, that which binds together") — Used in Col 2:19 of the "ligaments" that hold the body together. Love is the binding agent of the Christian community.
  - **τελειότητος** ("perfection, completeness, maturity") — The genitive can be read in multiple ways: (a) the bond that produces perfection/unity, (b) the bond that characterizes perfection, or (c) the perfect bond. All three are legitimate. The point: love is what holds the virtues — and the community — together in completeness. Without love, the other virtues are incomplete.

### Verse 15

- **ἡ εἰρήνη τοῦ Χριστοῦ** (*hē eirēnē tou Christou*) — "The peace of Christ." Not merely inner tranquility but the objective peace Christ accomplished through the cross (Col 1:20 — "having made peace through the blood of His cross"). This is both vertical (peace with God) and horizontal (peace within the community). The genitive "of Christ" indicates that Christ is the source and giver of this peace.

- **βραβευέτω** (*brabeuetō*) — "Let it rule." Present active imperative of βραβεύω — a term from the athletic arena meaning "to act as umpire, to arbitrate, to decide." Used only here in the New Testament. The metaphor is vivid: when competing impulses, disagreements, or decisions arise, the peace of Christ should function as the referee who makes the final call. This is not passivity but active governance — letting Christ's peace have the deciding authority.

- **ἐν ἑνὶ σώματι** (*en heni sōmati*) — "In one body." The Colossians were called to peace as members of one body — the church. This reinforces the corporate dimension. Peace is not merely a private spiritual experience; it is the calling of a community.

- **εὐχάριστοι γίνεσθε** (*eucharistoi ginesthe*) — "Be thankful." Present imperative of γίνομαι — "become, be, continually be." Thanksgiving is to be the settled, ongoing disposition of the community. This is the first of three references to thanksgiving in vv. 15-17, establishing gratitude as a defining mark of the new self.

### Verse 16

- **ὁ λόγος τοῦ Χριστοῦ** (*ho logos tou Christou*) — "The word of Christ." This phrase appears only here in the New Testament. The genitive "of Christ" can be subjective (the word spoken by Christ), objective (the word about Christ), or both. In context, it likely encompasses both: the gospel message about Christ and the teachings of Christ. This is the content that fills the community — Christ's truth in all its fullness.

- **πλουσίως** (*plousiōs*) — "Richly." An adverb meaning abundantly, lavishly. The word of Christ is not to dwell in the community in a thin, superficial way but richly — permeating every aspect of corporate life. The same adverb appears in 1 Tim 6:17 ("God, who richly supplies us with all things to enjoy") and Titus 3:6 ("the Holy Spirit, whom He poured out upon us richly").

- **ἐνοικείτω** (*enoikeitō*) — "Let it dwell." Present active imperative of ἐνοικέω — "to dwell in, to be at home in." The compound prefix ἐν- ("in") emphasizes indwelling. The word of Christ is not a visitor to the community but a permanent resident. The same verb describes the Holy Spirit's indwelling in Romans 8:11 and 2 Timothy 1:14.

- **ἐν πάσῃ σοφίᾳ** (*en pasē sophia*) — "With all wisdom." Wisdom is the context or manner of the teaching and admonishing. The mention of wisdom is pointed in Colossians: the false teachers claimed to offer wisdom (2:3, 23), but Paul has already declared that in Christ "are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (2:3). True wisdom comes from the word of Christ, not from human philosophy.

- **διδάσκοντες καὶ νουθετοῦντες ἑαυτούς** (*didaskontes kai nouthetountes heautous*) — "Teaching and admonishing one another." Two complementary activities:
  - **διδάσκοντες** ("teaching") — Positive instruction in truth.
  - **νουθετοῦντες** ("admonishing") — Corrective warning. νουθετέω literally means "to put in mind" — to correct someone's thinking. It implies that something is wrong that needs addressing.

  The reflexive pronoun ἑαυτούς ("yourselves / one another") indicates that this is a mutual, congregational responsibility — not the exclusive domain of a single teacher or leader.

- **ψαλμοῖς, ὕμνοις, ᾠδαῖς πνευματικαῖς** (*psalmois, hymnois, ōdais pneumatikais*) — "With psalms and hymns and spiritual songs." Whether these are three distinct categories or overlapping descriptions is debated. The most common understanding:
  - **ψαλμοί** ("psalms") — Old Testament psalms, originally accompanied by stringed instruments. Connects church worship to Israel's liturgical tradition.
  - **ὕμνοι** ("hymns") — Songs of praise to God, likely including early Christian compositions. Some scholars identify Col 1:15-20 as an early Christian hymn.
  - **ᾠδαὶ πνευματικαί** ("spiritual songs") — Songs inspired by or expressive of spiritual truth. The adjective πνευματικαῖς ("spiritual") modifies this category specifically or all three categories.

  The key point is that singing is a means of teaching and admonishing — worship is not merely emotional expression but a vehicle for truth.

- **ἐν [τῇ] χάριτι ᾄδοντες ἐν ταῖς καρδίαις ὑμῶν τῷ θεῷ** (*en [tē] chariti adontes en tais kardiais hymōn tō theō*) — "Singing with thankfulness in your hearts to God." The phrase ἐν τῇ χάριτι can be translated "with grace/thankfulness" (the internal disposition) or "by grace" (the means). Either way, the singing is heartfelt ("in your hearts") and directed to God (τῷ θεῷ). Worship is ultimately vertical, even when it involves horizontal teaching and admonishing.

### Verse 17

- **πᾶν ὅ τι ἐὰν ποιῆτε** (*pan ho ti ean poiēte*) — "Whatever you do." The most comprehensive possible expression: πᾶν ("all, everything") combined with ὅ τι ἐάν (an indefinite relative clause — "whatever, no matter what"). Nothing is excluded. There is no domain of life outside the scope of this command.

- **ἐν λόγῳ ἢ ἐν ἔργῳ** (*en logō ē en ergō*) — "In word or in deed." The two domains of human activity — speech and action — are both included. This echoes the distinction Paul made in the vice lists: sins of speech (3:8-9) and sins of action (3:5). Just as every kind of sin must be put off, so every kind of activity must be brought under Christ's lordship.

- **ἐν ὀνόματι κυρίου Ἰησοῦ** (*en onomati kyriou Iēsou*) — "In the name of the Lord Jesus." "In the name of" means under the authority of, as a representative of, and for the glory of. The full title "Lord Jesus" combines the confession of Jesus' deity/sovereignty (κύριος) with His human identity (Ἰησοῦς). Every act is to be performed as one who belongs to and represents the Lord Jesus.

- **εὐχαριστοῦντες τῷ θεῷ πατρὶ δι' αὐτοῦ** (*eucharistountes tō theō patri di' autou*) — "Giving thanks to God the Father through Him." The Trinitarian shape of Christian life: the believer gives thanks to God the Father, through the mediation of Christ the Son. Thanksgiving is the constant accompaniment of all Christian activity. The preposition δι' αὐτοῦ ("through Him") affirms Christ's ongoing mediatorial role (cf. 1 Tim 2:5; Heb 7:25).

## Questions Arising from Study

1. **What is the relationship between "the peace of Christ" (v. 15) and "the word of Christ" (v. 16)?** These two commands stand in parallel: let peace rule, let the word dwell. Are these two aspects of one reality — Christ governing the community through His truth and His peace? The Ephesians parallel attributes this dynamic to the Holy Spirit (Eph 5:18), suggesting that the peace of Christ, the word of Christ, and the Spirit's filling are complementary descriptions of the same spiritual reality.

2. **Why is love placed "beyond all these things" (v. 14) rather than listed alongside the other virtues?** Paul elevates love above the list. Love is not merely the most important virtue — it is the virtue that integrates and perfects all the others. Without love, compassion becomes patronizing, kindness becomes transactional, patience becomes passive-aggressive. Love is the "bond of perfection" that holds everything together.

3. **What is the relationship between the three references to thanksgiving (vv. 15, 16, 17)?** Thanksgiving appears as a command ("be thankful," v. 15), a manner of worship ("with thankfulness," v. 16), and an accompaniment to all activity ("giving thanks," v. 17). This progressive repetition suggests that gratitude is the fundamental disposition of the new self — the antidote to the greed (= idolatry) of 3:5. Greed says, "I deserve more." Thanksgiving says, "God has given everything."

4. **How does "teaching and admonishing one another" (v. 16) relate to singing?** The grammar suggests that the teaching and admonishing happen *through* the psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs. Singing is not merely an emotional exercise but a cognitive and communal one — a way of embedding truth in hearts and correcting error through beauty. This elevates corporate worship from entertainment to edification.

5. **How comprehensive is "in the name of the Lord Jesus" (v. 17)?** Paul leaves no exceptions: πᾶν ὅ τι ἐάν — "whatever." This abolishes the sacred/secular divide. Eating, working, resting, speaking, serving — all fall under Christ's lordship. The question for every activity becomes: can I do this as a representative of Jesus, under His authority, for His glory?

## Parallel Passage Comparison: Colossians 3:12-17 and Ephesians 4:1-6, 4:32-5:2, 5:18-21

These passages were likely written at the same time and share extensive thematic and verbal parallels.

| Theme | Colossians 3 | Ephesians |
|---|---|---|
| Virtue list | "compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness and patience" (3:12) | "with all humility and gentleness, with patience" (4:2) |
| Bearing with one another | "bearing with one another" (3:13) | "showing tolerance for one another in love" (4:2) |
| Unity/bond | "love, which is the perfect bond of unity" (3:14) | "being diligent to preserve the unity of the Spirit in the bond of peace" (4:3) |
| One body | "called in one body" (3:15) | "There is one body and one Spirit" (4:4) |
| Forgiveness grounded in Christ | "just as the Lord forgave you, so also should you" (3:13) | "forgiving each other, just as God in Christ also has forgiven you" (4:32) |
| Love as the standard | "put on love" (3:14) | "walk in love, just as Christ also loved you and gave Himself up for us" (5:2) |
| Psalms, hymns, spiritual songs | "with psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, singing with thankfulness in your hearts to God" (3:16) | "speaking to one another in psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, singing and making melody with your heart to the Lord" (5:19) |
| Thanksgiving | "be thankful" (3:15); "with thankfulness" (3:16); "giving thanks" (3:17) | "always giving thanks for all things in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ to God, even the Father" (5:20) |
| Source of communal life | "Let the word of Christ richly dwell within you" (3:16) | "Be filled with the Spirit" (5:18) |

The most striking parallel is the last row: what Colossians attributes to "the word of Christ dwelling richly," Ephesians attributes to "being filled with the Spirit." The resulting community practices are nearly identical (teaching, singing, thanksgiving). This suggests that the word of Christ and the Spirit's filling are complementary — the Spirit works through the word, and the word is made effective by the Spirit. Neither passage contradicts the other; together they provide a fuller picture.

The parallels confirm that Paul was addressing the same ethical-communal framework in both letters, adapted to each congregation's needs. Colossians emphasizes Christ's sufficiency (against the Colossian heresy), while Ephesians emphasizes the Spirit-filled unity of the church (the "mystery" of Jew-Gentile inclusion).
