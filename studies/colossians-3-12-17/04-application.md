# Colossians 3:12-17 — Application

This document moves from meaning (authorial intent) and significance (theology) to application (personal and corporate action), following the methodology's requirement that application be reserved until after exegesis.

---

## Foundation: From Identity to Action

Paul grounds every command in this passage in the believer's identity: "chosen of God, holy and beloved" (v. 12). Application must follow the same order. We do not put on these virtues to become God's people — we put them on because we already are God's people. Any application that reverses this order — making obedience the condition rather than the response — misreads the passage.

---

## Verse-by-Verse Application

### Verse 12 — Put On Compassion, Kindness, Humility, Gentleness, and Patience

The aorist imperative "put on" (ἐνδύσασθε) calls for decisive, intentional action — like selecting clothes in the morning. These virtues are not personality traits some people naturally have and others don't. They are garments every believer is commanded to wear.

**Personal:**

- **Compassion** (σπλάγχνα οἰκτιρμοῦ — gut-level mercy): Am I moved by the suffering of others, or do I insulate myself from it? Compassion is not a feeling I wait for — it is a posture I choose. This means entering into the pain of others rather than offering easy fixes or emotional distance. Practically: when someone shares a burden, my first response should be to feel it with them, not to solve it for them.

- **Kindness** (χρηστότητα — active goodness): Kindness is not passive pleasantness. It is deliberate, practical goodness extended to others. Romans 2:4 says God's kindness leads to repentance — His kindness is active and purposeful. Mine should be too. This means looking for ways to do concrete good for the people around me, especially when it costs something.

- **Humility** (ταπεινοφροσύνην — lowliness of mind): In Colossae, the false teachers practiced a counterfeit humility — self-abasement as spiritual performance (2:18, 23). Paul reclaims the word. True humility is not self-deprecation but an accurate view of myself in relation to God and others. It means not needing to be recognized, not needing to be right, and not needing to control outcomes. Philippians 2:3 — "regard one another as more important than yourselves."

- **Gentleness** (πραΰτητα — controlled strength): Gentleness is not weakness. The Greek word describes a powerful horse under the rider's control. Jesus called Himself "gentle" (Matt 11:29). Gentleness means having the power to retaliate, to insist, to dominate — and choosing not to. It shows up in tone of voice, in how I respond to criticism, in how I treat people who can do nothing for me.

- **Patience** (μακροθυμίαν — being slow to anger with people): This is specifically relational patience — longsuffering with difficult people, not merely endurance of difficult circumstances. God Himself is patient (Rom 2:4; 2 Pet 3:9). If God is patient with me in my slow growth and repeated failures, I have no ground for impatience with others in theirs.

**Corporate:**

- A church that wears these five virtues will be markedly different from the surrounding culture. Compassion in a culture of indifference. Kindness in a culture of transactional relationships. Humility in a culture of self-promotion. Gentleness in a culture of outrage. Patience in a culture of instant gratification. These are not internal feelings to cultivate privately — they are visible, relational qualities that define how the church interacts.

### Verse 13 — Bear With and Forgive One Another

Paul is realistic: community involves friction. People will irritate, disappoint, and genuinely wrong each other. The word "complaint" (μομφήν) refers to a legitimate grievance, not a petty annoyance. Paul does not say "you won't have complaints" — he says "forgive them."

**Personal:**

- **Bearing with one another** (ἀνεχόμενοι) is the daily practice of tolerating the things about other believers that frustrate me — their quirks, their weaknesses, their different perspectives. It means choosing not to let irritation become resentment. Not every offense needs to be addressed; some need simply to be absorbed.

- **Forgiving each other** (χαριζόμενοι — literally "gracing" someone) goes beyond tolerance. It addresses real wrongs. The standard is staggering: "just as the Lord forgave you." Christ's forgiveness was complete, unearned, and costly. My forgiveness must mirror that. This means:
  - I cannot put conditions on forgiveness ("I'll forgive you if you...").
  - I cannot put an expiration date on forgiveness ("I forgave you, but...").
  - I cannot withhold forgiveness because the offense was too great — no offense against me is greater than my offense against God, which He freely forgave.

- Practically: Is there anyone I am holding a grudge against? Any relationship where I have withdrawn because of an unresolved complaint? The command is clear — forgive, as you have been forgiven.

**Corporate:**

- A church that does not practice forgiveness will fracture. Every congregation contains people who have been wronged by one another. The question is not whether offenses will happen but whether the community has the character to absorb and forgive them. Churches that cultivate a culture of grudge-holding, gossip, or silent withdrawal are disobeying this text directly.

### Verse 14 — Above All, Put On Love

Love is not one more virtue added to the list. It is placed "beyond all these things" (ἐπὶ πᾶσιν) — the outer garment that holds all the others together. Without love, compassion becomes patronizing, kindness becomes self-serving, humility becomes false modesty, patience becomes passive-aggression.

**Personal:**

- Love (ἀγάπη) is self-giving, other-oriented commitment that seeks the good of the other regardless of cost. It is not a feeling to be waited for but a decision to be made. The test: Am I seeking the good of this person, or am I seeking my own comfort, reputation, or convenience?

- Love is "the perfect bond of unity" (σύνδεσμος τῆς τελειότητος). In my relationships — at home, at church, at work — love is what prevents fragmentation. When I feel the pull to withdraw, to criticize, to give up on someone, love is the bond that holds.

**Corporate:**

- Love is the integrating center of the church. A congregation can have programs, theology, and gifted leaders, but without love it is incomplete. First Corinthians 13:1-3 makes this explicit: without love, everything else is nothing. Love is what makes a collection of individuals into a body.

### Verse 15 — Let the Peace of Christ Rule; Be Thankful

The word "rule" (βραβευέτω) means "act as umpire." When competing impulses, disagreements, or anxieties arise, the peace of Christ makes the final call.

**Personal:**

- When I face a decision and feel torn, the question is: which option leads toward the peace of Christ? When conflict arises in a relationship, am I letting Christ's peace arbitrate — or am I letting my pride, my need to be right, or my desire for control make the call?

- "Be thankful" (εὐχάριστοι γίνεσθε) — a present imperative: keep being thankful, continually. Thanksgiving is the settled posture of the new self. It is the opposite of the greed condemned in 3:5. Greed says "I need more." Thanksgiving says "God has given enough." Practically: I should cultivate a daily habit of recognizing and expressing gratitude — not as a self-help technique but as obedience to a command grounded in who God is and what He has done.

**Corporate:**

- "You were called in one body" — the peace of Christ is a corporate calling. When the church faces disagreement, the question is not "who wins?" but "what does the peace of Christ require?" This does not mean avoiding hard conversations or suppressing truth for the sake of false peace. It means letting Christ's peace — not personal agendas — govern how the community navigates conflict.

### Verse 16 — Let the Word of Christ Dwell Richly

The word of Christ is to dwell "richly" (πλουσίως) — abundantly, not thinly. And it dwells "within you" (ἐν ὑμῖν) — the pronoun is plural, meaning in the community, not just in individuals.

**Personal:**

- Am I saturated with the word of Christ, or is my engagement with Scripture thin and occasional? "Richly" implies depth, regularity, and abundance. This is not about checking a box ("I read my chapter today") but about allowing Christ's truth to permeate my thinking, my decisions, my conversations, my worldview. The word must go from page to mind to heart to life.

- "Teaching and admonishing one another" — this is mutual, not one-directional. I have a responsibility to build others up with truth (teaching) and to lovingly correct when necessary (admonishing). And I have a responsibility to receive the same from others. Am I in relationships where this kind of mutual instruction happens? Am I open to being admonished, or do I resist correction?

**Corporate:**

- A church where the word of Christ dwells richly is a church where Scripture permeates everything — not just the sermon but the conversations, the counseling, the decision-making, the worship. This verse also elevates singing from entertainment to edification: "psalms and hymns and spiritual songs" are vehicles for teaching and admonishing. Corporate worship should be rich in theological content, not thin on truth. The songs a church sings shape the theology the church holds.

- The Ephesians parallel (5:18) attributes this same dynamic to being "filled with the Spirit." A community filled with Christ's word is a Spirit-filled community. These are not competing emphases but complementary realities.

### Verse 17 — Do Everything in the Name of the Lord Jesus

This is the most comprehensive command in the passage: "whatever you do in word or deed." Nothing is excluded. There is no sacred/secular divide.

**Personal:**

- The test for every word and every action: Can I do this in the name of the Lord Jesus? Can I do this as His representative, under His authority, for His glory? This question applies to:
  - How I speak to my family
  - How I do my work
  - How I spend my money
  - How I use my time
  - What I consume (media, entertainment, information)
  - How I treat strangers
  - How I handle conflict

- "Giving thanks through Him to God the Father" — thanksgiving accompanies everything. This is the third mention of thanksgiving in three verses. Gratitude is not an occasional feeling but the constant atmosphere of the Christian life. Every good thing is received through Christ, and thanks is given through Christ to the Father.

**Corporate:**

- A church that does everything "in the name of the Lord Jesus" is a church where Christ's lordship governs not only the worship service but the business meetings, the budget decisions, the hiring practices, the conflict resolution, the outreach strategy. Every corporate act and word falls under the same standard: is this done in Jesus' name, under His authority, for His glory?

## Diagnostic Questions

These questions are derived directly from the passage and can be used for self-examination or small-group discussion.

### Identity
1. Do I live out of my identity as "chosen, holy, and beloved" — or do I try to earn God's approval through performance?
2. When I fail morally, do I return to my identity in Christ or spiral into shame and self-condemnation?

### Virtues (v. 12)
3. Which of the five virtues — compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, patience — is most absent from my life right now?
4. Am I deliberately putting on these virtues, or am I passively waiting to feel them?
5. Would the people closest to me describe me as compassionate? Kind? Humble? Gentle? Patient?

### Forgiveness (v. 13)
6. Is there anyone I am refusing to forgive or struggling to forgive?
7. Am I holding any grudges, even quietly?
8. When I forgive, do I forgive as the Lord forgave me — completely, freely, without conditions — or do I forgive partially, with strings attached?

### Love (v. 14)
9. Is love the integrating center of my relationships, or are other things driving me — duty, fear, approval, control?
10. Are there relationships where I have stopped actively seeking the other person's good?

### Peace (v. 15)
11. When I face conflict or decisions, am I letting the peace of Christ arbitrate — or am I letting anxiety, pride, or self-interest make the call?
12. Am I a peacemaker in my community, or a source of division?

### The Word of Christ (v. 16)
13. Is my engagement with Scripture rich and regular, or thin and sporadic?
14. Am I in relationships where mutual teaching and admonishing happens?
15. Am I open to being corrected by others through the word of Christ, or do I resist admonishment?

### Total-Life Lordship (v. 17)
16. Is there any area of my life — work, finances, entertainment, relationships — that I have exempted from Christ's lordship?
17. Can I honestly say that my words and deeds today were done "in the name of the Lord Jesus"?

### Thanksgiving (vv. 15-17)
18. Is gratitude the settled posture of my heart, or do I default to complaint, entitlement, or anxiety?
19. Do I regularly give thanks to God the Father through Christ — not just for big blessings, but as the constant atmosphere of my life?

## Summary

Colossians 3:12-17 calls for nothing less than a complete reorientation of life around Christ. The application moves in three concentric circles:

1. **Character** (vv. 12-14) — Clothe yourself with Christlike virtues, crowned by love. This is who you are to be.
2. **Community** (vv. 15-16) — Let Christ's peace and word govern the life of the church through mutual teaching, admonishing, and worship. This is how you are to live together.
3. **Comprehensive lordship** (v. 17) — Do everything in the name of the Lord Jesus, with thanksgiving. This is how you are to live in every domain.

The passage leaves no area of life untouched. There is no virtue that is optional, no relationship exempt from forgiveness, no corner of existence outside Christ's authority, and no moment that should not be marked by thanksgiving.
