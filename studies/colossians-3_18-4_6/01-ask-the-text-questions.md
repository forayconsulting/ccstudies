# Colossians 3:18-4:6 — Ask the Text Questions

---

## (1) What did this text mean to the human author?

### (1A) Who were they?

The human author is the Apostle Paul (Col 1:1), writing with Timothy. Paul was a Pharisee trained under Gamaliel (Acts 22:3), a former persecutor of the church converted on the road to Damascus (Acts 9), and a Roman citizen by birth (Acts 22:28). By the time he wrote Colossians, he was a seasoned apostle, missionary, and church planter with decades of ministry behind him.

Paul's dual formation — deeply rooted in Jewish Torah scholarship and fluent in the Greco-Roman world — is on full display in this passage. The "household code" (Haustafel) of 3:18-4:1 engages a literary form well known in Greco-Roman ethics (Aristotle, *Politics* 1.1253b; Seneca, *Ep.* 94.1; also found in Hellenistic Jewish authors like Philo and Josephus), but Paul radically transforms it by introducing a theological motivation absent from its pagan counterparts: the phrase "in the Lord" (or its equivalent) appears in nearly every instruction, reorienting every relationship around Christ's lordship.

Paul writes with apostolic authority, but also with pastoral sensitivity. He had never visited Colossae (Col 2:1), yet his instructions are remarkably specific and practical — addressing wives, husbands, children, fathers, slaves, and masters individually. This specificity reflects both the report he received from Epaphras (Col 1:7; 4:12-13) and his deep understanding of how the gospel transforms ordinary social structures from the inside out.

### (1B) When did they write this?

Paul wrote during his first Roman imprisonment, approximately AD 60-62 (Col 4:3, 10, 18). This places the letter under the early, relatively tolerant years of Nero's reign, before the persecutions of AD 64.

The social world of the first-century Roman Empire is essential context for the household code. Roman society was structured around the *paterfamilias* — the male head of household who held legal authority over wife, children, and slaves. The household (*oikos*) was considered the foundational unit of the state. Greco-Roman moralists routinely discussed the proper ordering of household relationships (husband/wife, father/children, master/slave) as a matter of civic duty and social stability.

Slavery was ubiquitous in the Roman Empire. Estimates suggest that slaves constituted 15-30% of the population in Italy. Slaves had no legal personhood; they were property. They could be bought, sold, beaten, and sexually exploited with legal impunity. A master's power over slaves was virtually unlimited.

Into this world Paul writes instructions that, while not abolishing Roman social structures outright, introduce a revolutionary principle: every relationship is now accountable to a higher Lord. Wives submit "in the Lord," children obey because "this is well-pleasing to the Lord," slaves serve "the Lord Christ," and masters answer to "a Master in heaven." This theological reframing would have been radical in its context.

### (1C) Where did they live?

Paul wrote from Rome under house arrest (Acts 28:16, 30-31), chained to a Roman guard but free to receive visitors and preach.

The recipients lived in Colossae, a small city in the Lycus Valley of Phrygia (modern western Turkey), near Laodicea and Hierapolis (Col 4:13). The population was ethnically mixed: native Phrygians, Greek settlers, and a significant Jewish community settled there by Antiochus III around 200 BC.

The Colossian church likely met in homes (cf. Philemon 2; Col 4:15) and would have been relatively small. The household code is addressed to people who live and worship together in close proximity. The relationships described — spouse to spouse, parent to child, master to slave — were not abstract categories but the daily reality of the congregation. Many of these households may have included both free and enslaved members who worshiped side by side on the Lord's Day and then returned to their socially stratified roles during the week.

The connection to Philemon is particularly striking. Onesimus — the runaway slave Paul sends back to Philemon — is identified in Col 4:9 as "one of your number." The instructions to slaves and masters in 3:22-4:1 would have landed with concrete force in a congregation where this very situation was unfolding.

### (1D) What were they addressing?

The immediate literary context places this passage within the practical section of Colossians ("The New Life in Christ," 3:1-4:6). Paul has established the theological foundation (Christ's supremacy and sufficiency, 1:15-2:23), commanded the inward transformation (seek things above, 3:1-4; put off the old self, 3:5-11; put on the new self, 3:12-17), and now addresses how this transformation works out in the specific relationships of daily life.

The passage addresses three concentric circles of relationship:

1. **Relational / Household** (3:18-4:1) — How the new life in Christ transforms the most intimate social structures: marriage, family, and the master-slave dynamic. Paul applies the comprehensive lordship of Christ (established in 3:17 — "whatever you do in word or deed, do all in the name of the Lord Jesus") to the concrete relationships within the household.

2. **External / Toward Outsiders** (4:2-6) — How the church relates to the world beyond its walls. This includes dependence on God in prayer (4:2-4) and wisdom in conduct and speech toward unbelievers (4:5-6).

This passage also continues Paul's implicit refutation of the Colossian heresy. The false teachers promoted ascetic rules and visionary experiences as the path to spiritual maturity (2:16-23). Paul's household code demonstrates that genuine Christian maturity does not withdraw from ordinary life into mystical asceticism — it transforms ordinary life from within. The most spiritual thing a Christian can do is love a spouse, raise children well, work honestly, and speak graciously to an unbeliever.

### (1E) What was in their Bible?

Paul had access to the entire Old Testament and likely many New Testament writings that preceded his prison epistles — his own earlier letters (Romans, 1-2 Corinthians, Galatians, 1-2 Thessalonians), likely the epistle of James, and possibly the Gospel of Mark and an early form of Luke's Gospel (Luke being present during this imprisonment — Col 4:14).

The Old Testament provides essential background:

- **Marriage** — Genesis 2:18-25 establishes marriage as a creation ordinance. The "one flesh" union grounds Paul's household code. The Old Testament portrays both the beauty and brokenness of marriage (cf. Gen 3:16, where the curse introduces relational distortion between husband and wife).

- **Children and parents** — The fifth commandment, "Honor your father and your mother" (Exod 20:12; Deut 5:16), is the direct background for 3:20. Paul's instruction to children echoes this commandment, while his warning to fathers (3:21) reflects the wisdom tradition's concern for wise and loving discipline (Prov 3:11-12; 19:18; 22:6; 23:13-14).

- **Slavery** — The Old Testament regulated slavery within Israel (Exod 21:1-11; Lev 25:39-55; Deut 15:12-18) and provided protections far beyond those of surrounding cultures. The Exodus narrative — God delivering an enslaved people — establishes God's concern for the oppressed. Deuteronomy 15:15 commands Israel to treat servants generously "because you were a slave in the land of Egypt, and the LORD your God redeemed you."

- **God's impartiality** — Paul's statement that God judges "without partiality" (3:25) echoes Deuteronomy 10:17 ("the LORD your God is the God of gods... who does not show partiality nor take a bribe") and 2 Chronicles 19:7 ("there is no injustice with the LORD our God, or partiality, or taking of bribes").

- **Prayer** — The Old Testament is saturated with prayer, from Abraham's intercessions (Gen 18:22-33) to Daniel's practice of devoted prayer (Dan 6:10). Paul's command to "devote yourselves to prayer" (4:2) stands in this tradition.

- **Wise speech** — Proverbs extensively addresses the power and responsibility of speech (Prov 10:19; 12:18; 15:1-2; 16:24; 25:11). Paul's instruction that speech be "with grace, as though seasoned with salt" (4:6) resonates with the wisdom tradition's emphasis on the fitting word.

## (2) What does this text teach me about God?

### (A) What is God saying or doing in this text?

Through Paul's apostolic instruction, God is showing how Christ's lordship transforms every human relationship — not by demolishing social structures, but by infusing them with a new authority, a new motivation, and a new accountability.

God is establishing that no relationship exists in a vacuum. Every interaction between husband and wife, parent and child, master and slave, believer and unbeliever operates under the lordship of Christ. God is not merely offering moral advice for better relationships — He is declaring that Christ is Lord over the household, the workplace, and the public square.

God is also calling His people to prayer — not as an afterthought but as their primary posture toward the world. Before Paul instructs wise conduct toward outsiders (4:5-6), he calls for devoted prayer (4:2-4). God's mission advances through the prayers of His people.

And God is opening a "door for the word" (4:3) — actively working to advance the gospel even through Paul's imprisonment. God's sovereignty over Paul's chains and the spread of the gospel is woven into the fabric of this passage.

### (B) Why is God saying or doing this?

1. **God is completing the picture of the new life.** Colossians 3:1-17 addressed internal transformation (mindset, vices, virtues, worship). Now God shows that this transformation is not merely interior — it radiates outward into every relationship. The new life in Christ is not a private spiritual experience but a comprehensive reordering of all human interactions under Christ's authority.

2. **God is countering the false teaching with embodied holiness.** The Colossian heresy offered withdrawal from the body and the material world as the path to spirituality (2:20-23). God counters by showing that true spirituality is lived out in the most ordinary, embodied, relational contexts: marriage, parenting, work, and conversation with neighbors. Asceticism retreats from life; the gospel transforms it.

3. **God is protecting the vulnerable.** In the ancient world, the household code tradition addressed only the *paterfamilias* — the one with all the power. Paul does something unprecedented: he addresses the subordinate parties first (wives, children, slaves) as moral agents with dignity before God. And he places reciprocal obligations on those with power (husbands: love; fathers: don't exasperate; masters: grant justice). God limits the exercise of authority and dignifies those under it.

4. **God is advancing His mission.** The prayer request (4:3-4) and the instructions for wise conduct toward outsiders (4:5-6) reveal that the household code is not merely about internal church order — it serves the mission. A household transformed by Christ becomes a testimony to the watching world. Gracious speech and wise conduct create opportunities for the gospel.

### (C) How is God saying or doing this?

God communicates through **special revelation** — the inspired writing of the Apostle Paul, carried by Tychicus and Onesimus (Col 4:7-9) to be read aloud to the congregation. The Holy Spirit superintended Paul's writing so that the words carry divine authority (2 Tim 3:16-17).

God works through **providential means**: Paul's imprisonment, Epaphras's report, the letter's composition and delivery, and even the social structures of the first-century Roman household all serve God's purposes. The household code itself is a remarkable example of God using an existing cultural form (the Greco-Roman Haustafel) and filling it with radically new theological content — Christ's lordship over every relationship.

God also works through the **prayers of His people** (4:2-4). Paul asks the Colossians to pray for an open door for the word, indicating that God's mission advances through the intercessory partnership of the church.

### (D) Which of God's attributes are on display?

- **Sovereignty** — God is Lord over every relationship and every social structure. The repeated phrase "the Lord" (ὁ κύριος) — appearing in nearly every verse of the household code — declares Christ's total dominion over marriage, family, work, and mission. Masters have "a Master in heaven" (4:1). God's sovereignty extends to Paul's imprisonment and the advance of the gospel through it (4:3).

- **Justice/Impartiality** — "He who does wrong will receive the consequences of the wrong which he has done, and that without partiality" (3:25). God does not play favorites based on social status. This is addressed directly to the slave-master dynamic, but the principle is universal: God judges everyone by the same standard. Masters too will answer to their heavenly Master (4:1).

- **Love** — Husbands are commanded to love their wives (3:19), reflecting God's own covenant love. The entire household code is an expression of the love commanded in 3:14 applied to specific relationships.

- **Wisdom** — Believers are to conduct themselves "with wisdom toward outsiders" (4:5). God is a God of wisdom, and His people are to reflect that wisdom in their public witness. The wisdom here is practical, relational, and missional — not the speculative "philosophy" of the false teachers (2:8).

- **Grace** — Speech is to be "with grace, as though seasoned with salt" (4:6). God is gracious, and His people's words are to carry that same quality. Grace here is not merely kindness but the attractive, flavorful quality that draws people toward the gospel.

- **Providence** — God is at work even in Paul's chains. The request for prayer that God would "open up to us a door for the word" (4:3) reveals a God who sovereignly controls the opportunities for gospel proclamation, even — perhaps especially — in circumstances of apparent defeat.

## (3) How should this text contribute to my theology?

### (A) How does this text advance the metanarrative of Scripture?

This passage advances the metanarrative at the point of **Restoration** — showing what redeemed life looks like in the real structures of a fallen world:

- **Creation** — God established marriage (Gen 2:18-25), the family (Gen 1:28), and human labor (Gen 2:15) before the fall. These are creation ordinances, not merely cultural conventions. Paul's household code does not invent new institutions but restores the ones God originally designed, now reoriented around Christ.

- **Fall** — Genesis 3:16-19 describes the curse's corruption of each of these relationships: marital conflict ("he will rule over you"), painful child-rearing, and toilsome labor. The vices Paul addressed in 3:5-11 (anger, wrath, malice, bitterness) are precisely the sins that poison households. The household code addresses the relational fallout of the fall.

- **Redemption** — Christ's lordship — established through His death, resurrection, and exaltation (Col 1:15-20; 2:13-15; 3:1-4) — is now applied to every human relationship. The repeated refrain "in the Lord" / "as for the Lord" / "the Lord Christ" grounds the household code in redemption. These are not moralistic instructions — they flow from union with the risen Christ.

- **Restoration** — The household code paints a picture of what the coming kingdom looks like in microcosm: marriages marked by love rather than bitterness, families marked by obedience rather than exasperation, workplaces marked by integrity and justice, and a community whose speech is gracious and whose prayer life is devoted. This is not yet the consummation, but it is a genuine anticipation of it.

### (B) How does this text point to Christ?

Christ is the controlling reality of this entire passage:

- **Christ as Lord of the household** — The phrase "in the Lord" (ἐν κυρίῳ) or its equivalent saturates the household code: submission is "fitting in the Lord" (3:18); obedience is "well-pleasing to the Lord" (3:20); slaves fear "the Lord" (3:22); work is done "as for the Lord" (3:23); "the Lord Christ" is the one served (3:24); masters have "a Master in heaven" (4:1). Christ's lordship is the gravitational center of every relationship.

- **Christ as the true Master** — The climactic declaration "It is the Lord Christ whom you serve" (3:24b) redefines all human authority. Every earthly master, employer, and authority figure is relativized by the reality that believers ultimately serve Christ. This transforms the experience of subordination: serving a human master becomes an act of devotion to the divine Master.

- **Christ as the content of Paul's message** — Paul requests prayer "that we may speak forth the mystery of Christ" (4:3). Christ is not merely the Lord of the household but the message the church carries to the world. The "mystery" is Christ Himself — His person, work, and the inclusion of Gentiles in His body (Col 1:27; 2:2-3).

- **Christ as the model for husbands** — While Colossians does not develop the Christ/church analogy as extensively as Ephesians 5:25-33, the command to husbands to "love your wives" (3:19) stands in the same theological tradition. Christ's self-giving love is the implicit standard.

### (D) Which areas of systematic theology relate?

- **Christology** — Christ's lordship is the organizing principle of the entire passage. He is Lord of the household (3:18-4:1), Lord of the mission (4:3), and the implicit model for sacrificial love in marriage. The title "the Lord Christ" (3:24) combines His divine sovereignty (κύριος) with His messianic identity (Χριστός).

- **Ecclesiology** — The household code is addressed to the church. The relationships described are not merely private family matters but aspects of the church's corporate life and public witness. The connection between household order and gospel mission (4:5-6; cf. Titus 2:10) reveals that the church's internal life is inseparable from its external testimony.

- **Anthropology** — Paul addresses every social category in the household: wives and husbands, children and parents, slaves and masters. Every person — regardless of social status — is treated as a moral agent responsible before God. The subordinate parties (wives, children, slaves) are addressed first and directly, which was extraordinary in the ancient world and reflects the dignity of every human being before God.

- **Soteriology** — The household code is grounded in the believer's union with Christ. The motivation for obedience is not social convention but relationship to "the Lord." The promise of "the reward of the inheritance" (3:24) to slaves — who had no earthly inheritance — reveals that salvation transcends and ultimately overturns social inequity.

- **Ethics** — This passage is one of the primary New Testament texts for Christian ethics in the household, workplace, and public square. The ethical framework is not autonomous human reason or cultural convention but Christ's lordship. The principle is not "submit to all authority without question" but "conduct yourself in every relationship as one who serves the Lord Christ."

- **Eschatology** — The promise of future reward (3:24) and future judgment (3:25) introduces an eschatological horizon. Present obedience is motivated by the coming evaluation when "the Lord Christ" will render just recompense. Justice that is not fully realized in this age will be realized at Christ's return.

## (4) How does this truth apply to me?

### (A) What is my relationship to the human author?

Paul is an apostle of Jesus Christ, uniquely commissioned by the risen Lord. I am not an apostle and do not hold apostolic authority. However, as a believer in Christ, Paul's inspired letters carry the authority of God's Word and are binding on me in every era (2 Tim 3:16-17). Paul's household code is not first-century cultural advice to be accepted or discarded at will — it is apostolic instruction grounded in the lordship of Christ. At the same time, the specific social institution of Roman slavery no longer exists, which requires careful thought about how those instructions apply in a modern context.

### (B) What is my relationship to the original audience?

Like the Colossians, I am a believer in Christ living in a world with its own social structures, authority relationships, and cultural assumptions. Like them, I occupy multiple roles simultaneously — I may be a spouse, a parent, an employee, and a neighbor to unbelievers. Like them, I need to understand that every one of these relationships operates under Christ's lordship. The household code speaks to me not in identical social circumstances but in the same theological reality: Christ is Lord of all my relationships.

### (C) What are the differences between my world and their world?

- **Slavery** — The most significant difference. The institution of Roman slavery — in which a human being was owned as property with virtually no legal rights — no longer exists in modern Western society. This does not mean the slave-master instructions are irrelevant. The theological principles (work as for the Lord; God judges impartially; those in authority are accountable to a higher Master) apply to modern employer-employee, authority-subordinate, and economic relationships. But the direct social parallel is gone, and the abolitionist trajectory embedded in Paul's theology (Gal 3:28; Phlm 16; Col 3:11) has been realized in ways the first-century church could only anticipate.

- **Household structure** — The first-century *paterfamilias* held legal authority over wife, children, and slaves in ways that have no modern legal parallel. Modern marriage law is based on the equality of spouses before the law. Modern parenting occurs in a context of child welfare protections. The power dynamics have shifted, but the theological principles — sacrificial love, mutual respect, Christ-centered authority — remain directly applicable.

- **Public witness** — The Colossians were a tiny minority in a pagan culture. Modern Western Christians may live in contexts ranging from cultural Christianity (where Christian language is familiar but often hollow) to post-Christian secularism (where Christian claims are increasingly foreign or offensive). Paul's instruction to conduct ourselves "with wisdom toward outsiders" (4:5) and to speak "with grace, as though seasoned with salt" (4:6) applies in both settings but takes different shape depending on the cultural context.

- **Communication** — Paul asked for prayer that he could speak the gospel clearly while in prison. Modern believers have communication tools (writing, digital media, social platforms) that amplify both the opportunity and the responsibility. The principle — clarity, grace, intentionality in gospel communication — is unchanged; the means are vastly expanded.

### (D) Is my relationship to God different than theirs?

No. As a New Testament believer, my relationship to God is the same as the Colossians'. I serve the same Lord Christ (3:24). I am accountable to the same impartial Judge (3:25). I have the same Master in heaven (4:1). I am called to the same devoted prayer life (4:2), the same wise conduct toward outsiders (4:5), and the same gracious speech (4:6). The theological motivation for every instruction in this passage — Christ's lordship, God's justice, the hope of future reward — applies to me directly and without qualification.

### (E) Which of our modern sensibilities are at odds with this text? Which of them resonate with it? And why?

**At odds:**

- **The concept of submission.** Modern Western culture prizes individual autonomy and views hierarchical relationships with deep suspicion. The instruction for wives to "be subject to your husbands" (3:18) and for children to "obey your parents in everything" (3:20) cuts against the grain of a culture that defines freedom as freedom from authority. However, Paul's framework is not authoritarian: submission is "in the Lord" (not absolute), and authority is bounded by reciprocal obligations (love, not bitterness; care, not exasperation).

- **The slave-master instructions.** Modern readers rightly recoil from any text that appears to condone slavery. The critical exegetical question is whether Paul is endorsing the institution of slavery or addressing believers within an institution he cannot immediately dismantle. The evidence points to the latter: Paul undermines slavery's ideological foundation by declaring God's impartiality (3:25), commanding masters to grant justice (4:1), calling slaves to serve Christ rather than men (3:23-24), and — in Philemon — urging a master to receive his slave "as a beloved brother." The New Testament does not provide a political program for abolition, but it plants the theological seeds that made abolition inevitable.

- **The gendered language.** Paul addresses wives and husbands with different instructions (submit/love), which is at odds with modern egalitarian sensibilities. The exegetical question is what Paul means by "be subject to" (ὑποτάσσεσθε) and whether this reflects a creation-order principle or a culturally conditioned application. Faithful readers disagree on this point, but the LHG hermeneutic requires us to understand what Paul meant in his context before evaluating how it applies in ours. What is clear is that Paul's instruction to husbands — to love and not be bitter — was itself a radical limitation on the *paterfamilias*'s unchecked authority.

**Resonates:**

- **The dignity of every person.** Paul addresses wives, children, and slaves as morally responsible agents who can hear and obey God's Word. This was countercultural in the first century and resonates deeply with modern convictions about human dignity, even if those convictions have different philosophical roots.

- **Accountability for those in power.** The instructions to husbands, fathers, and masters — love, don't exasperate, grant justice — resonate strongly with modern concerns about the abuse of authority. Paul insists that authority is not a license for self-serving domination but a responsibility exercised under God's watchful eye.

- **Gracious public engagement.** In an era of outrage culture, tribal polarization, and scorched-earth rhetoric, Paul's call for speech that is "with grace, as though seasoned with salt" (4:6) and conduct marked by "wisdom toward outsiders" (4:5) speaks directly to the moment. The church's public witness is not advanced by combativeness or capitulation but by Christlike wisdom and grace.

- **The integration of faith and daily life.** The passage refuses to separate "spiritual" life from "ordinary" life. Work, marriage, parenting, and neighborly conversation are all sacred — all done "as for the Lord" (3:23). This resonates with the growing modern awareness that faith must be embodied and practical, not merely cognitive or ceremonial.
