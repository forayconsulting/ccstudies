# Colossians 3:18-4:6 — Exegetical Resources and Notes

This document records observations, questions, and findings from the use of exegetical tools, following the methodology in Chapter 7 of *Hermeneutics and Exegesis for Laypeople*.

---

## Key Greek Words and Phrases

### The Household Code (3:18-4:1)

#### Verse 18

- **Αἱ γυναῖκες, ὑποτάσσεσθε** (*hai gynaikes, hypotassesthe*) — "Wives, be subject to / submit to." Present middle/passive imperative of ὑποτάσσω. The word literally means "to arrange under" or "to place oneself under" (ὑπό + τάσσω). In the middle voice (as here), it carries the sense of voluntary self-ordering — not forced subjugation but willing alignment within a structure. The same verb is used of Christ's submission to the Father (1 Cor 15:28), the church's relationship to Christ (Eph 5:24), and the general call to mutual submission (Eph 5:21). The present tense indicates an ongoing disposition, not a one-time act.

  Important: ὑποτάσσω is not synonymous with ὑπακούω ("obey"), which is the verb used for children (3:20) and slaves (3:22). Paul deliberately chooses a different word for wives — one that implies ordered relationship rather than command-obedience. This distinction is exegetically significant and should not be flattened.

- **ὡς ἀνῆκεν ἐν κυρίῳ** (*hōs anēken en kyriō*) — "As is fitting in the Lord." ἀνῆκεν is the imperfect of ἀνήκω ("to be fitting, proper, appropriate"). The imperfect tense is unusual and likely carries the sense "as has been fitting" or "as is [continually] fitting" — this is an established standard, not a new invention. The prepositional phrase ἐν κυρίῳ ("in the Lord") qualifies the submission: it is fitting specifically within the sphere of Christ's lordship. Submission that contradicts Christ's lordship would, by definition, not be "fitting in the Lord."

#### Verse 19

- **Οἱ ἄνδρες, ἀγαπᾶτε τὰς γυναῖκας** (*hoi andres, agapate tas gynaikas*) — "Husbands, love your wives." Present active imperative of ἀγαπάω — self-giving, other-oriented love. The present tense indicates habitual, ongoing action. While Colossians does not develop the Christ/church analogy as Ephesians 5:25 does ("just as Christ also loved the church and gave Himself up for her"), the verb ἀγαπάω carries that connotation by virtue of its NT usage. In the context of a culture where the husband held virtually all legal and social power, the command to *love* (not merely to provide for or manage) was itself a radical limitation on patriarchal authority.

- **μὴ πικραίνεσθε πρὸς αὐτάς** (*mē pikrainesthe pros autas*) — "Do not be embittered against them." Present passive imperative (with negative μή) of πικραίνω — "to make bitter, to embitter." The same root appears in Ephesians 4:31 (πικρία, "bitterness") among the vices to be put away, and in Hebrews 12:15 ("root of bitterness"). The passive voice may suggest "do not let yourself become bitter" — bitterness is something that happens to a person over time through resentment, unresolved conflict, or the corrosion of love. Paul identifies the specific relational danger for husbands: not a dramatic failure of love, but the slow accumulation of bitterness that poisons a marriage from within.

#### Verse 20

- **Τὰ τέκνα, ὑπακούετε τοῖς γονεῦσιν κατὰ πάντα** (*ta tekna, hypakouete tois goneusin kata panta*) — "Children, obey your parents in everything." Present active imperative of ὑπακούω — "to listen under, to obey." This is a stronger word than ὑποτάσσω (used for wives): it denotes compliance with specific commands. The phrase κατὰ πάντα ("in everything") is comprehensive, though it must be read in light of the qualifier that follows: this obedience is grounded in what is "well-pleasing to the Lord." Obedience that contradicts the Lord's will would not be "well-pleasing" to Him.

  Note: τέκνα ("children") likely refers to children still in the household — young enough to be under parental authority — rather than adult offspring.

- **τοῦτο γὰρ εὐάρεστόν ἐστιν ἐν κυρίῳ** (*touto gar euareston estin en kyriō*) — "For this is well-pleasing in the Lord." εὐάρεστος means "well-pleasing, acceptable." The motivation for obedience is not the parent's authority per se but the Lord's approval. This reframes the child's obedience theologically: by obeying parents, the child is pleasing Christ. The parallel in Ephesians 6:1-3 adds a further ground: "for this is right" (δίκαιος) and quotes the fifth commandment with its promise.

#### Verse 21

- **Οἱ πατέρες, μὴ ἐρεθίζετε τὰ τέκνα ὑμῶν** (*hoi pateres, mē erethizete ta tekna hymōn*) — "Fathers, do not exasperate/provoke your children." Present active imperative (negative) of ἐρεθίζω — "to stir up, provoke, irritate." The word can mean to provoke either positively (to stimulate, 2 Cor 9:2) or negatively (to irritate, embitter). Here, in context with the negative μή, it means to provoke to resentment. The parallel in Ephesians 6:4 uses παροργίζω ("do not provoke to anger") and adds the positive counterpart: "bring them up in the discipline and instruction of the Lord."

  Paul addresses "fathers" (πατέρες) specifically, not "parents" (γονεῖς). In the Roman household, the *paterfamilias* held legal power of life and death (*patria potestas*) over his children. Paul's instruction targets the one with the power to abuse it.

- **ἵνα μὴ ἀθυμῶσιν** (*hina mē athymōsin*) — "So that they will not lose heart / become discouraged." ἀθυμέω (only here in the NT) means "to be disheartened, to become spiritless, to lose motivation." The alpha-privative (ἀ-) negates θυμός ("spirit, passion, courage"). A father who relentlessly provokes, criticizes, or demands without encouragement can crush a child's spirit. Paul's concern is pastoral: harsh parenting produces not holiness but despair.

#### Verse 22

- **Οἱ δοῦλοι, ὑπακούετε κατὰ πάντα τοῖς κατὰ σάρκα κυρίοις** (*hoi douloi, hypakouete kata panta tois kata sarka kyriois*) — "Slaves, in all things obey those who are your masters according to the flesh." δοῦλος refers to a bondservant or slave — a person owned by another as property. The phrase κατὰ σάρκα ("according to the flesh") is a critical qualifier. It distinguishes the earthly master (κύριος κατὰ σάρκα) from the heavenly Lord (ὁ κύριος, vv. 23-24). The master's authority is real but limited to the fleshly, temporal sphere. The slave has a higher Lord.

- **μὴ ἐν ὀφθαλμοδουλίᾳ ὡς ἀνθρωπάρεσκοι** (*mē en ophthalmodouli̱a hōs anthrōpareskoi*) — "Not with external service, as those who merely please men." Two compound words unique to Paul in the NT:
  - **ὀφθαλμοδουλία** ("eye-service") — ὀφθαλμός ("eye") + δουλεία ("service"). Work done only when the master is watching. Surface compliance.
  - **ἀνθρωπάρεσκοι** ("man-pleasers") — ἄνθρωπος ("man") + ἀρέσκω ("to please"). Those whose ultimate aim is human approval rather than divine approval.

  These coined terms create a vivid picture: the kind of work that looks busy when the boss walks by but slacks off the moment he leaves.

- **ἀλλ' ἐν ἁπλότητι καρδίας, φοβούμενοι τὸν κύριον** (*all' en haplotēti kardias, phoboumenoi ton kyrion*) — "But with sincerity of heart, fearing the Lord." ἁπλότης means "singleness, simplicity, sincerity" — an undivided heart, the opposite of the "eye-service" hypocrisy. The participle φοβούμενοι ("fearing") indicates the controlling motivation: reverence for the Lord, not fear of the master. The fear of the Lord transforms the slave's work from grudging compliance into genuine devotion.

#### Verses 23-24a

- **ὃ ἐὰν ποιῆτε, ἐκ ψυχῆς ἐργάζεσθε** (*ho ean poiēte, ek psychēs ergazesthe*) — "Whatever you do, do your work heartily." The phrase ἐκ ψυχῆς literally means "from the soul" — wholeheartedly, with your entire being. This echoes the Shema's call to love God "with all your soul" (Deut 6:5, בְּכָל־נַפְשְׁךָ). The slave's labor, however menial, becomes an act of whole-souled devotion when directed toward the Lord.

  The opening ὃ ἐὰν ποιῆτε ("whatever you do") directly echoes 3:17 (πᾶν ὅ τι ἐὰν ποιῆτε — "whatever you do"). The comprehensive principle of 3:17 is now being applied to the slave's specific situation.

- **ὡς τῷ κυρίῳ καὶ οὐκ ἀνθρώποις** (*hōs tō kyriō kai ouk anthrōpois*) — "As for the Lord rather than for men." This comparative-adversative construction makes the reorientation explicit: the slave's work is directed toward the Lord, not toward the human master. This does not negate the master's authority (the command to obey remains), but it fundamentally transforms the motivation and dignity of the work.

- **τὴν ἀνταπόδοσιν τῆς κληρονομίας** (*tēn antapodosin tēs klēronomias*) — "The reward of the inheritance." ἀνταπόδοσις means "recompense, repayment" — a reward that corresponds to the service rendered. κληρονομία ("inheritance") is theologically loaded: in Roman law, slaves could not inherit. They were property, not heirs. Paul declares that the slave who serves the Lord Christ will receive *the* inheritance — the eschatological reward reserved for God's children (cf. Rom 8:17; Gal 4:7; Eph 1:14, 18). This is a stunning reversal: the one with no earthly inheritance receives the ultimate heavenly inheritance.

#### Verse 24b

- **τῷ κυρίῳ Χριστῷ δουλεύετε** (*tō kyriō Christō douleuete*) — "It is the Lord Christ whom you serve." This can be read as an indicative declaration ("you are serving the Lord Christ") or as an imperative ("serve the Lord Christ!"). Either way, it is the theological center of the slave instructions.

  The title **κύριος Χριστός** ("Lord Christ") is unique — appearing only here in Paul's letters. It combines the divine title κύριος ("Lord," used throughout this passage for Christ's sovereign authority) with Χριστός ("Christ / Messiah," the anointed deliverer). The juxtaposition is deliberate: the slave serves not merely a heavenly Lord in the abstract, but the Messiah — the one who has come to redeem and who will come to judge and reward.

#### Verse 25

- **ὁ γὰρ ἀδικῶν κομίσεται ὃ ἠδίκησεν** (*ho gar adikōn komisetai ho ēdikēsen*) — "For he who does wrong will receive the consequences of the wrong which he has done." ἀδικέω ("to do wrong, to act unjustly") is used twice — once as a participle ("the one doing wrong") and once as a finite verb ("what he has done wrong"). The future κομίσεται ("will receive back") indicates eschatological judgment: God will repay injustice. This cuts both ways — it warns the wrongdoing slave and the wrongdoing master. God's justice is comprehensive.

- **οὐκ ἔστιν προσωπολημψία** (*ouk estin prosōpolēmpsia*) — "There is no partiality." Literally "face-receiving" — showing favoritism based on outward appearance or social status. This is a direct echo of Deuteronomy 10:17 and 2 Chronicles 19:7. God does not adjust His justice based on whether someone is slave or free, powerful or weak. In a world built on patronage, status, and preferential treatment, this is a radical declaration.

  The ambiguity of who "the one who does wrong" refers to — slave or master — is likely intentional. In context, it may primarily warn slaves against using their freedom in Christ as a license for poor work (cf. 1 Tim 6:2). But the principle of impartial justice applies equally to masters, as 4:1 makes clear.

#### Verse 4:1

- **Οἱ κύριοι, τὸ δίκαιον καὶ τὴν ἰσότητα τοῖς δούλοις παρέχεσθε** (*hoi kyrioi, to dikaion kai tēn isotēta tois doulois parechesthe*) — "Masters, grant to your slaves justice and fairness." Two key terms:
  - **τὸ δίκαιον** ("what is just/right") — The master is bound by a standard of justice. This was not a Roman legal obligation toward slaves but a divine one.
  - **τὴν ἰσότητα** ("equity, fairness") — From ἴσος ("equal"). This is a remarkable word to use in the master-slave relationship. It does not mean Paul is demanding social equality, but that the master must treat the slave with equitable consideration — a standard of fairness that recognizes the slave's humanity before God.

- **εἰδότες ὅτι καὶ ὑμεῖς ἔχετε κύριον ἐν οὐρανῷ** (*eidotes hoti kai hymeis echete kyrion en ouranō*) — "Knowing that you too have a Master in heaven." The word κύριος is used for both the earthly master and the heavenly Master — a deliberate ambiguity. The master who exercises authority over slaves must remember that he is himself under authority. The heavenly Master will hold the earthly master accountable for how he has treated those in his charge.

### External Conduct (4:2-6)

#### Verse 2

- **Τῇ προσευχῇ προσκαρτερεῖτε** (*tē proseuchē proskartereite*) — "Devote yourselves to prayer." Present active imperative of προσκαρτερέω — "to persist in, to be devoted to, to adhere firmly to." The word implies not casual prayer but tenacious, consistent commitment. The same verb describes the early church's devotion in Acts 1:14 ("devoting themselves to prayer") and Acts 2:42 ("continually devoting themselves to... prayer").

- **γρηγοροῦντες ἐν αὐτῇ** (*grēgorountes en autē*) — "Keeping alert in it." Present active participle of γρηγορέω — "to be watchful, to stay awake, to be alert." Jesus used this word in Gethsemane: "Keep watching and praying" (Matt 26:41). The call to alertness in prayer implies spiritual vigilance — praying with awareness, not rote repetition. It may also carry eschatological overtones: watchfulness for Christ's return (cf. 1 Thess 5:6; 1 Pet 5:8).

- **ἐν εὐχαριστίᾳ** (*en eucharistia*) — "With thanksgiving." This is the fourth reference to thanksgiving in the immediate context (after 3:15, 16, 17). Thanksgiving is the atmosphere in which prayer occurs. The connection between prayer and thanksgiving is consistent throughout Paul (Phil 4:6 — "with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God").

#### Verses 3-4

- **θύραν τοῦ λόγου** (*thyran tou logou*) — "A door for the word." The "door" metaphor for gospel opportunity appears also in 1 Corinthians 16:9 ("a wide door for effective service has opened to me") and 2 Corinthians 2:12 ("a door was opened for me in the Lord"). Paul does not ask for release from prison but for an open door for proclamation. The priority is the advance of the message, not the comfort of the messenger.

- **τὸ μυστήριον τοῦ Χριστοῦ** (*to mystērion tou Christou*) — "The mystery of Christ." μυστήριον in Paul is not a puzzle to be solved but a truth previously hidden and now revealed (Col 1:26-27; 2:2-3). The "mystery of Christ" is the gospel itself — particularly the inclusion of Gentiles in Christ's body (Col 1:27 — "Christ in you, the hope of glory") and the fullness of God's redemptive plan in Christ (Eph 3:3-6).

- **δι' ὃ καὶ δέδεμαι** (*di' ho kai dedemai*) — "For which I have also been imprisoned." δέω means "to bind" — Paul is literally in chains (Col 4:18). The perfect tense (δέδεμαι) indicates a present state resulting from a past action: Paul was bound and remains bound. His imprisonment is directly caused by (δι' ὅ, "on account of which") his proclamation of the mystery. The chains are evidence of faithfulness, not failure.

- **ἵνα φανερώσω αὐτὸ ὡς δεῖ με λαλῆσαι** (*hina phanerōsō auto hōs dei me lalēsai*) — "That I may make it clear in the way I ought to speak." φανερόω means "to make visible, to reveal, to manifest." The gospel is not merely to be spoken but made *clear* — visible, transparent, understandable. The phrase ὡς δεῖ ("as it is necessary / as I ought") introduces a note of obligation: there is a *right* way to communicate the mystery, and Paul seeks to meet that standard. Clarity is not optional.

#### Verse 5

- **Ἐν σοφίᾳ περιπατεῖτε πρὸς τοὺς ἔξω** (*en sophia peripateite pros tous exō*) — "Conduct yourselves with wisdom toward outsiders." περιπατέω ("to walk") is Paul's standard metaphor for the conduct of life (cf. Col 1:10; 2:6; Eph 4:1; 5:2, 8, 15). "Outsiders" (τοὺς ἔξω) is a technical term for those outside the Christian community (1 Cor 5:12-13; 1 Thess 4:12; Mark 4:11). The instruction to walk "in wisdom" (ἐν σοφίᾳ) echoes the σοφία theme throughout Colossians: true wisdom is found in Christ (2:3), not in the false teachers' philosophy (2:8, 23). Wisdom toward outsiders means engaging the world with Christlike discernment, not with the speculative "wisdom" the heresy offered.

- **τὸν καιρὸν ἐξαγοραζόμενοι** (*ton kairon exagorazomenoi*) — "Making the most of the opportunity / redeeming the time." ἐξαγοράζω means "to buy up, to purchase from the marketplace" — hence "to buy up for oneself, to make full use of." καιρός is not χρόνος (chronological time) but *opportune* time — the decisive moment, the window of opportunity. The participle suggests urgency: opportunities for witness are limited and must be seized. The parallel in Ephesians 5:16 adds "because the days are evil."

#### Verse 6

- **Ὁ λόγος ὑμῶν πάντοτε ἐν χάριτι** (*ho logos hymōn pantote en chariti*) — "Let your speech always be with grace." λόγος here refers to ordinary speech — conversation, responses, daily verbal interaction. The qualifier πάντοτε ("always") makes this comprehensive: there is no context in which graceless speech is appropriate. χάρις ("grace") here carries the sense of attractiveness, charm, and kindness — speech that is winsome, pleasant, and reflective of God's own grace. Cf. Luke 4:22, where the crowd marveled at "the gracious words" (λόγοι τῆς χάριτος) proceeding from Jesus' mouth.

- **ἅλατι ἠρτυμένος** (*halati ērtymenos*) — "Seasoned with salt." ἀρτύω means "to season, to prepare, to make palatable." Salt in the ancient world served multiple functions: preservation, flavoring, and purification. The metaphor suggests speech that is flavorful and preserving — not bland, not corrupt, but savory and wholesome. Cf. Mark 9:50 ("Have salt in yourselves, and be at peace with one another") and Matthew 5:13 ("You are the salt of the earth"). The combination of "grace" and "salt" suggests speech that is both kind and substantive — gracious but not insipid.

- **εἰδέναι πῶς δεῖ ὑμᾶς ἑνὶ ἑκάστῳ ἀποκρίνεσθαι** (*eidenai pōs dei hymas heni hekastō apokrinesthai*) — "So that you will know how you should respond to each person." The phrase ἑνὶ ἑκάστῳ ("to each one") is significant: Paul does not envision a one-size-fits-all script for evangelistic conversations. Gracious, salt-seasoned speech is tailored to the individual. This requires discernment, attentiveness, and genuine relationship. The verb ἀποκρίνεσθαι ("to answer, to respond") implies dialogue, not monologue — believers are responding to questions, challenges, and conversations initiated by outsiders. Cf. 1 Peter 3:15 ("always being ready to make a defense to everyone who asks you to give an account for the hope that is in you, yet with gentleness and reverence").

## Questions Arising from Study

1. **How does Paul's household code relate to the Greco-Roman Haustafel tradition?** Ancient moralists (Aristotle, *Politics* 1.1253b; Seneca; Philo; Josephus) addressed the proper ordering of household relationships as a standard topic. Paul adopts the form but transforms the content by introducing Christ's lordship as the governing principle. The key difference: Greco-Roman household codes addressed only the *paterfamilias* and were concerned with maintaining social order and male authority. Paul addresses both parties in each pair, limits the power of the authority figure, and grounds every instruction in relationship to Christ. The form is borrowed; the theology is revolutionary.

2. **To whom does 3:25 ("he who does wrong") refer — the slave or the master?** The verse is positioned within the slave instructions (3:22-25), which might suggest it refers to the slave. But the principle of "no partiality" applies universally and transitions naturally to the instruction to masters (4:1). The ambiguity is likely intentional: God's impartial justice applies to everyone in the relationship. Wrongdoing by either party will be recompensed.

3. **What is the relationship between the household code and the Colossian heresy?** The false teachers promoted ascetic withdrawal from the material world as the path to spirituality (2:20-23). Paul's response is not more asceticism but the transformation of ordinary life. The most spiritual thing a Christian can do is not retreat into mystical experiences but love a spouse, raise children wisely, work honestly, and speak graciously. The household code is itself an anti-ascetic statement: holiness is embodied in real relationships, not escaped through ascetic performance.

4. **Why does Paul address slaves at such greater length than wives or children?** Several factors likely converge: (a) the pastoral situation — Onesimus is returning to Colossae (4:9), making the slave-master relationship a live issue; (b) the theological richness — the slave's situation provides the starkest illustration of Christ's lordship transforming the most oppressive human circumstance; (c) the vulnerability of the audience — slaves needed the most encouragement, the clearest theological framework, and the strongest assurance of divine justice and reward.

5. **What does "making the most of the opportunity" (4:5) look like in practice?** The verb ἐξαγοράζω ("to buy up") suggests seizing moments that are limited in supply. In the Colossian context, this likely meant recognizing and responding to openings in conversation, social interaction, and daily life where the gospel could be shared or demonstrated. The instruction is paired with "wisdom" — discernment about when to speak, what to say, and how to engage effectively.

6. **How do prayer (4:2-4) and wise conduct (4:5-6) work together?** They represent the vertical and horizontal dimensions of mission. Prayer acknowledges dependence on God for gospel opportunity (God opens doors); wise conduct and speech are the means by which believers walk through those doors. The order is significant: prayer comes first. The church's mission begins on its knees.

## Parallel Passage Comparison: Colossians 3:18-4:1 and Ephesians 5:21-6:9

| Theme | Colossians | Ephesians |
|---|---|---|
| Introductory framing | 3:17: "Whatever you do... in the name of the Lord Jesus" | 5:21: "Be subject to one another in the fear of Christ" |
| Wives | "Be subject to your husbands, as is fitting in the Lord" (3:18) | "Wives, be subject to your own husbands, as to the Lord. For the husband is the head of the wife, as Christ also is the head of the church" (5:22-24) |
| Husbands | "Love your wives and do not be embittered against them" (3:19) | "Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ also loved the church and gave Himself up for her... So husbands ought also to love their own wives as their own bodies" (5:25-33) |
| Children | "Obey your parents in everything, for this is well-pleasing to the Lord" (3:20) | "Children, obey your parents in the Lord, for this is right. 'Honor your father and mother'..." (6:1-3) |
| Fathers | "Do not exasperate your children, so that they will not lose heart" (3:21) | "Do not provoke your children to anger, but bring them up in the discipline and instruction of the Lord" (6:4) |
| Slaves | "Obey... not with external service... but with sincerity of heart, fearing the Lord. Whatever you do, do your work heartily, as for the Lord" (3:22-24) | "Obey... not by way of eyeservice, as men-pleasers, but as slaves of Christ, doing the will of God from the heart. With good will render service, as to the Lord" (6:5-8) |
| Masters | "Grant justice and fairness, knowing that you too have a Master in heaven" (4:1) | "Do the same things to them, and give up threatening, knowing that both their Master and yours is in heaven, and there is no partiality with Him" (6:9) |
| Impartiality | "Without partiality" (3:25 — within slave section) | "No partiality with Him" (6:9 — within master section) |

**Key differences:**

1. **Ephesians is significantly longer.** The husband-wife section in Ephesians (5:22-33) is 12 verses compared to Colossians' 2 verses. Ephesians develops the Christ/church analogy at length; Colossians omits it entirely. This suggests that the two letters serve different purposes: Colossians is more concise and focused on the Christological principle ("in the Lord"); Ephesians expands the theological foundation of the household code.

2. **Ephesians begins with mutual submission.** Ephesians 5:21 ("be subject to one another in the fear of Christ") provides a mutual-submission framework before the household code begins. Colossians has no equivalent verse, though the reciprocal structure of the code implies mutual accountability.

3. **Ephesians adds positive instruction for fathers.** Colossians only prohibits ("do not exasperate"); Ephesians adds "bring them up in the discipline and instruction of the Lord" (6:4). Colossians gives the reason not to provoke (lest children lose heart); Ephesians gives the alternative approach (nurture and instruction in the Lord).

4. **The impartiality statement appears in different locations.** In Colossians, "without partiality" appears in the slave section (3:25); in Ephesians, "no partiality" appears in the master section (6:9). The principle is the same; the placement shifts the emphasis.

## Parallel: Colossians 4:2-6 and Ephesians 6:18-20

| Theme | Colossians | Ephesians |
|---|---|---|
| Prayer command | "Devote yourselves to prayer, keeping alert in it with thanksgiving" (4:2) | "With all prayer and petition pray at all times in the Spirit, and with this in view, be on the alert with all perseverance and petition for all the saints" (6:18) |
| Prayer for Paul | "Pray for us, that God will open a door for the word, to speak forth the mystery of Christ" (4:3) | "Pray on my behalf, that utterance may be given to me in the opening of my mouth, to make known with boldness the mystery of the gospel" (6:19) |
| Paul's imprisonment | "For which I have also been imprisoned" (4:3) | "For which I am an ambassador in chains" (6:20) |
| Clarity/boldness | "That I may make it clear in the way I ought to speak" (4:4) | "That in proclaiming it I may speak boldly, as I ought to speak" (6:20) |

The requests are nearly identical. Colossians emphasizes *clarity*; Ephesians emphasizes *boldness*. Both use the phrase ὡς δεῖ με/ἐμὲ λαλῆσαι ("as I ought to speak"). Paul's need for prayer is consistent: he asks not for comfort or release but for effective gospel proclamation.

## The Philemon Connection

The slave-master instructions of Colossians 3:22-4:1 must be read alongside the letter to Philemon, written and delivered at the same time by the same carrier (Tychicus, accompanied by Onesimus — Col 4:7-9; Phlm 10-12).

| Colossians | Philemon |
|---|---|
| Slaves, obey... with sincerity of heart (3:22) | Onesimus is returning to Philemon's household |
| It is the Lord Christ whom you serve (3:24b) | Onesimus is now "useful" (ὄνησιμος = "useful") to both Paul and Philemon "in the Lord" (Phlm 11) |
| God judges without partiality (3:25) | Philemon is to receive Onesimus "no longer as a slave, but... a beloved brother" (Phlm 16) |
| Masters, grant justice and fairness (4:1) | Paul appeals to Philemon to act out of love (Phlm 9), to receive Onesimus as he would Paul himself (Phlm 17) |

The letter to Philemon is the concrete, personal application of the principles laid out in the Colossian household code. Paul does not merely give abstract instructions about slaves and masters — he sends a runaway slave back to his master with a letter asking the master to treat him as a brother. The theological principles of Colossians 3:22-4:1 are immediately tested by the real-life situation of Philemon and Onesimus.
