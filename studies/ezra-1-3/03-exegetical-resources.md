# Ezra 1-3 — Exegetical Resources and Notes

This document records observations, questions, and findings from the use of exegetical tools, following the methodology in Chapter 7 of *Hermeneutics and Exegesis for Laypeople*.

---

## Key Hebrew Words and Phrases

### Chapter 1

- **הֵעִיר** (*he'ir*) — "stirred up" (1:1, 5). Hiphil (causative) of עוּר (*'ur*, "to arouse, awaken"). God is the subject both times: He stirred the spirit of Cyrus (v. 1) and He stirred the spirits of the people (v. 5). The Hiphil stem is significant — it means God *caused* the awakening. This is not an internal, self-generated impulse; it is divine initiative. The same verb is used of God stirring up the spirit of the Medes against Babylon (Jer 51:11) and stirring up a destroyer against Babylon (Jer 51:1). God's sovereign arousal of human agents is a pattern throughout the prophets.

- **רוּחַ** (*ruach*) — "spirit" (1:1, 5). Used for both Cyrus's spirit and the people's spirits. In this context, *ruach* refers to the inner disposition, will, or motivation of a person. God moved the deepest motivational center of both the pagan king and His own people. The same word can mean "wind" or "breath" — it carries the sense of an animating, invisible force.

- **פָּקַד** (*paqad*) — "appointed/charged" (1:2). Cyrus says the LORD "appointed" him (*peqad 'alay*) to build the temple. The verb *paqad* has a wide semantic range: to visit, to attend to, to appoint, to commission, to muster. In this context it carries the force of a royal commission — God has tasked Cyrus with a specific assignment. The same verb is used of God "visiting" His people in both judgment and deliverance (Gen 50:24; Exod 3:16; Ruth 1:6).

- **הַנִּשְׁאָר** (*hannish'ar*) — "the survivor/remnant" (1:4). Niphal participle of שָׁאַר (*sha'ar*, "to remain, survive"). This word connects Ezra's audience to the broader remnant theology of the prophets. Isaiah repeatedly spoke of a remnant that would return (Isa 10:20-22; 11:11, 16). The returnees are the fulfillment of prophetic expectation — the surviving remnant whom God preserved through exile.

- **נְדָבָה** (*nedavah*) — "freewill offering" (1:4, 6; 2:68; 3:5). From the root נָדַב (*nadav*, "to be willing, to volunteer"). This term denotes a voluntary, uncompelled offering — generosity that arises from a willing heart. It is used throughout the Pentateuch for offerings given beyond what is required (Lev 7:16; 22:18; Deut 16:10). The repetition of this word throughout Ezra 1-3 emphasizes that the restoration is characterized by willing, Spirit-stirred generosity — not coercion.

- **כְּלֵי** (*keley*) — "articles/vessels" (1:7-11). These are the sacred temple vessels that Nebuchadnezzar had plundered and placed in the temple of his gods (cf. Dan 1:2; 5:2-3). Their return is theologically significant: what was taken by conquest is restored by divine decree. The detailed inventory (vv. 9-11) underscores that God preserved these articles through 70 years of exile. Nothing was lost.

### Chapter 2

- **הַקָּהָל** (*haqqahal*) — "the assembly" (2:64). A technical term for the gathered congregation of God's people. This is the same word used for the assembly of Israel at Sinai (Deut 9:10; 10:4) and throughout Israel's history for the covenant community gathered before God. By using this term, Ezra signals that the returnees are not merely refugees returning to ancestral land — they are the reconstituted assembly of God's people, the continuation of the Sinai covenant community.

- **אוּרִים וְתֻמִּים** (*'urim vetummim*) — "Urim and Thummim" (2:63). Sacred lots used by the high priest for determining God's will (Exod 28:30; Num 27:21; 1 Sam 28:6). The governor's instruction that disputed priestly genealogies must wait for a priest with Urim and Thummim reveals two things: (1) the community took priestly purity seriously — they would not compromise on who could serve; (2) the full restoration of the priesthood was still future. This is an honest admission of the incompleteness of the present restoration.

### Chapter 3

- **כְּאִישׁ אֶחָד** (*ke'ish echad*) — "as one man" (3:1). An idiom expressing complete unity. The same phrase is used of Israel's unified response in Judges 20:1, 8 and Nehemiah 8:1. After 70 years of dispersion, the people gather with one heart and one purpose. This unity is remarkable given the diversity of families, towns, and social classes listed in chapter 2.

- **כַּכָּתוּב** (*kakkatuv*) — "as it is written" (3:2, 4). This phrase anchors the returnees' worship to the Mosaic Law. They are not improvising — they are deliberately returning to the written instructions of Torah. The phrase recurs throughout Ezra-Nehemiah (Ezra 3:2, 4; 6:18; Neh 8:15; 10:34-36) as a programmatic marker: the post-exilic community defines itself by faithfulness to Scripture. This is especially significant because one cause of the exile was Israel's departure from the written covenant (2 Kgs 17:13-15; 2 Chr 36:15-16).

- **אֵימָה** (*'eimah*) — "terror/dread" (3:3). The people were "terrified because of the peoples of the lands." This is not mere nervousness — *'eimah* denotes paralyzing fear (cf. Gen 15:12; Exod 15:16; Job 9:34). The returnees are a small, vulnerable community surrounded by hostile populations. Their decision to build the altar *despite* this fear is an act of faith: they prioritize worship over self-protection. They run *to* God in their fear rather than *from* their calling.

- **יְסַד** (*yesad*) — "to lay a foundation" (3:6, 10, 11, 12). This verb carries both literal and theological weight. Literally, they are laying the stone foundation of the second temple. Theologically, God is re-establishing His dwelling among His people. The repetition of this word in verses 10-12 (four times in four verses) creates emphasis: the foundation is the focal point of this scene. It represents the tangible beginning of God's promise fulfilled.

- **חֶסֶד** (*chesed*) — "lovingkindness/steadfast love" (3:11). The central covenant term of the Old Testament. When the people sing "His lovingkindness is toward Israel forever" (echoing Psalm 136), they are declaring that God's covenant faithfulness endures — even through judgment and exile. The exile did not exhaust God's *chesed*. This word appears over 240 times in the Old Testament and is the primary descriptor of God's covenant loyalty.

|---|---|
| **Jeremiah 25:11-12; 29:10** | The prophecy that Ezra 1:1 says is being fulfilled. God promised 70 years of Babylonian dominance, after which He would restore His people. Cyrus's decree in 538 BC marks the end of that period. |
| **Isaiah 44:28-45:1** | God names Cyrus by name approximately 150 years before his birth, calling him "My shepherd" and "His anointed." This is the most explicit Old Testament prophecy about a specific pagan ruler. Ezra 1 is the fulfillment. |

| **Daniel 1:1-2; 5:1-4** | The temple vessels that Nebuchadnezzar took (Dan 1:2) and that Belshazzar profaned at his feast (Dan 5:2-3) are the same vessels Cyrus returns in Ezra 1:7-11. What was desecrated by Babylon is restored by God's decree. |
| **Psalm 126** | "When the LORD restored the fortunes of Zion, we were like those who dream. Then our mouth was filled with laughter and our tongue with joyful shouting." Likely composed about the return described in Ezra 1-3. |
| **Deuteronomy 30:1-5** | Moses prophesied that after exile, God would restore His people and bring them back to the land. Ezra 1-3 fulfills this Mosaic promise. |
| **Exodus 25:1-7; 35:4-29** | The freewill offerings for the tabernacle construction parallel the freewill offerings in Ezra 1:4, 6; 2:68-69; 3:5. The restoration mirrors the original constitution of Israel's worship. |
| **1 Kings 5-6** | Solomon's original temple construction. The parallels with Ezra 3:7 are striking: both projects use cedar from Lebanon, employ Sidonians and Tyrians, and transport materials by sea to Joppa. The second temple deliberately echoes the first. |

| **Numbers 28-29** | The daily, monthly, and festival offerings that are restored in Ezra 3:3-5. The returnees are re-establishing the full Levitical sacrificial system. |

| **Zechariah 4:10** | "For who has despised the day of small things?" God encourages the community not to despise the humble beginning of the second temple. This directly addresses the weeping of Ezra 3:12. |
| **Psalm 136** | The refrain sung at the foundation-laying — "For His lovingkindness is toward Israel forever" (Ezra 3:11) — echoes the liturgical refrain of Psalm 136, where every verse ends with "His lovingkindness is everlasting." |
| **Ezra 6:3-5** | The Aramaic text of Cyrus's official decree, which includes dimensions for the temple and orders the return of the vessels. This provides the administrative counterpart to the Hebrew proclamation in 1:2-4. |

## Questions Arising from Study

1. **Who is Sheshbazzar (1:8, 11), and what is his relationship to Zerubbabel?** Sheshbazzar is called "the prince of Judah" and receives the temple vessels. Zerubbabel leads the returnees (2:2) and oversees the temple construction (3:8). Some scholars identify them as the same person; others see Sheshbazzar as an older figure (perhaps Shenazzar, a son of Jehoiachin — 1 Chr 3:18) who served as the initial political leader, with Zerubbabel succeeding him. The text does not explicitly resolve this.

2. **Why does the list in Ezra 2 include such detailed genealogical records?** The genealogical register is not bureaucratic filler — it establishes the legitimacy of the returning community as the true continuation of pre-exilic Israel. Identity in the covenant community required documented lineage. This is underscored by the exclusion of those who could not prove their ancestry (2:59-63). In a context where the people's identity as God's covenant community had been threatened by exile, these records are a declaration: *we are still Israel.*

3. **Why did the returnees build the altar before the temple (3:2-6)?** The altar could function without a temple building — sacrifice did not require a structure, only a consecrated altar (cf. the patriarchs, the tabernacle). The people prioritized the restoration of the sacrificial system because it restored their covenant relationship with God. Access to God through sacrifice was more urgent than architectural completion.

4. **What is the significance of the old men weeping in 3:12?** The old men who remembered Solomon's temple wept because the new foundation represented both fulfillment and loss. The second temple could not match the glory of the first (cf. Hag 2:3). Their tears express the tension of partial restoration: God is keeping His promise, but the present reality falls short of the former glory. This is an honest, faithful response — not a lack of gratitude but a recognition that full restoration has not yet come.

5. **How does the Feast of Booths (3:4) function in this context?** The Feast of Booths (Sukkot) commemorated Israel's wilderness wanderings and celebrated God's provision during that vulnerable period (Lev 23:42-43). For a community that had just endured exile — their own "wilderness" experience — this festival carried profound resonance. Celebrating it "as it is written" in the seventh month (Tishri) signals that the returnees understood their experience through the lens of Israel's founding story: God delivered them from captivity, sustained them through hardship, and brought them to a place of promise.

## Parallel Passage Comparison: Ezra 2 and Nehemiah 7

The list of returnees in Ezra 2 has a nearly identical parallel in Nehemiah 7:6-73. The two lists record the same community but with minor numerical variations.

| Category | Ezra 2 | Nehemiah 7 | Notable Differences |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sons of Arah | 775 (2:5) | 652 (7:10) | 123 difference |
| Sons of Pahath-moab | 2,812 (2:6) | 2,818 (7:11) | 6 difference |
| Sons of Zattu | 945 (2:8) | 845 (7:13) | 100 difference |
| Sons of Azgad | 1,222 (2:12) | 2,322 (7:17) | 1,100 difference |
| Sons of Adin | 454 (2:15) | 655 (7:20) | 201 difference |
| Sons of Bezai | 323 (2:17) | 324 (7:23) | 1 difference |
| Sons of Bethlehem/Netophah | 123 + 56 (2:21-22) | 188 (combined, 7:26) | Combined vs. separate listing |
| Singers | 128 (2:41) | 148 (7:44) | 20 difference |
| Total assembly | 42,360 (2:64) | 42,360 (7:66) | **Identical** |
| Servants | 7,337 (2:65) | 7,337 (7:67) | **Identical** |

The identical totals (42,360) despite differing individual figures suggest that both lists represent the same community census, with minor scribal variations accumulated through transmission. The agreement on totals confirms the reliability of the core record. Some scholars propose that the individual figures in one or both lists have suffered minor copying errors over centuries of transmission, while the sum totals were preserved independently and thus match perfectly.

The existence of two copies of this list — one in Ezra's context (the original return) and one in Nehemiah's context (used for community verification decades later) — demonstrates the ongoing importance of this registry for establishing identity in the covenant community.
