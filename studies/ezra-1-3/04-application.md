# Ezra 1-3 — Application

---

## (4) How does this truth apply to me?

### (A) What is my relationship to the human author?

Ezra was a priest and scribe — a man devoted to studying, practicing, and teaching the Law of the LORD (Ezra 7:10). He was not an apostle in the New Testament sense, but he was a recognized leader in the post-exilic community whose historical record was inspired by God and preserved as Scripture. I am a beneficiary of his faithful record-keeping: without Ezra's work, much of the history of God's restoration of Israel after exile would be lost. His writing carries the authority of Scripture (2 Tim 3:16-17), and the events he records reveal God's character and purposes for all generations.

### (B) What is my relationship to the original audience?

The original audience was the post-exilic Jewish community — people who had experienced (or whose parents had experienced) the devastating judgment of exile. They had lost their land, their temple, their king, and their national identity. Now they were returning to rebuild.

Like them, I am part of a community that lives between God's promises and their full realization. Like them, I worship a God who has proven faithful to His word even when circumstances seemed hopeless. Like them, I am called to respond to God's initiative with obedience, generosity, and worship — even when the surrounding culture is hostile and the work ahead is daunting.

### (C) What are the differences between my world and their world?

- **They lived under foreign imperial rule** (Persia), with limited political autonomy. Their ability to worship and rebuild depended on the permission of a pagan king. I live in a context with greater religious freedom, though that freedom is not guaranteed.
- **Their worship was centered on a physical temple** with animal sacrifices, a priesthood, and ceremonial law. As a New Testament believer, I worship through Christ, the true temple (John 2:19-21), the final sacrifice (Heb 10:10), and the great high priest (Heb 4:14-16). The physical temple was a shadow; the reality is Christ.
- **They were a national/ethnic community** (Israel). The people of God in the New Testament era is the church — drawn from every nation, tribe, and tongue (Rev 7:9). My "return from exile" is spiritual: rescue from sin and death through Christ.
- **They faced the tension of partial restoration.** The second temple was inferior to Solomon's (Ezra 3:12; Hag 2:3). I live in a time when the "greater temple" has come — Jesus Himself — but I still await the full consummation of God's kingdom.

### (D) Is my relationship to God different than theirs?

Yes and no. The God who stirred Cyrus's spirit and moved His people to return is the same God I worship — faithful, sovereign, covenant-keeping. His character has not changed.

However, my position in redemptive history is different. I live on the other side of the cross and resurrection. The returnees looked forward to a restored temple where God's presence would dwell; I know that God's presence now dwells in His people through the Holy Spirit (1 Cor 3:16; 6:19). The sacrifices they restored pointed forward to Christ's once-for-all sacrifice (Heb 9:11-12). I have a fuller revelation of God's redemptive plan than they did, which brings greater privilege and greater responsibility.

## General Application

### 1. God is sovereign over human history — even over rulers who do not know Him

The LORD stirred up the spirit of Cyrus (1:1). Cyrus did not come to saving faith in the God of Israel — his own cylinder inscription credits the Babylonian god Marduk for his conquests. Yet God used him precisely as prophesied (Isa 44:28-45:1). This truth anchors confidence: no political situation, no cultural shift, no human leader is beyond God's sovereign direction. When circumstances feel out of control, Ezra 1:1 reminds us that God moves kings like chess pieces to fulfill His word.

### 2. God keeps His promises — even when fulfillment requires generations

Jeremiah prophesied 70 years of exile (Jer 25:11-12; 29:10). God fulfilled that word to the year. The returnees in Ezra 1 were living proof that God does what He says He will do. Many who heard Jeremiah's original prophecy did not live to see its fulfillment. Faithfulness sometimes means trusting a promise whose fulfillment you will not personally witness. But God's word does not return void (Isa 55:11).

### 3. God stirs hearts — both to initiate and to respond

God stirred Cyrus to decree (1:1) and stirred the people to go (1:5). The same Hebrew word (עוּר, *'ur*) is used for both. God is the primary mover in salvation history. Human obedience is real and necessary — but it is God who awakens the desire. This should produce both humility (I did not generate my own faith) and confidence (God who began a good work will complete it — Phil 1:6).

### 4. Worship comes before building

The returnees rebuilt the altar and restored sacrifices (3:1-6) before they laid the temple foundation (3:10). Their first priority was not construction but worship. They did not wait for ideal conditions — they were "terrified because of the peoples of the lands" (3:3) — yet they worshiped anyway. Application: corporate worship and personal devotion are not activities to begin once life is stable. They are the foundation upon which everything else is built.

### 5. Obedience is anchored in Scripture — "as it is written"

The phrase "as it is written" appears repeatedly in chapter 3 (vv. 2, 4). The returnees did not improvise worship based on personal preference or cultural adaptation. They returned to the written Word of God — the Law of Moses — as the pattern for right worship. This models the priority of Scripture over tradition, experience, or innovation. How we worship God is determined by what He has revealed, not by what we find meaningful.

### 6. Generosity flows from gratitude

Those who stayed behind gave generously to those who went (1:4, 6). The heads of households gave freely for the temple treasury (2:68-69). This generosity was not compelled — it was "freewill" offering (1:4). When God moves hearts, the response includes open hands. Generosity is not a transaction with God; it is an overflow of gratitude for what He has already done.

### 7. Joy and grief can coexist in God's people

When the foundation was laid, some shouted for joy and others wept (3:12-13). The joy was real — God was restoring what was lost. The grief was real — the old men remembered the glory of Solomon's temple and saw how far short this new beginning fell. Both responses were legitimate. The life of faith includes moments where gratitude and lament are inseparable. We do not need to suppress either one.

## Application for Young Children (Ages 3-5)

The following activities and conversations translate the main truths of Ezra 1-3 into language, concepts, and experiences accessible to preschool and kindergarten-age children.

### Big Idea for Kids

**God keeps His promises, and He helps His people come home.**

---

### Truth 1: God Is in Charge of Everything — Even Kings

**The Story in Simple Words:**
A long time ago, God's people had to leave their home because they disobeyed God. They lived far, far away in a place called Babylon. But God made a promise — He would bring them back home. There was a big, powerful king named Cyrus. Cyrus didn't know God, but God put an idea in King Cyrus's heart: "Let My people go home and build My house." And that's exactly what Cyrus did!

**Conversation Starters:**
- "Who is in charge of everything — even kings and presidents and grown-ups? God is!"
- "Can anyone stop God's plans? No! When God decides to do something, it happens."
- "Has Mommy or Daddy ever changed plans because something surprised them? God is never surprised. He always knows what's going to happen."

**Activity — Crown and Decree:**
Help the child make a simple paper crown. Explain: "King Cyrus wore a crown and told everyone what to do. But even the king with the biggest crown does what God wants. God is the real King over everybody." Let the child "announce" a decree like Cyrus: "Let God's people go home!"

---

### Truth 2: God Always Keeps His Promises

**The Story in Simple Words:**
A man named Jeremiah told God's people, "You will be away from home for a long, long time — 70 years. But then God will bring you back." And that's exactly what happened! God remembered His promise even when it took a very, very long time.

**Conversation Starters:**
- "When Mommy or Daddy says, 'We'll go to the park tomorrow,' do they keep that promise? God always keeps every single promise He makes — always."
- "Sometimes we have to wait a long time for something. Waiting is hard! But God never forgets what He said He would do."
- "Can you think of something you had to wait for? God's people waited and waited — and then God did exactly what He said!"

**Activity — Promise Chain:**
Cut strips of colored paper. On each strip, write (or have the child decorate) a simple promise from God: "I will be with you," "I love you," "I will never leave you." Link them together into a paper chain. Hang it up as a reminder: "God has so many promises, and He keeps every one."

---

### Truth 3: God Helps Us Want to Do the Right Thing

**The Story in Simple Words:**
When King Cyrus said, "You can go home," not everybody wanted to go. It was a long, hard trip. But God put something special in some people's hearts — He made them *want* to go. God helped them be brave and say, "Yes! We'll go!"

**Conversation Starters:**
- "Sometimes it's hard to do the right thing, isn't it? Like sharing your toys or being kind when you're upset. But God can help you *want* to do the right thing."
- "When you feel like being brave or kind or generous, that might be God helping your heart — just like He helped those people want to go home!"
- "Can you ask God to help you want to obey? He loves to help us."

**Activity — Heart Craft:**
Cut out a large paper heart. Inside it, draw or paste pictures of things God helps us want to do: share, be kind, pray, help. Title it: "God Helps My Heart."

---

### Truth 4: We Talk to God First — Before Anything Else

**The Story in Simple Words:**
When God's people got back home, what do you think they did first? Did they build their houses? No! Did they plant their gardens? No! The very first thing they did was build a place to pray and worship God. They wanted to talk to God and thank Him before they did anything else.

**Conversation Starters:**
- "What is the first thing we do in the morning? What if we talked to God first — before breakfast, before playing?"
- "Why do you think God's people wanted to worship Him first? Because they loved God and knew He was the one who brought them home!"
- "We can talk to God anytime — but it's extra special to talk to Him first."

**Activity — "First Things First" Chart:**
Make a simple morning routine chart with the child. Put "Talk to God" (a picture of praying hands) at the very top. Reinforce: "Just like God's people worshiped first, we start our day with God."

**Activity — Build an Altar (Block Play):**
Using building blocks, let the child "build an altar" like the people did. Explain: "An altar was a special place where people worshiped God. They built it first because God is the most important." After building, say a simple prayer of thanks together at the "altar."

---

### Truth 5: We Can Give Because God Gives to Us

**The Story in Simple Words:**
God's people gave their gold and silver and even their animals to help build God's house. Nobody made them do it — they *wanted* to give because they were so happy and thankful that God brought them home.

**Conversation Starters:**
- "Have you ever wanted to give something to someone because you love them — like a drawing for Mommy or a hug for Daddy? That's what God's people did! They gave because they loved God."
- "Everything we have — our toys, our food, our home — comes from God. When we share and give, we're saying 'Thank you, God!'"

**Activity — Giving Box:**
Decorate a small box together. Label it "Giving Box" or "Thank You, God Box." Throughout the week, the child can put small items in it (coins, toys to donate, drawings to give away). Talk about how giving makes God happy — and how it makes us happy too.

---

### Truth 6: It's Okay to Be Happy and Sad at the Same Time

**The Story in Simple Words:**
When the builders finished the bottom part of God's new house, something amazing happened. Some people were SO happy that they shouted and cheered! But some of the old grandpas and grandmas cried — they remembered how beautiful the old house was, and this new one wasn't the same. Some people were laughing and some were crying, all at the same time! It was so loud you couldn't tell the happy sounds from the sad sounds.

**Conversation Starters:**
- "Have you ever felt happy and sad at the same time? Like when you're excited about something new but you miss something old?"
- "It's okay to cry even when good things are happening. And it's okay to be happy even when things aren't perfect. God understands all our feelings."
- "The grandpas were sad because they missed the old beautiful temple. The young people were happy because God was doing something new. Both feelings were okay with God."

**Activity — Feelings Faces:**
Draw two big circles. On one, draw a big smile ("happy face"). On the other, draw tears ("sad face"). Hold them both up at the same time. "Sometimes we feel both at once — and God sees both and loves us."

---

### Songs and Memory Verse

**Memory Verse (simplified):**
> "He is good. His love lasts forever." — from Ezra 3:11

Practice this with hand motions:
- "He is good" — point up (to God) and give a thumbs up
- "His love" — cross arms over chest in a hug
- "lasts forever" — stretch arms out wide, then spin in a circle

**Song Suggestion:**
Sing the refrain from Ezra 3:11 to a simple tune the child knows (like "Are You Sleeping?" or any familiar melody):
> "He is good, He is good, His love lasts forever, His love lasts forever, praise the Lord, praise the Lord!"

### Prayer for Kids

> Dear God,
> Thank You that You are in charge of everything.
> Thank You that You always keep Your promises.
> Please help my heart want to do the right thing.
> Help me talk to You first and give because You give to me.
> Thank You that You love me — forever and ever.
> Amen.

### Tips for Parents and Teachers

1. **Read the story, don't lecture.** Young children learn theology through narrative. Tell the story of Ezra 1-3 with energy and expression. Use toy figures or blocks to act it out.

2. **Repeat the Big Idea.** Come back to "God keeps His promises, and He helps His people come home" throughout the week. Repetition builds retention at this age.

3. **Connect to daily life.** When a child experiences waiting, remind them: "Remember how God's people waited 70 years? God kept His promise. He keeps His promises to us too." When a child is generous, affirm: "You're being like the people who gave for God's house!"

4. **Don't over-explain.** Children ages 3-5 do not need to understand Persian imperial politics or the theology of the remnant. They need to know: God is big, God keeps promises, God helps us, and God loves us. The deeper layers will come with age and maturity.

5. **Let the emotions be real.** When discussing the mixed joy and weeping of Ezra 3:12-13, validate the child's emotional experience. Young children often feel big, complex emotions they can't name. This passage gives permission: you can feel many things at once, and God is with you in all of them.

6. **Use the crafts as conversation anchors.** The paper crown, promise chain, heart craft, and block altar are not just busywork — they are physical reminders of spiritual truths. Leave them visible and refer back to them: "Remember your promise chain? God keeps all those promises."
