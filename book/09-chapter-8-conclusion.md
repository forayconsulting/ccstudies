# 8. Conclusion

What have we learned in this book?

We began with some interpretive concepts.

We've seen how foundational the doctrine of inspiration is to the entire practice of studying our Bibles. Therefore, we affirm the view of Christians throughout history: Verbal Plenary Inspiration, meaning that the precise wording of Scripture is God-breathed and thus bears God's authority. In other words, the Bible is both the Word of God and the words of God.

Hermeneutics (the principles used to determine the meaning of a text) are built upon the doctrine of inspiration. 2 Peter 1:19-21 tells us that the way we get meaning out of the text is dependent on the way God put meaning into the text. Therefore, we ascribe to a Literal Historical Grammatical Hermeneutic, a commonsense reading, taking into account the historical setting and linguistic choices of the author to determine his originally intended meaning.

And sound exegesis (the process used to determine the meaning of a text) is based upon this Literal Historical Grammatical Hermeneutic. Getting to authorial intent takes hard work! But the work of exegesis is worth it; we reap invaluable benefits when we seek to grasp the context and original meaning of every passage we study.

In addition to these interpretive concepts, we've also learned some practical skills.

We learned how to ask the text questions. What did this text mean to the human author? What does this text teach me about God? How should this text contribute to my theology? And how does this text apply to me? We should bring these essential questions to the table when we study, seeking to understand the meaning, significance, and application of every text. But how do we actually answer them?

Well, we started with outlining. Outlining is essential because it develops authorial intent, discovers the passage's structure, and determines sections for concentrated study. As we notate the structures, themes, and argument flow of a book of the Bible, we gain valuable literary and grammatical context to help us answer our questions about the text.

We also learned how to utilize exegetical tools. With solid reading comprehension, organized note-taking, and interpretive freedom, we can use others' conclusions to help us form our own. Study Bibles, commentaries, Bible dictionaries, lexicons, concordances, and other reliable tools can provide valuable context to help us answer our questions about the text and double-check our interpretive instincts.

Speaking of resources, I hope this book proves helpful for you.

As I stated in the introduction, my goal is to foster confidence. These things matter, not only for pastors and "professionals," but for ordinary Christians. These things matter because they were revealed to you, because wolves don't go after shepherds, and because you're in the ministry.

That being said, our confidence is not found in ourselves as able interpreters, but in God's Word as clear and understandable. Go forth and dive into the riches of the Scriptures. Explore ancient truths. Uncover new treasures. And remember:

> *"The grass withers, the flower fades, but the word of our God stands forever"* (Isaiah 40:8).
