# 1. Introduction

This book is an introduction to intermediate Bible study concepts.

If you're a brand new believer, unfamiliar with the Bible or inductive study, some of these concepts may go over your head. And if you're an experienced exegete, this book will probably retread old ground. However (with that disclaimer aside), I've seen new believers and seminarians alike benefit from this material. Sometimes, the best way to learn something new is to get just slightly in over your head. And sometimes, the best way to stay sharp is to go back to the basics.

I hope this book encourages and serves you wherever you are. My goal is to foster confidence, not primarily in ourselves but in God's Word. When you study your Bible, I want you to feel confident that it is clear. Through informed and prayerful study, you can understand what God has revealed and explain it to edify yourself and others.

## Why This Matters

Most people would agree that hermeneutics and exegesis matter for pastors, professors, and other "professional" Christians. But why do ordinary Christians need to go that deep? Why does it matter for you and me? I want to propose three reasons why.

### Because it was revealed to you!

Deuteronomy 29:29 says this, "The secret things belong to the LORD our God, but the things revealed belong to us and to our sons forever, that we may observe all the words of this law." In other words, some things have not been revealed to us, things we will never know this side of heaven. But, on the other hand, there is much that has been revealed to us.

The Bible is full of information about God, His plan, His desires, His people, and the world He created. And Deuteronomy 29:29 says that these things were revealed to us "that we may observe" them – so that we would understand and obey. Hermeneutics and exegesis matter to ordinary Christians because Scripture has been revealed to us, and we are responsible for understanding and obeying it.

### Because wolves don't go after the shepherds!

In Matthew 7:15, Jesus says, "Beware of false prophets, who come to you in sheep's clothing, but inwardly are ravenous wolves." He's warning us about people who pretend to be fellow Christians to serve their own malicious interests. And He calls them "false prophets." They're preachers and pastors and professors – public men claiming to speak for God. But Jesus calls them "ravenous wolves," that is, predators.

Later on, in Acts 20:29-30, Paul uses similar language with the elders at Ephesus. "I know that after my departure savage wolves will come in among you, not sparing the flock; and from among your own selves men will arise, speaking perverse things, to draw away the disciples after them." Note that Paul is speaking to the leaders of the church. He's saying that these spiritual predators will arise "from among your own selves," that is, not from among the congregants but from among the elders.

It's very rare to find a false teacher who is content to remain an ordinary Christian. False teachers are usually in it for money or power, and you can't get those things by being a nobody. They crave influence; they need followers. Like wolves who don't hunt the shepherds but rather the sheep, these false teachers don't usually go after our pastors. They go after us. Hermeneutics and exegesis matter to ordinary Christians because ordinary Christians are the primary target of our enemy's deceit.

### Because you're in the ministry!

I love what Ephesians 4:11-12 says about ministry. "And [God] gave some as apostles, and some as prophets, and some as evangelists, and some as pastors and teachers, for the equipping of the saints for the work of ministry." It's not the apostles, prophets, evangelists, and pastor-teachers who are "in the ministry" (as we like to say). It's the saints who are "in the ministry." The work of the leaders is to equip the saints for their ministry. Think about that.

We aren't in the pew to support our pastor in his ministry; our pastor is in the pulpit to support us in our ministry! God wants to use us to encourage, admonish, instruct, and inform the people around us. It's our job to evangelize the lost and disciple the saved. And it's our pastors' job to set us up for success. Hermeneutics and exegesis matter to ordinary Christians because we're in the ministry.

## Defining Terms

Before we dive in, there are some essential terms I'd like to define. While it's unnecessary to memorize these, I will continue to refer to them throughout this book. It might be helpful to read these definitions a few times, carefully, before moving on.

- **Interpretation** – A judgment as to the meaning of a text.
- **Hermeneutics** – The principles used to determine an interpretation of a text.
- **Exegesis** – The process used to determine an interpretation of a text.
- **Authorial Intent** – The original meaning intended by the author of a text.

As you've probably noticed, this book derives its title from these two middle terms: hermeneutics and exegesis. Some people use these terms almost interchangeably, but I want to commend a more precise usage because they do mean slightly different things. Where hermeneutics refers to principles, exegesis refers to a process. It's a bit like doing science.

On the one hand, you have the scientific method: a set of principles that govern the practice of scientific discovery. But on the other hand, you have the actual process of "doing science." You could be a field researcher fetching soil samples in a remote jungle, a lab technician examining those samples under a microscope, or a developer building a virtual dashboard to analyze millions of those samples statistically. All these different jobs and procedures are ultimately governed by the same investigative principles that drive all scientific pursuits.

Hermeneutics and exegesis are similar. A set of hermeneutical principles ultimately govern all the various exegetical processes involved in interpreting a passage of Scripture. These principles are often unconscious, but everyone has a hermeneutic, whether they know it or not. We must become more self-aware. How are we reading the Bible? What underlying presuppositions do we bring to the table? And are those principles coherent and consistent?

## The Pyramid of Interpretation

There's an illustration I like to use to demonstrate the importance of foundations when interpreting the Bible. I call it "The Pyramid of Interpretation." This illustration will also function as our outline for the remainder of this book, and I'll return to it often.

### Why a pyramid?

Because there's only one way to build a pyramid: from the ground up. You can't reach the finest point without first building upon a much broader foundation. And the study of God's Word works the same way. Increasingly specific sets of principles drive our practices.

As we work our way up the pyramid, we're going to brave a little complexity in order to arrive at a more educated kind of simplicity. I'll first introduce you to some complex topics to challenge you to think more intentionally about how you study the Bible. Then I'll give you some simple, practical tools to use in your own Bible study.

Remember our definitions. Interpretation (a judgment about the meaning of a text) rests upon exegesis. Exegesis (the process used to determine an interpretation) rests upon hermeneutics. And Hermeneutics (the principles used to determine an interpretation) rests upon something called "The Doctrine of Inspiration." So that's where we'll start.
