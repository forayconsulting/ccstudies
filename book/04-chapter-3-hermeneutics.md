# 3. Hermeneutics

We've already looked at the foundation of our pyramid. Now we're going to move up a level. And as we do, it's important to remember our definition of Hermeneutics: The principles used to determine an interpretation of a text.

Throughout the gospels, we find several passages in which Jesus asks the religious leaders a particularly piercing question: "Have you not read?" (Matthew 12:3, 5, 19:4, 21:16, 42, 23:31, Mark 2:25, 12:10, 26, Luke 6:3) Now, Jesus didn't mean they had never read the Scriptures; it was a rhetorical question. As the premier experts in Mosaic Law, they had read the Scriptures more than anyone. Instead, Jesus was implying that they had misread the Scriptures. Their hermeneutic was so misinformed that it was almost as if they had never read the Scriptures at all.

You see, hermeneutics is all about the presuppositions we bring to the text. Why do we read the Bible the way we do? As we consider this topic, we mustn't miss the brilliance of 2 Peter 1:19-21.

> "So we have the prophetic word made more sure, to which you do well to pay attention as to a lamp shining in a dark place, until the day dawns and the morning star arises in your hearts. But know this first of all, that no prophecy of Scripture is a matter of one's own interpretation, for no prophecy was ever made by an act of human will, but men moved by the Holy Spirit spoke from God."

In verse 19, Peter says, "We have the prophetic word made more sure." More sure than what? If we read the previous sentence, we find a reference to the Mount of Transfiguration. "For when [Jesus] received honor and glory from God the Father, such an utterance was made to Him by the Majestic Glory, 'This is my beloved Son with whom I am well-pleased' – and we ourselves heard this utterance made from heaven when we were with Him on the holy mountain." (For the full story, read Matthew 17:1-9.) Peter's encounter on the Mount of Transfiguration wasn't a vision or a dream; it was a genuine experience of the glory of God. But one verse later, he says that Scripture is "made more sure." In other words, the God-breathed writings of the Bible are a more trustworthy source of truth than even personal experience. If we hold to verbal plenary inspiration, this should come as no surprise. If we truly possess the written revelation of the One who "created the heavens and the earth" (Genesis 1:1) and "upholds all things by the word of His power" (Hebrews 1:3), then it surely exceeds whatever limited wisdom or experience we possess.

Notice the imagery in what Peter says next: "To which you do well to pay attention as to a lamp shining in a dark place, until the day dawns and the morning star arises in your hearts." The world has been a dark place ever since sin entered it. People keep secrets from one another. Our human faculties – perception, reasoning, conceptualization – have been corrupted due to the Fall. It's difficult to determine the truth. There will come a day when God will remove those limitations and withhold no cosmic mystery from us. 1 Corinthians 13:12 puts it eloquently, "For now we see in a mirror dimly, but then face to face; now I know in part, but then I will know fully just as I also have been fully known."

But until that day, we must use Scripture as "a lamp shining in a dark place." Like a flashlight on a starless night, Scripture is a tool for understanding the world around us. That's what Peter's getting at in this verse. Because the Word of God is more trustworthy than personal experience, we must strive to examine everything in its light. We must "take every thought captive to the obedience of Christ" (2 Corinthians 10:5), "leaning not on our own understanding" (Proverbs 3:5), but acknowledging the wisdom of God in the Scriptures as our primary source of truth.

That's a compelling reality, isn't it? I hope we're eager to comply! But even as we rush to put this into practice, Peter pumps the brakes. His next phrase comes abruptly: "But know this first of all." In other words, before you take these principles and run with them, there's something you must know.

"No prophecy of Scripture is a matter of one's own interpretation." When Peter uses the term "prophecy" here, he's not referring to passages about the future or a genre of Old Testament literature. He's referring to the concept of inspired proclamation. In this sense, any statement that proceeds directly from God is a "prophecy." Since "all Scripture is inspired by God" (2 Timothy 3:16), every word, phrase, sentence, paragraph, and book of the Bible is "prophecy" in the way Peter is using the term here. So what's he getting at?

He's saying that the meaning of a biblical text is not a matter of subjective interpretation. As human readers, we don't get to determine the meaning of the Scriptures; we simply discover the meaning of the Scriptures. Think about that. One passage can't mean two different things based on who's reading it or how they understand it.

Well, why not? Peter goes on to give us the reason. "For no prophecy was ever made by an act of human will." In saying this, Peter rejects the Natural Theory of Inspiration and affirms that Scripture is God-breathed. Scripture didn't originate from men alone, and it didn't acquire meaning apart from God. Therefore, we're not free to reinterpret it. We are bound to understand it the way God meant it to be understood. Notice how Peter leans into Verbal Plenary Inspiration in his final phrase.

"But men moved by the Holy Spirit spoke from God." We've already discussed passages like 2 Timothy 3:16-17 and 2 Samuel 23:2. And as we can see, Peter's clearly on the same page as Paul and David. God sovereignly used the skills, languages, and life experiences of human authors to produce the exact message He desired to communicate to His people. In describing this beautiful dual authorship, Peter affirms that the precise wording of Scripture is God-breathed and bears God's authority.

This passage is brilliant, and it demonstrates something essential. For Peter, the Doctrine of Inspiration was foundational for hermeneutics. Do you see that? If we hold to anything other than Verbal Plenary Inspiration, we can ultimately reinterpret any passage to mean anything we want. But if we agree with what the Bible says about itself, affirming its dual authorship, we can derive objective meaning from the Scriptures.

In 2 Peter 1:19-21, Peter advocates a straightforward, literal, but Spirit-empowered reading of the Scriptures. And from this text, we can derive an important hermeneutical principle.

> **How we get meaning out of the text depends on how God put meaning into the text!**

As we interpret Scripture, we're not changing the meaning of Scripture. God determined the meaning of Scripture when He inspired it. And we must extract that meaning in alignment with the circumstances and intentions of the human authors that God used to communicate His message.

This principle also highlights the role of prayer in accurate Bible interpretation. God put meaning into the text through the Holy Spirit. Therefore, our efforts to extract meaning from the text must also involve the Holy Spirit. Just as the Spirit "moved" the authors, He must illuminate the readers. We must keep this in mind as we study the Bible. I often begin my daily reading with a short prayer from Psalm 119:18, asking the Spirit to guide my interpretation. "Open my eyes, that I may behold wonderful things from Your law."

Now, just as there are multiple theories of inspiration, there are also multiple approaches to hermeneutics. We'll look at some imbalanced, insufficient views in a moment. But first, I'll recommend what I believe is the most consistent, biblical way of reading the Scriptures. This approach is called the "literal historical grammatical" hermeneutic. Let's explore this concept.

> **Literal Historical Grammatical Hermeneutic** – A commonsense reading, taking into account the historical setting and linguistic choices of the author to determine his originally intended meaning.

By "literal," we mean rooted in normal human methods of communication. We don't interpret Scripture as an ethereal, symbolic, or mystical mode of communication. Instead, we interpret it as literature. The Bible communicates God's truth in regular, understandable terms; no secret meanings hide beneath the surface.

Now, a word of clarification: by "literal," I do not mean "hyper-literal." You may be familiar with a series of children's books called Amelia Bedelia, in which the titular nanny constantly misinterprets figurative language. When her employer tells her to "draw the drapes," she gets out a pen and paper; or when she is asked to "dust the furniture," she coats the couch in baby powder. Obviously, these are exaggerated scenarios meant to teach children the importance of context when interpreting normal human methods of communication. But the same rules apply to the Bible.

For example, Solomon used figurative language to describe the sun in Ecclesiastes 1:5. "The sun rises and the sun sets; and hastening to its place it rises there again." But in the 1500s, when Copernicus first proposed the heliocentric model of the solar system, religious opponents used this verse against him. They insisted that God's Word contradicted his model because, at face value, this verse describes the sun moving around the earth. However, we understand that Solomon is simply using normal human methods of communication. In reality, the earth revolves around the sun. But even knowing this, we still speak of the "sunrise" and "sunset." We aren't making a scientific statement; we're just using language grounded in our experience. And we must assume the same of Solomon. Like any of us, the biblical authors used figurative language that we should not interpret hyper-literally.

By "historical," we mean rooted in the historical context of the time it was written. We understand that even though God's Word is timeless, He inspired it through the life experiences of human authors who lived in various historical eras. Therefore, to understand the meaning of the Scriptures, we must understand the people, places, events, and worldviews they reference.

When you're reading a book from a different historical era, themes and references often go unnoticed until you learn a bit more about its original context. Imagine reading Huckleberry Finn with no awareness of American slavery. You might enjoy the swashbuckling adventure, but you would miss a great deal of what Mark Twain was trying to say. And it's the same way with the Bible; its meaning is rooted in a historical context.

And by "grammatical," we mean rooted in the linguistic context of the languages in which it was written. God did not inspire the Scriptures in English. The human authors wrote in ancient Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek. Even though God's Word transcends ethnic and linguistic barriers, He inspired it through human authors who wrote in various ancient languages.

Have you ever watched a foreign film? Some things get lost in translation. Even with subtitles or English dubs, you miss some of the original screenplay's nuances. It's complicated to translate humor, rhyme, wordplay, or slang from one language into another, even more so with ancient languages. And yet, the Bible is chock-full of humor, rhyme, wordplay, and slang in ancient Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek. In the 21st Century, we're blessed with plenty of reliable, academically rigorous translations of the Bible – but no translation will capture every nuance. We must acknowledge that the truth of the Scriptures is rooted in various linguistic contexts.

Now, we're getting into territory that requires frequent clarification, and here's another one. Although I am commending the "literal historical grammatical" hermeneutic, I am not saying that literature, history, and grammar are the only important considerations in understanding the Bible. For example, we must also understand a text's canonical and theological significance. Biblical theology ought to inform our reading of any Scripture. However, this quickly becomes a complicated subject, and one we will touch on later. For now, let's examine some imbalanced, insufficient approaches to hermeneutics.

## Imbalanced Views

At one time, I called this section "Competing Views," but a friend encouraged me to rename it. There are two reasons for this. First of all, there are far more competing views than can be captured in this chapter, and an effort to catalog all of them would be a book unto itself. Instead, what you'll find here are three streamlined categories representing a wide swath of objections to the literal historical grammatical hermeneutic. Secondly, these views are not truly "competing." In fact, each revolves around an important truth that is simply over-emphasized. That is to say: none of these views are *never* the right way to read Scripture, and none of these views are *always* the right way to read Scripture. Let's look at them one by one.

### Moral/Allegorical Hermeneutic

> **Moral/Allegorical Hermeneutic** – An application-driven reading, prioritizing the personal relevance of the text over the actual meaning of the text.

In this view, what the text means to me is more important than what the text meant to the original authors. If we read the story of David and Goliath in this way, we might take its meaning to be: "God wants me to be courageous and face my giants in His strength!" Now, that's not an invalid thought. But the original author (presumably Samuel) didn't record that story to make that point. In our rush to apply this text to ourselves, we may have missed its actual significance.

For another example, take John 19:30. "Therefore when Jesus had received the sour wine, He said, 'It is finished!' And He bowed His head and gave up His spirit." If our hermeneutic prioritized the personal relevance of this text, we might arrive at something like this: "Christ's death teaches us the meaning of sacrificial love for one another." Now, that is indeed a biblical truth. And Paul makes that point in Philippians 2:5 ("Have this attitude in yourselves which was also in Christ Jesus"). But that's not John's purpose in this particular narrative. In this case, John is highlighting the completed nature of Christ's work on the cross.

As I mentioned, there's an important truth in this view that's simply over-emphasized. There are indeed morals, fables, and allegories in Scripture. For example, Jesus' parables were often allegorical. Many Old Testament prophecies used metaphor and symbolic imagery. And we also find stories and statements that teach moral lessons. The Proverbs are an obvious example of this. When Nathan confronted David about his sin with Bathsheba, he told an allegorical story. In other words, it didn't actually happen; it was a carefully constructed illustration designed to impart a moral lesson. However, this is not how we are to interpret all of Scripture.

### Anagogical Hermeneutic

> **Anagogical Hermeneutic** – A mystical or codical reading, often attempting to identify secret patterns of words, numbers, or locations in order to unpack a more significant meaning.

In this view, word choice (diction) is more significant than word order (context.) I sometimes refer to this as the "National Treasure" approach to the Scriptures. If you've seen that movie, you know what I'm talking about. Historical documents like the Declaration of Independence are full of riddles and codes, unbeknownst to the average reader. But if you can follow the clues and solve the puzzle, you will discover long-hidden wealth and riches.

Many of the Early Church Fathers had a similar view of Scripture. Today, this anagogical hermeneutic is most common in Jewish and Roman Catholic interpretive traditions. For example, Kabbalists believe there is a mystical meaning to the Hebrew Scriptures, which can only be unlocked by decoding secret clues in the text. The Catholic Church holds similar views, understanding specific keywords and phrases as mystical references to the Christian's eternal destiny. Another example, perhaps more familiar to evangelicals, is those who attempt to calculate the Day of the Lord. They pore through the Scriptures, forming hypotheses about the date when Jesus will return to earth. These are ultimately anagogical readings of the Bible.

There's an important but over-emphasized truth here, too. Scripture does indeed contain some fascinating patterns, genealogies, and literary devices. For example, Psalm 119 is an acrostic; each section starts with the next letter in the Hebrew alphabet to convey the fullness of God's Word. Just like contemporary poetry, the Psalms and Proverbs are full of double meanings, turns of speech, and chiastic structures. Biblical names are also laden with meaning. God spoke to the people of Israel through the names of Isaiah's sons, Shear-Jashub ("A remnant shall return") and Maher-Shalal-Hash-Baz ("Swift is the spoil, speedy is the prey"). And it's no accident that our Lord was given the names Emmanuel ("God with us") and Jesus (a Hellenized form of Joshua, meaning "God saves").

There are even times when a text may have a higher, mystical interpretation. In Hebrews 9:1-5, the author alludes to the metaphysical meaning behind the instruments of Temple worship (and then, frustratingly, says, "but of these things we cannot now speak in detail"). And in Genesis 49:10, when Jacob is blessing his sons, he alludes prophetically to the Messiah, calling him "Shiloh." In such instances, an anagogical sense is indeed present, and we must not ignore it. However, this is not how we are to interpret all of Scripture.

### Theological Hermeneutic

> **Theological Hermeneutic** – A theology-driven reading, interpreting individual passages of Scripture in the light of theological presuppositions.

This one sounds good, doesn't it? We like theology, and we like hermeneutics. So why not do them both together? Well, if we were perfect beings, our minds un-corrupted by the Fall, perhaps we could. But this side of heaven, we are prone to intellectual error. We tend to read the Scriptures with a kind of confirmation bias, always interpreting new evidence as confirmation of our pre-existing theological beliefs. In reality, we must work to avoid this. We want to derive our theology from the text, not impose our theology on the text.

One example of this would be excessive typology. In the Bible, a "type" is an Old Testament character, object, or situation that foreshadows something in the New Testament or the Church. There are plenty of types in the Bible. The Scriptures explicitly identify Adam, Noah, Melchizedek, Isaac, Moses, Aaron, David, Solomon, and others as types of Christ. The Old Testament sacrificial system typifies the atonement that Christ fulfilled in His death on the cross. The Scriptures themselves establish and explain the significance of such typological events. But an overly theological hermeneutic would "go beyond what is written" (1 Corinthians 4:6) and attempt to find types in every story. In this view, every morally upright protagonist is a type of Christ. Every action in David's life (even those which were sinful!) must parallel something positive in the work of Christ. Because this approach goes too far, some have called it "excessive typology."

Another example would be extreme eschatology. There are lots of opinions about the "end times," and I'll spare you mine. But in this area of study, individual texts are often overshadowed by theological presuppositions. If you hold a more dispensational view, you'll tend to read prophecies about Israel's future more literally. If you have a more covenantal view, you might see those prophecies as fulfilled in Christ or the New Testament Church. But on both sides of the debate, there's often a tendency to read theological presuppositions into the text. A New Testament passage about the sobering reality of future judgment becomes a sermon series on the order of events in the last days. An Old Testament text about Israel's return from Babylon becomes a message about international missions in the Church.

In *Toward an Exegetical Theology*, Walt Kaiser warns us about an overly theological hermeneutic. "In no way may a theological grid be arbitrarily dropped over the text as a substitute for a diligent search for a unifying theological principle through the process of induction. Simply to impose a theological grid on the text must be condemned as the mark of a foolish and lazy exegete."

## Summary

None of these imbalanced views contain the nuance and balance necessary. The Literal Historical Grammatical hermeneutic is the most logical, consistent way to read Scripture. As we continue to the next level of our pyramid, we'll continue to flesh out the implications of this view for the study of God's Word.
