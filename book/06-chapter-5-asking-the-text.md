# 5. Asking the Text

We've arrived at the fourth and final level of our pyramid. Over the previous chapters, we've laid a foundation of Bibliology and built upon it with Hermeneutics and Exegesis. Now it's time to bring it all together. At this level, we arrive at the goal of the entire process.

> **Interpretation:** *A judgment as to the meaning of a text.*

This is where everything we've learned converges. The goal of this chapter is to bridge the gap between beginner Bible study and advanced seminary studies. You don't need to be a scholar to interpret the Bible well. You simply need to know what questions to ask. And that's exactly what we're going to learn.

One of the best ways to study the Bible is to *ask questions of the text*. In this chapter, we'll explore four "primary" questions, each with its own set of "subordinate" questions. Together, these questions form a comprehensive framework for interpreting any passage of Scripture. You'll find the complete bulleted list in Appendix A, and I'd encourage you to keep it handy as you study. But for now, let's walk through each one with examples.

## (1) What did this text mean to the human author?

This first question aims to discover authorial intent. If we hold to the literal historical grammatical hermeneutic, then the meaning of a text is rooted in the intention of its author. Before we can understand what a passage means for us, we must first understand what it meant to the person who wrote it. The following subordinate questions help us get there.

### (1A) Who were they?

Knowing the identity of the author sheds light on the meaning of their words. Consider Matthew 22:41-46, where Jesus turns the tables on the Pharisees with a question of His own. He asks them about Psalm 110, pointing to David as its author: "If David then calls Him 'Lord,' how is He his son?" Jesus' question only works if we know who wrote the psalm. The identity of the author -- David, the king of Israel -- is essential to understanding the significance of the text.

### (1B) When did they write this?

The timing of a text's composition can dramatically affect its meaning. Take Ezekiel 19:1 as an example. Ezekiel writes a lamentation for the princes of Judah. What makes this remarkable is that Ezekiel wrote it three to four years *before* the events he was lamenting actually happened. Understanding when this was written transforms it from a retrospective funeral song into a stunning prophetic pronouncement.

### (1C) Where did they live?

Geography matters. The physical location of the author can illuminate the emotional and theological weight of a passage. Psalm 137 is a powerful example.

> "By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down and wept, when we remembered Zion."

The psalmist isn't writing from the comfort of home. He's writing from exile, far from the land God promised to His people. The setting is essential to the meaning.

### (1D) What were they addressing?

Context is everything. One of the most frequently quoted verses in the Bible is Jeremiah 29:11: "For I know the plans that I have for you, declares the Lord, plans for welfare and not for calamity to give you a future and a hope." But if we look at verse 10, we discover that God is speaking specifically about the completion of seventy years in Babylon. Jeremiah was addressing the exiles, assuring them that their captivity had a divinely appointed end. Understanding what the author was addressing prevents us from ripping a verse out of its context and assigning it a meaning the author never intended.

### (1E) What was in their Bible?

This question deals with the concept of progressive revelation. The Bible was written over roughly 1,500 years, and God revealed His truth progressively. Each biblical author had a different amount of Scripture available to them. Understanding what was in an author's Bible helps us trace the threads of thought they were weaving.

Consider 2 Timothy 4:6, where Paul writes, "For I am already being poured out as a drink offering." That phrase, "drink offering," is not arbitrary. Paul is alluding to the drink offerings described in Leviticus 23 and Numbers 15. He's drawing on imagery from his Bible to describe the sacrificial nature of his life and ministry. We can only catch this if we ask what was in his Bible.

## (2) What does this text teach me about God?

The Bible is not merely a book about us; it is a book about God. This second primary question observes how God reveals Himself through His Word. Because of the dual authorship of Scripture, every text teaches us something about the God who inspired it. Let's use Exodus 34:6-7 as our example.

> "Then the Lord passed by in front of him and proclaimed, 'The Lord, the Lord God, compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in lovingkindness and truth; who keeps lovingkindness for thousands, who forgives iniquity, transgression and sin; yet He will by no means leave the guilty unpunished, visiting the iniquity of fathers on the children and on the grandchildren to the third and fourth generations.'"

### (A) What is God saying or doing in this text?

In Exodus 34:6-7, God is revealing Himself to Moses. He is making a verbal declaration of His own character and nature. This is one of the most concentrated self-revelations of God in all of Scripture.

### (B) Why is God saying or doing this?

God reveals Himself to Moses because Moses asked. In Exodus 33:18, Moses made a bold request: "I pray You, show me Your glory!" God's self-revelation in chapter 34 is His gracious response. He chose to answer Moses' prayer by proclaiming His character in unmistakable terms.

### (C) How is God saying or doing this?

God reveals Himself to Moses in two ways. First, He does so in a physical, optical form -- He "passed by in front of him." Moses experienced something visible, tangible, and real. Second, He does so in a verbal, descriptive form -- He "proclaimed" His own name and attributes. God used both sight and speech to communicate His glory.

### (D) Which of God's attributes are on display?

This is where we connect the text to systematic theology. It is helpful to work from an organized list of theological categories -- Bibliology, Theology Proper, Christology, Pneumatology, Angelology, Anthropology, Hamartiology, Soteriology, Ecclesiology, and Eschatology. Asking which of God's attributes are on display helps us see how a given text contributes to our understanding of who God is.

In Exodus 34:6-7, we see that God is compassionate, gracious, slow to anger, abounding in lovingkindness and truth, One who forgives iniquity, transgression, and sin, and yet holy, just, and all-powerful -- One who will by no means leave the guilty unpunished. This single passage reveals a breathtaking portrait of divine character.

What does this teach us about God? It teaches us that God wants to be known. He desires to reveal Himself to His people. It teaches us that God must be related to rightly -- He is holy and just, and He will not ignore sin. And it teaches us that God loves to express mercy, grace, and forgiveness to those who seek Him.

> "If we are faithless, He remains faithful, for He cannot deny Himself." (2 Timothy 2:13)

## (3) How should this text contribute to my theology?

This question aims to understand an individual text as part of a cohesive whole. Here we shift from meaning to significance -- from what a passage says to what it means in the grand scope of God's redemptive story. Let's use Exodus 26:31-33 as our example, which describes the veil that separated the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place in the Tabernacle.

### (A) How does this text advance the metanarrative of Scripture?

> **Metanarrative:** *The overarching story comprised of and connecting smaller stories.*

The veil in the Tabernacle speaks to one of the Bible's central themes: the separation between man and God. This theme stretches all the way back to the Garden of Eden. After Adam and Eve sinned, God "drove the man out; and at the east of the garden of Eden He stationed the cherubim and the flaming sword which turned every direction to guard the way to the tree of life" (Genesis 3:24). And what was embroidered on the veil? Cherubim. The imagery is unmistakable. The veil is a vivid reminder that sin has separated humanity from the presence of God.

### (B) How does this text point to Christ?

The veil symbolizes the problem that Christ came to solve. It represents the barrier between sinful humanity and a holy God. As Paul writes, "the Law has become our tutor to lead us to Christ" (Galatians 3:24). And the New Testament makes the connection between the veil and Christ's work explicit.

Consider what happened at the moment of Christ's death. "And Jesus cried out again with a loud voice, and yielded up His spirit. And behold, the veil of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom" (Matthew 27:50-51). The veil was torn -- not from bottom to top, as if by human hands, but from top to bottom, by the hand of God Himself.

The author of Hebrews makes the theological significance unmistakable. "This hope we have as an anchor of the soul, a hope both sure and steadfast and one which enters within the veil, where Jesus has entered as a forerunner for us" (Hebrews 6:19-20). And again, "Therefore, brethren, since we have confidence to enter the holy place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way which He inaugurated for us through the veil, that is, His flesh, and since we have a great priest over the house of God, let us draw near with a sincere heart in full assurance of faith" (Hebrews 10:19-22). The author concludes, "But we are not of those who shrink back to destruction, but of those who have faith to the preserving of the soul" (Hebrews 10:39).

### (C) What other passages of Scripture clarify and/or illustrate this text?

As we've already demonstrated, the cross-references are abundant. Genesis 3:24 illuminates the cherubim imagery. Matthew 27:50-51 records the veil's tearing. Hebrews 6, 9, and 10 provide the theological commentary. This question encourages us to let Scripture interpret Scripture, building a richer understanding by weaving together related texts.

### (D) Which areas of systematic theology relate to this text?

The major categories of systematic theology are Bibliology, Theology Proper, Christology, Pneumatology, Angelology, Anthropology, Hamartiology, Soteriology, Ecclesiology, and Eschatology. As we consider Exodus 26, several of these come into play.

The veil relates to Theology Proper, as it reveals God's holiness and the conditions under which He dwells among His people. It relates to Anthropology, as it highlights humanity's sinful condition and separation from God. It relates to Angelology, as the cherubim embroidered on the veil recall the guardians of Eden. It relates to Soteriology, as Hebrews 10:20 tells us that Christ opened a "new and living way" through the veil, and Hebrews 9:23-24 explains that the earthly Tabernacle was a copy of heavenly realities. And it relates to Ecclesiology, as the New Testament describes believers themselves as the temple of God (1 Corinthians 3:16-17, 6:19-20, 1 Peter 2:4-8).

## (4) How does that truth apply to me?

This is the question of application. What must we do? How should this text change us? Here, we must be careful. As we discussed in our chapter on hermeneutics, we want to avoid the "Moral/Allegorical" hermeneutic -- the tendency to rush past the meaning of a text and jump straight to personal application. We must reserve our interpretive judgment until the exegetical work is done. Only after we've answered the first three questions are we ready to ask the fourth.

Let's use 1 Corinthians 8:4-13 as our example, where Paul addresses the issue of eating meat sacrificed to idols.

### (A) What is my relationship to the human author?

Paul was a devout Jew, rigorously trained in the Scriptures, who was dramatically converted to faith in Christ. He became the apostle to the Gentiles, planting churches throughout the Roman Empire and writing much of the New Testament. Understanding Paul's background -- his Jewish heritage, his Pharisaic training, and his radical transformation -- helps us appreciate the perspective from which he writes.

### (B) What is my relationship to the original audience?

The original audience was the church in Corinth. Corinth was a diverse, metropolitan city -- the "Las Vegas of its time," so to speak. It was a commercial hub full of cultural diversity, moral permissiveness, and religious pluralism. The Corinthian believers came from this environment and carried many of its influences into the church.

### (C) What are the differences between my world and their world?

In the ancient world, religious culture permeated daily life. There were no grocery stores as we know them. Marketplaces were stocked with meat that had been sacrificed to pagan idols, and eating such meat was a matter of serious concern for early Christians. Was it sinful? Was it acceptable? These were live questions in a way they simply aren't for most of us today.

However, the underlying principle translates easily into our world. We face analogous questions about issues like alcohol, entertainment choices, movies, and other matters of personal freedom. The specifics differ, but the dynamic is the same: how do we exercise our liberty in Christ without causing a brother or sister to stumble?

### (D) Is my relationship to God different than the original audience?

This is a crucial question, and the answer varies from text to text. Sometimes the answer is yes. For example, the original recipients of Jeremiah 29:11 were Old Testament Israelites living under the Mosaic Covenant before the coming of Christ. Our relationship to God is fundamentally different from theirs. But in the case of 1 Corinthians 8, the answer is no. Paul was writing to New Testament believers who share the same spiritual status in Christ that we do. His instructions apply to us directly.

> "One person regards one day above another, another regards every day alike. Each person must be fully convinced in his own mind." (Romans 14:5)

> "Whatever is not from faith is sin." (Romans 14:23)

The application of 1 Corinthians 8 is this: we must be willing to lay down our rights and freedoms to protect the conscience of a weaker brother or sister. At the same time, we must not accept legalism -- the imposition of man-made rules as if they were God's commands.

## Summary

Good questions lead to helpful answers. The four primary questions we've explored in this chapter -- What did this text mean to the human author? What does this text teach me about God? How should this text contribute to my theology? How does that truth apply to me? -- form a comprehensive framework for interpreting any passage of Scripture.

I'd encourage you to keep these questions in your Bible and use them often. You don't need to know Greek or Hebrew. You don't need a seminary degree. You just need curiosity and a willingness to let the text speak for itself. In the chapters ahead, we'll explore some additional tools for Bible study, including outlining and extrabiblical resources.
