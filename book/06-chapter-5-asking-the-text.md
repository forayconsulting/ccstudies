# 5. Asking the Text

At the fourth and final level of the pyramid, we're bringing it all together. We'll leverage hermeneutics and exegesis to actually interpret the Scriptures. Remember the definition:

> **Interpretation:** *A judgment as to the meaning of a text.*

The whole goal of this book is to bridge the gap between beginner Bible study and advanced seminary studies. You don't need to know Greek or get a degree to study your Bible confidently. Hermeneutics and exegesis matter for ordinary Christians. The author wants readers to feel empowered, not overwhelmed. So the remaining chapters get very practical.

One of the best ways to study the Bible is to ask questions of the text. The author keeps a list of questions in his Bible. Four "primary" questions, each with "subordinate" questions:

1. What did this text mean to the human author?
2. What does this text teach me about God?
3. How should this text contribute to my theology?
4. How does that truth apply to me?

Each primary question has subordinate questions. A complete bulleted list is in Appendix A.

## (1) What did this text mean to the human author?

This first question aims to discover authorial intent. Subordinate questions identify relevant context.

### (1A) Who were they?

- Who was the human author? What biographical info do we know?
- Example: Jesus in Matthew 22:41-46, quoting Psalm 116, identifying David as the author to make a point about the Messiah being more than David's son
- We should ask this every time we study the Bible

### (1B) When did they write this?

- Historical context is essential. What was going on historically, politically, culturally?
- Example: Ezekiel 19:1 - "take up a lamentation for the princes of Israel" - the word "lamentation" is *qinah* in Hebrew
- Ezekiel meticulously recorded dates. Cross-referencing with 2 Kings shows Ezekiel wrote this "funeral song" 3-4 years BEFORE the events happened - this changes the tone from mourning to warning

### (1C) Where did they live?

- Geographical context matters
- Example: Psalm 137 - "By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down and wept" - one of the few psalms written in Babylon, verbalizing the anguish of exile

### (1D) What were they addressing?

- The author's purpose in writing
- Example: Jeremiah 29:11 - often taken out of context. Going back one verse (29:10) shows Jeremiah was addressing Israel's despair going into exile, promising rescue after 70 years. This applies to us indirectly - it tells us about God's faithfulness

### (1E) What was in *their* Bible?

- The Bible was written over 1,500 years. God revealed progressively. Each author had a different amount of Scripture available.
- Example: 2 Timothy 4:6 - Paul alludes to Leviticus 23 and Numbers 15 about "drink offering" (libation offering). Priests would pour sweet wine over roasting meat. Paul compares his impending death to a libation offering - a mere garnish on a sacrifice.

## (2) What does this text teach me about God?

This second "primary" question observes how God reveals Himself through His Word. It embraces dual authorship.

Example using Exodus 34:6-7:

> *"Then the LORD passed by in front of him and proclaimed, 'The LORD, the LORD God, compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in lovingkindness and truth; who keeps lovingkindness for thousands, who forgives iniquity, transgression and sin; yet He will by no means leave the guilty unpunished, visiting the iniquity of fathers on the children and on the grandchildren to the third and fourth generations.'"*

### (A) What is God saying or doing in this text?

- God is revealing Himself to Moses, describing Himself, declaring who He is

### (B) Why is God saying or doing this?

- Moses asked God to reveal Himself (Exodus 33:18). So God is revealing Himself because Moses asked.

### (C) How is God saying or doing this?

- Two ways: physically (passing by Moses in the cleft of the rock) and verbally (speaking truth about Himself)

### (D) Which of God's attributes are on display in this text?

- It is helpful to work from an organized list of "attributes of God" from systematic theology
- Compassionate, gracious, slow to anger, abounding in lovingkindness and truth
- Forgives iniquity, transgression, and sin
- Yet will not leave the guilty unpunished
- Will visit the iniquity of the fathers on later generations

Summary answer: God wants to be known accurately. He is holy, just, all-powerful. He loves mercy, grace, forgiveness. He is caring, patient, faithful. "If we are faithless, He remains faithful, for He cannot deny Himself" (2 Timothy 2:13).

## (3) How should this text contribute to my theology?

This question aims to understand an individual text as part of a cohesive whole - moving from meaning to significance.

Example: Exodus 26:31-33:

> *"You shall make a veil of blue and purple and scarlet material and fine twisted linen; it shall be made with cherubim, the work of a skillful workman. You shall hang it on four pillars of acacia overlaid with gold, their hooks also being of gold, on four sockets of silver. You shall hang up the veil under the clasps, and shall bring in the ark of the testimony there within the veil; and the veil shall serve for you as a partition between the holy place and the holy of holies."*

> **Metanarrative:** *the overarching story comprised of and connecting smaller stories.*

### (A) How does this text advance the metanarrative of Scripture?

- Theme of separation between man and God. The veil served as a partition. The decorative use of pomegranates and cherubim recalls the Garden of Eden (Genesis 3:24).

### (B) How does this text point to Christ?

- The veil symbolizes the problem Christ came to solve. Galatians 3:24 - "the law has become our tutor to lead us to Christ"
- Matthew 27:50-51 - when Jesus died, the veil was torn from top to bottom (from God down, not man up)
- Hebrews 6:19-20 - Jesus entered within the veil as forerunner
- Hebrews 10:19-22 - confidence to enter the holy place by the blood of Jesus, through the veil, His flesh

### (C) What other passages of Scripture clarify and/or illustrate this text?

- The passages above (Matthew 27, Hebrews 6, Hebrews 10) all clarify Exodus 26
- Hebrews 10:39 - "We are not of those who shrink back to destruction"

### (D) Which areas of systematic theology relate to this text?

- The categories of systematic theology include: Bibliology, Theology Proper, Christology, Pneumatology, Angelology, Anthropology, Hamartiology, Soteriology, Ecclesiology, Eschatology
- In Exodus 26: Theology Proper and Anthropology (God is holy, men are sinful)
- Angelology (cherubim depictions)
- Soteriology (Christ "inaugurated for us through the veil, that is, His flesh" - Hebrews 10:20; "copies of the things in the heavens" - Hebrews 9:23-24)
- Ecclesiology (1 Corinthians 3:16-17, 6:19-20, 1 Peter 2:4-8 - believers described with Temple imagery)

## (4) How does that truth apply to me?

Application. How should this text change us?

We don't want a "Moral/Allegorical" hermeneutic (see Chapter 3). We must reserve interpretive judgment until after exegesis.

Example: 1 Corinthians 8:4-13 (simplified core arguments about eating meat sacrificed to idols)

### (A) What is my relationship to the human author?

- Paul was a devout Jew converted to Christ. 2,000 years later, we're beneficiaries of his evangelistic efforts.

### (B) What is my relationship to the original audience?

- The church at Corinth was diverse. Corinth was "the Las Vegas of its time." A contemporary American city isn't too different from an ancient Greco-Roman city.

### (C) What are the differences between my world and their world?

- In ancient Corinth, there were no grocery stores - marketplaces were stocked with religious sacrifices. Some Christians saw eating this meat as sin, others didn't. Paul addressed the conflict, urging the high path.
- Today: similar issues arise with things like alcohol, movies, etc.

### (D) Is my relationship to God different than the original audience?

- Sometimes yes (e.g., Jeremiah 29:11 - Old Testament believers were under the law of Moses, not indwelt by the Holy Spirit)
- For 1 Corinthians 8: No - the Corinthian believers were Gentiles who received the gospel, having the same spiritual status as us

Application answer: As believers, we should lay down our rights and freedoms to protect the spiritual well-being of our brothers and sisters. But we must not accept legalism - "each person must be fully convinced in his own mind" and "whatever is not from faith is sin" (Romans 14:5, 23).

## Summary

Good questions lead to helpful answers. You don't need to know Greek or Hebrew or have a degree. All you need is curiosity.

Looking ahead: outlining (literary and grammatical context) and extrabiblical resources.
