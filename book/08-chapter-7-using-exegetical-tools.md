# 7. Using Exegetical Tools

Outlining is a powerful method of studying the Scriptures, but it doesn't give us all the answers. Sometimes we need external resources to fill in the gaps — historical context, biographical information, cultural background, and more. We need tools that help us answer the questions the text raises.

We're blessed in the 21st Century with accessible, affordable resources for Bible study. More tools are available today than at any other time in Church history. But the sheer volume of what's out there can be overwhelming. How do we know what to use, when to use it, and how to use it well? In this chapter, we'll survey the major types of exegetical tools available and demonstrate how to put them into practice.

## Types of Tools

### Study Bibles

Study Bibles are Bibles with limited commentary built into the margins and footnotes. They also include detailed introductory material for each book, covering authorship, date, purpose, outline, and key themes. For quick, concise answers about a book's background, a good study Bible is often the first place to look. Two recommended study Bibles are the *ESV Study Bible* and *The Open Bible*.

### Commentaries

A commentary is an author's interpretation of a biblical text, following its consecutive order. There is great variation among commentaries, and they generally fall into three sub-categories.

**Devotional Commentaries** offer straightforward exposition and application, written for a general audience. They focus on practical takeaways and spiritual enrichment rather than technical analysis. Two recommended devotional commentaries are *Matthew Henry's Commentary on the Whole Bible* and *The NIV Application Commentary*.

**Exegetical Commentaries** provide detailed explanation and exploration of the text. They assume familiarity with Greek and Hebrew and are written for an academic audience. Two recommended exegetical commentary series are *The New International Commentary* series and *The Word Biblical Commentary* series.

**Hybrid Commentaries** offer a mix of both devotional and exegetical approaches, balancing accessibility with scholarly depth. Two recommended hybrid commentaries are *The Expositor's Bible Commentary* and *The Tyndale Old and New Testament Commentaries*.

All three sub-categories play a role in a well-balanced approach to Bible study. Many commentaries are available for free or at low cost online. Appendix B includes links to several of these resources.

### Bible Dictionaries

Bible dictionaries are collections of articles on names, places, texts, and topics found in the Bible. Like an encyclopedia, they are alphabetically organized and typically include bibliographies for further research. Two recommended Bible dictionaries are *The Holman Illustrated Bible Dictionary* and *The Anchor Yale Bible Dictionary*.

### Concordances

Concordances list usages of specific words throughout the Bible. They are organized alphabetically, allowing you to quickly locate every occurrence of a particular word in Scripture. Two recommended concordances are *Strong's Exhaustive Concordance of the Bible* and *Young's Analytical Concordance to the Bible*.

### Lexicons

A lexicon is a dictionary of terms in the biblical languages — Greek, Hebrew, and Aramaic. Lexicons are organized in the alphabet of the target language. "Abridged" lexicons provide simple glosses, giving the reader a basic sense of a word's meaning without extensive discussion.

For Greek, a recommended lexicon is *Abbott-Smith's Manual Greek Lexicon of the New Testament*. For Hebrew, a recommended lexicon is *The Brown-Driver-Briggs Hebrew-English Lexicon of the Old Testament*. More expensive and comprehensive alternatives include BDAG (for Greek) and HALOT (for Hebrew).

A word of caution about lexicons: don't build your interpretation on a lexicon alone. Just because a word *can* mean something doesn't mean it *does* mean that in a given passage. There's a well-known story about the Greek word *baptizo* being used to argue that baptism means "to pickle" — since the word was used in ancient recipes for pickling vegetables. But context is king. A lexicon tells you the range of meaning a word carries; the context of the passage tells you which meaning the author intended.

### Cross Referencing Tools

Cross referencing tools — sometimes called "harmonies," "synopses," or simply "cross-references" — connect passages to other passages throughout Scripture. They help the reader trace themes, ideas, and language across the Bible. Two recommended cross referencing tools are *The Treasury of Scripture Knowledge* and *The Thompson Chain Reference Bible*.

### Other Types of Tools

Beyond these primary categories, there are several other types of resources worth knowing about: Systematic Theologies, Historical Surveys, Grammars, and Bible Atlases. Each of these serves a distinct purpose and can enrich your study in different ways.

You can access exegetical tools in three ways: hard copy (printed books), digital (software and apps), or internet archive (free resources through sites like Archive.org and Google Books).

## Using Tools

Having the right tools is one thing; using them well is another. There are three areas of intentionality that will help you get the most out of your exegetical resources.

First, **normal reading comprehension**. Before diving into commentaries and lexicons, make sure your basic English grammar skills are sharp. Much of Bible study begins with carefully reading what the text actually says. Refreshing your understanding of grammar, syntax, and literary devices will pay dividends in your study.

Second, **organized note-taking**. Keep lists of questions, answers, observations, and intuitive interpretations as you study. Write things down. Whether you prefer writing in the margins, using Sticky Notes, digital tools, or something like the ESV Scripture Journals, the key is to capture your thoughts as they come so you can revisit and refine them later.

Third, **interpretive freedom**. Don't simply "fall in line" with someone else's interpretation because they wrote a commentary or hold an advanced degree. Use their conclusions to inform and sharpen your own thinking. Ultimately, the Scriptures must make sense to you. Study with integrity, and let the text speak for itself.

## Exegetical Examples

To see these tools in action, let's return to Philippians 3, the passage we outlined in the previous chapter. Here are three examples of how exegetical tools can enhance our understanding.

**Example 1: When and where did Paul write Philippians?**

Turning to *The Open Bible*, the introductory material for Philippians tells us that Paul first came to Philippi around A.D. 51 and wrote this letter approximately eleven years later, from Rome. The *ESV Study Bible* confirms this, noting that Philippians was written from Rome, circa A.D. 62.

Both sources align. Paul wrote to the Philippians from prison, around A.D. 62. This background contextualizes passages like Philippians 3:12-21, where Paul urges perseverance in the Lord. He isn't speaking from a place of comfort; he's modeling perseverance from a Roman prison cell. The exhortation carries greater weight when we understand the circumstances of the author.

**Example 2: Does Philippians 3:15 say "perfect" or "mature"?**

Depending on which translation you use, Philippians 3:15 reads differently. The NASB says "perfect," while the ESV says "mature." Why the difference?

The commentary by Jamieson, Fausset, and Brown clarifies this. The word translated "perfect" means "full grown" in the Christian life — fit for running the race. It does not refer to moral perfection. The NASB itself includes a footnote that reads "Mature," confirming this understanding. By comparing the commentary with the translation's own footnote, we can clarify Paul's intent: he is speaking of spiritual maturity, not sinless perfection. This distinction is important because it avoids the misinterpretation that Christians can achieve moral perfection in this life.

**Example 3: What does "citizenship in heaven" mean in Philippians 3:20?**

Turning to *The Treasury of Scripture Knowledge*, the entry for Philippians 3:20 presents a dense wall of cross-references organized by keyword. Among these is a reference to Ephesians 2:18-20, which describes believers as "fellow citizens with the saints, of God's household."

This cross-reference enriches our understanding of Paul's language. Our heavenly citizenship is "through" Jesus Christ. It binds believers together as members of one household. And the foundation of that household is the apostles and prophets, with Christ Himself as the cornerstone. A single cross-reference tool opened up an entire network of related passages that deepen our grasp of what Paul means by "citizenship in heaven."

## Summary

Exegetical tools are invaluable companions in Bible study, but they must be used with both curiosity and intellectual honesty. Don't lean on them so heavily that you stop thinking for yourself, and don't study in a vacuum, ignoring the insights of those who have gone before you.

Be like the Bereans in Acts 17:11, who "received the word with great eagerness, examining the Scriptures daily to see whether these things were so." They didn't reject what they heard, and they didn't accept it uncritically. They examined it in light of Scripture. That's the posture we should bring to every tool, commentary, and resource we use.
