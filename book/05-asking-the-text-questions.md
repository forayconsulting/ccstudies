# 5. Asking the Text Questions

[The Pyramid of Interpretation: from bottom to top — The Doctrine of Inspiration → Hermeneutics → Exegesis → Interpretation]

At the fourth and final level of our pyramid, we're bringing it all together. We'll leverage what we've learned about the principles of hermeneutics and the process of exegesis to help us actually interpret the Scriptures. Remember our definition of **Interpretation:** *A judgment as to the meaning of a text.*

The whole goal of this book is to bridge the gap between beginner Bible study techniques and advanced seminary studies. You don't need to know Greek or get a degree to study your Bible confidently. And I firmly believe that hermeneutics and exegesis matter for ordinary Christians. I want you to feel empowered, not overwhelmed. So, in the remaining chapters, we're going to get very practical.

In my experience, one of the best ways to study the Bible is to *ask questions of the text*. I keep a list of questions in my Bible. I nearly always use them when exploring a passage. These questions help me press into the right areas and avoid focusing on topics that aren't the passage's main point.

I ask four "primary" questions, which are listed below.

1. What did this text mean to the human author?
2. What does this text teach me about God?
3. How should this text contribute to my theology?
4. How does that truth apply to me?

Each of these primary questions contains several "subordinate" questions. We'll walk through each of these in detail, including biblical examples for each. The complete, bulleted list is available for your convenience in Appendix A.

## (1) What did this text mean to the human author?

This first question aims to discover the authorial intent of the passage. Under this heading, each subordinate question helps us identify relevant context related to the human author. As we look at these questions, notice how correctly identifying contextual details can cast a passage in an entirely new light.

### (1A) Who were they?

Who was the human author of this text? What biographical information do we know about them?

Jesus Himself modeled the importance of this question in Matthew 22:41-46. *"Now while the Pharisees were gathered together, Jesus asked them a question: 'What do you think about the Christ, whose son is He?' They said to Him, 'The son of David.' He said to them, 'Then how does David in the Spirit call Him "Lord," saying, "The Lord said to my Lord, sit at My right hand, until I put your enemies beneath your feet?" If David calls Him "Lord," how is He His son? No one was able to answer Him a word, nor did anyone dare from that day on to ask Him another question."*

Jesus made His point by correctly identifying the author of Psalm 116.

In this psalm, the author refers to the Messiah as his "Lord." This designation is a sign of submission. If someone is your "Lord," that means you're their servant! By correctly identifying the author, Jesus proved that David -- the King of Israel and patriarch of the Messianic line - viewed this promised savior as his superior. The Christ is more than the Son of Adam, Abraham, and David; He is also the Son of God.

Jesus made this powerful argument on a single interpretive observation: that the author of Psalm 116 is David. We should be asking this question every time we study the Bible. Who is the human author of this text? What do we know about them? And how does that impact the meaning and overall significance of the text?

(It's important to mention: we don't always have full answers to these questions! For example, ask ten biblical scholars, "who's the author of Hebrews?" You'll get ten different answers. At the end of the day, it's all speculation; we just don't know. However, we can still infer meaningful biographical information about them from their writings, even if it is somewhat vague.)

### (1B) When did they write this?

The Bible is ancient literature, and understanding its historical context is essential. At what point in history was this passage written? What was going on historically, politically, and culturally?

In Ezekiel 19:1, God tells Ezekiel to *"take up a lamentation for the princes of Israel."* This word "lamentation" is *qinah* in Hebrew -- a funeral song, a lament, a dirge -- evoking the same sad lyrical qualities as Jeremiah's "Book of Lamentations" written in the same period. In chapter 19, Ezekiel pens a mournful dirge for two princes of Judah: Jehoahaz II and his brother Zedekiah. We know from 2 Kings and 2 Chronicles that both suffered an awful end. Three months into his reign, the Egyptian Pharaoh captured Jehoahaz and exiled him permanently. While Zedekiah survived slightly longer, we read in 2 Kings 25:7 about his terrible fate at the hands of Nebuchadnezzar. *"They slaughtered the sons of Zedekiah before his eyes, then put out the eyes of Zedekiah and bound him with bronze fetters and brought him to Babylon."* In verse 14, Ezekiel's funeral song ends with a reiterative, resigned sigh, *"This is a lamentation, and has become a lamentation."*

But there's more to uncover here. You see, Ezekiel meticulously recorded the year and date of every prophecy he proclaimed. Each chronological section of the book starts with a detailed timestamp. Thanks to this structure, we know Ezekiel wrote chapter 19 sometime between "the sixth year" and "the seventh year" of his own exile in Babylon. Now, we also have access to the dates and timelines mentioned in 2 Kings. If we cross-reference Ezekiel 1:2, 8:1, and 20:1 with 2 Kings 24:15-18 and 25:2, we come to a surprising realization. Ezekiel wrote this "funeral song" 3-4 years *before* Nebuchadnezzar humiliated and exiled Zedekiah. The lament in chapter 19 wasn't looking back on past events; it was anticipating future events!

That changes the tone of the chapter quite dramatically, doesn't it? It's one thing to give a eulogy after someone has died. But it's an entirely different thing to start singing songs about someone who's still alive as if they're already dead! Suddenly, this passage becomes less about mourning and more about warning. God is warning the exiles not to put their hope in political deliverance. Kings will fall, and princes will pass away. God is their only hope now.

If we didn't do the hard work of correctly identifying the time of writing, we would miss this crucial nuance. We should ask this question whenever we study a text. When did the author write this? What was going on in that period?

### (1C) Where did they live?

The Bible is full of diverse places, nations, and people groups. When we study a text, we should dig into these areas. What geographical context informed this passage? Where was the author from originally? Where did they live?

Consider Psalm 137. *"(1) By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down and wept, when remembered Zion... (3) For there our captors demanded of us songs, and our tormentors mirth, saying, 'Sing us one of the songs of Zion.' How can we sing the Lord's song in a foreign land? If I forget you, O Jerusalem, may my right hand forget its skill."*

This is one of the few psalms (perhaps the only one) written in Babylon. The Israelites are an oppressed minority. We've already learned about Israel's defeat, exile, and struggle for identity. This psalm verbalizes the anguish of genocide, the shame of national defeat, the loss of a culture, and the pain of seeing Temple worship eradicated. We won't see these themes unless we consider the geographical context of the author.

### (1D) What were they addressing?

This question aims to discover the author's purpose in writing. What topics was the author commenting on? What events were they reacting to? What circumstances led them to write this text? I'll give you a straightforward example.

Jeremiah 29:11 says, *"'For I know the plans that I have for you,' declares the Lord, 'plans for welfare and not for calamity to give you a future and a hope.'"*

This verse is often taken out of context. But if we simply go back one verse, we learn what Jeremiah was addressing. *"(10) For thus says the Lord, 'When seventy years have been completed for Babylon, I will visit you and fulfill My good word to you, to bring you back to this place.'"* Jeremiah was addressing Israel's despair as they went into exile. He delivered a specific promise from the Lord: this will not be forever -- after seventy years have passed, I will rescue you from Babylon and bring you back into Israel.

This verse does indeed apply to us today -- it just applies to us indirectly. We're not ancient Israelites, we're not Babylonian slaves, and God hasn't promised us freedom after seventy years. These particular promises weren't given to us. But this verse does tell us something about God: it tells us that God is faithful to keep His promises. And there certainly are plenty of promises that we have received as New Testament believers. Jeremiah 29:11 is one of many examples proving God's faithfulness to His people. We worship the same God that brought Israel out of Egypt and Babylon. We can trust His promises.

### (1E) What was in *their* Bible?

We must remember that the Bible was written over a period of 1,500 years. God revealed His Word progressively. Until the Apostle John penned the final "Amen" in Revelation 22:21, no single author had access to the complete canon of Scripture. John had "more Bible" than James did, Paul had more than Isaiah, who had more than David, and so on.

We must consider a passage's placement in the timeline of progressive revelation. How much of the Bible did the author have? Which other books existed at that point? What Scriptures could they quote, allude to, or reference?

For example, let's look at 2 Timothy 4:6. *"For I am already being poured out as a drink offering, and the time of my departure has come."*

In this verse, Paul alludes to Leviticus 23 and Numbers 15, which describe the "libation offering" or "drink offering." While presenting a burnt sacrifice, priests would pour sweet wine over the roasting meat to enhance the aroma. The wine was valuable and time-consuming to produce, so pouring it out before the Lord was a way to honor Him sacrificially.

When Paul wanted to describe his final days, he looked to his Bible for illustrative imagery. And his Bible was the Old Testament. (He probably also had access to the books of James, Mark, and Luke, in addition to his own epistles.) At the end of it all, he compares his life to a libation offering. His impending death reminded him of sweet wine poured out upon a sacrifice -- a mere garnish.

## (2) What does this text teach me about God?

This second "primary" question observes how God reveals Himself through His Word. This question embraces the dual authorship of Scripture. How has God communicated Himself in this text? What do we learn about Him?

This time, let's stick with a single passage. Once we've run through our subordinate questions, we'll summarize our findings. Exodus 34:6-7 is laden with beautiful theology; let's see what it teaches us about God.

> *"Then the Lord passed by in front of [Moses] and proclaimed, 'The Lord, the Lord God, compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in lovingkindness and truth; who keeps lovingkindness for thousands, who forgives iniquity, transgression, and sin; yet He will by no means leave the guilty unpunished, visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children and on the grandchildren to the third and fourth generations."*

### (A) What is God saying or doing in this text?

This question aims to identify God's action or communication. What's God up to in this text? That's not always easy to answer. Sometimes God isn't directly mentioned in a passage, so you may need to think about it for a while. But in Exodus 34:6-7, this question has a straightforward answer.

God is revealing Himself to Moses. He's describing Himself, declaring who He is. After calling Himself *"The Lord, the Lord God,"* He goes on to list His own attributes.

### (B) Why is God saying or doing this?

This question aims to identify God's purpose in doing what He's doing. What is His motivation in this text? What prompted this action or communication? Of course, there's always an ultimate answer for why God does anything: for the sake of His own glory! But this question is looking for intermediate purposes. Are there any specific reasons listed? Has God revealed His particular motivations for us to study?

In this case, we have a relatively straightforward answer. A few verses back, Moses asked God to reveal Himself. According to Exodus 33:18, *"Moses said, 'I pray You, show me Your glory!"* So, why is God revealing Himself to Moses? Because Moses asked.

### (C) How is God saying or doing this?

This question aims to identify God's means. Is God acting through providential or miraculous means? Is He communicating through natural or special revelation? Is He speaking directly, or through a dream, an angel, or a prophet?

In Exodus 34:6-7, God is revealing Himself in two ways. First, God is revealing Himself in physical (or at least optical) form. God hides Moses in the cleft of the rock and passes by, somehow allowing Moses to actually see Him. And second, God is revealing Himself in verbal, descriptive form. God speaks truth about Himself, putting words to vision, declaring His nature openly.

### (D) Which of God's attributes are on display in this text?

This question aims to identify God's character and nature as it appears in the text. What does this text tell us about who -- and *what* -- God is? When answering this question, it may be helpful to work from an organized list of "the attributes of God." You can usually find these in a systematic theology book or similar resource (more on this later.)

In Exodus 34:6-7, we learn that God is caring and sympathetic, for He calls Himself *"compassionate."*

We learn that God is generous and benevolent, for He calls Himself *"gracious."*

We learn that God is patient with weakness, for He says He is *"slow to anger."*

And we learn that God is *"abounding in"* (overflowing with) two things.

First, He is overflowing with faithfulness to His people, for He says He is *"abounding in lovingkindness."*

And second, He is overflowing with faithfulness to Himself, for He says He is *"abounding in truth."*

We learn that God desires and delights in forgiveness, for He declares Himself a God who *"forgives iniquity, transgression, and sin."*

But at the same time, we learn that God is holy, just, and all-powerful, for He says that He *"will by no means leave the guilty unpunished."*

And we learn that God stands in relationship to the entire human race, for He says that He *"will visit the iniquity of the fathers"* on later generations.

What a theologically rich passage! By asking these subordinate questions, we've identified the actions, purpose, means, and character of God revealed in Exodus 34:6-7. Now we can revisit the primary question and summarize our findings.

So, **what does this text teach me about God?**

This text teaches me that God wants to be known -- and known accurately. God is revealing Himself visually and describing Himself verbally. He is disclosing Himself to Moses in unique and precise ways.

This text teaches me that God must be related to rightly -- and that sin prevents that. God reveals Himself to be holy, just, and all-powerful. He defines His relationship to individuals and indeed the entire human race in terms of sin and righteousness.

And this text teaches me that God loves to express mercy, grace, and forgiveness. He is caring, patient, and faithful to fulfill His promises. He doesn't discard His people when they fail; He's loyal to them because He's faithful to Himself and His promises. As the Apostle Paul put it many millennia later, *"If we are faithless, He remains faithful, for He cannot deny Himself"* (2 Timothy 2:13).

What a powerful expression of the gospel! By asking the right questions, we can extract meaningful theological truth from the text. As we study the Scriptures, we learn about who God is -- the greatest of all knowledge -- and this prompts us to love and worship Him more.

## (3) How should this text contribute to my theology?

This question aims to understand an individual text as part of a cohesive whole. We're now shifting from discovering the meaning of a text (its authorial intent) to identifying its significance (its broader theological implications). Both are necessary for interpretation.

Let's look at another Old Testament example, Exodus 26:31-33.

> *"You shall make a veil of blue and purple and scarlet material and fine twisted linen; it shall be made with cherubim, the work of a skillful workman. You shall hang it on four pillars of acacia overlaid with gold, their hooks also being of gold, on four sockets of silver. You shall hang up the veil under the clasps, and shall bring in the ark of the testimony there within the veil; and the veil shall serve for you as a partition between the holy place and the holy of holies."*

### (A) How does this text advance the metanarrative of Scripture?

> **Metanarrative** -- *the overarching story comprised of and connecting smaller stories.*

If you've read or watched the Harry Potter series, you're already familiar with this concept. Each story in the series is mostly self-contained. But each consecutive entry develops the overarching story. Themes, settings, and characters reappear and intersect in interesting ways throughout the series. The lore and plot expand into something more significant and exciting than any single entry by itself. That overarching story is the series' metanarrative.

The Bible works similarly. Each book of the Bible has its own context, characters, themes, genre, and sub-plot. But God's telling a story that's bigger than any single book on its own. From Moses to John, each consecutive revelation develops this overarching story: the story of redemption. This question aims to understand how any given text develops that story.

So how does Exodus 26:31-33 advance the metanarrative of Scripture?

Well, it picks up on the theme of separation between man and God. *"The veil shall serve for you as a partition between the holy place and the holy of holies."* It served a very functional purpose: preventing human priests from looking upon The Presence. But it had a symbolic purpose, too.

The veil was one of many artistic works adorning the Tabernacle, and the imagery crafted into those pieces was significant. In a space occupied by God's Presence, the decorative use of pomegranates and cherubim was no accident -- it reminds us of the Garden of Eden. Do you remember the state in which God left the Garden? After banishing Adam and Eve for eating the forbidden fruit, *"He stationed the cherubim and the flaming sword which turned every direction to guard the way to the tree of life"* (Genesis 3:24).

The imagery of the veil was intentional. It reminds us of what prevents humanity from enjoying perfect communion with God. The Tabernacle was "theological architecture." The structure, furniture, decorations, and materials communicated something specific about God. And in this case, Exodus 26:31-33 advances the metanarrative of Scripture by developing the theme of separation between man and God.

### (B) How does this text point to Christ?

The veil symbolizes the problem Christ came to solve: humans cannot approach God. And it was God Himself who commissioned the veil with its meaningful imagery, and indeed the whole sacrificial system that directs us to Christ. In Galatians 3:4, Paul says that *"the law has become our tutor to lead us to Christ, so that we may be justified by faith."* The veil illustrates the problem, and the sacrifices demonstrate the solution.

### (C) What other passages of Scripture clarify and/or illustrate this text?

Think about Matthew 27:50-51. *"And Jesus cried out with a loud voice, and yielded up His spirit. And behold, the veil of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom; and the earth shook and the rocks were split."*

As Jesus hung on the cross, as the metanarrative of Scripture reached its heart-wrenching climax, the narrator (Matthew) takes us across Jerusalem, into the Temple, to look at the torn veil. Think about that! When Christ gave His life as a perfect sacrifice for us, the curse of Eden was lifted at that very moment. The veil representing the separation between God and man was supernaturally torn in two. It wasn't torn from the bottom up (as if from man to God), but from the top down - salvation wrought by God on man's behalf.

And consider Hebrews 6:19-20. *"This hope we have as an anchor of the soul, a hope both sure and steadfast and one which enters within the veil, where Jesus has entered as a forerunner for us, having become a high priest forever according to the order of Melchizedek."*

This verse ties the hope of our salvation to the theology of the veil. We only have communion with God because Jesus entered into the holy of holies before us. Through His sacrificial death and righteous resurrection, Jesus has become to us a sure and steadfast hope. We can trust that God will not refuse us from His presence because Jesus has gone before us and invited us into fellowship with Him.

And what about Hebrews 10:19-22? *"Therefore, brethren, since we have confidence to enter the holy place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way which He inaugurated for us through the veil, that is, His flesh, and since we have a great priest over the house of God, let us draw near with a sincere heart in full assurance of faith, having our hearts sprinkled clean from an evil conscience and our bodies washed with pure water."*

Our hope in Jesus produces confidence before God. We don't need to be afraid of God! Jesus has made us friends of God, confident in our relationship with Him, and servants of God confident in our ministry to Him. *"We are not of those who shrink back to destruction, but of those who have faith to the preserving of the soul"* (Hebrews 10:39).

This obscure passage about fabric in Exodus 26 leads us to the highest, most glorious truths of the gospel. By asking what other passages of Scripture clarify or illustrate this text, we break into a vibrant world of significance. We are enlivened in our faith by the simplest texts when we subject them to the whole counsel of God.

### (D) Which areas of systematic theology relate to this text?

If you've ever read a systematic theology book (for example, Wayne Grudem's *Systematic Theology*), you'll recognize various categories of theology typically represented in such works. These vary somewhat from author to author, but they generally look something like this:

- Bibliology (the study of the Bible)
- Theology Proper (the study of God)
- Christology (the study of Christ)
- Pneumatology (the study of the Spirit)
- Angelology (the study of angels)
- Anthropology (the study of man)
- Hamartiology (the study of sin)
- Soteriology (the study of salvation)
- Ecclesiology (the study of the Church)
- Eschatology (the study of the end)

This question seeks to identify which areas of study are related to the passage at hand. Answering this helps ensure that our interpretation of one individual passage is cohesive with the whole system of thought proposed by God's Word.

So, in Exodus 26, which areas of systematic theology relate to this text?

First of all, we see *Theology Proper* and *Anthropology*. It is because God is holy and men are sinful that the veil is necessary. It will probably prove insightful to study the Bible's theology of God and man systematically.

We also find *Angelology* in the decorative depictions of the cherubim. What exactly are cherubim? Why are they associated with the Garden, the Temple, and the Throne of God? Since the veil included depictions of them, what did they look like?

We see a connection to *Soteriology*, as well. One cross-reference refers to our salvation as something that Christ *"inaugurated for us through the veil, that is, His flesh"* (Hebrews 10:20). Why does Hebrews equate the veil to Jesus' flesh? Another cross-reference tells us that Old Testament Temple decorations were simply *"copies of the things in the heavens,"* and that *"Christ did not enter a holy place made with hands, a mere copy of the true one, but into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us"* (Hebrews 9:23-24). In what way is the veil a copy of something in heaven? What exactly did Christ enter, and when? And how is this connected to our salvation?

We also see a hint of *Ecclesiology* since several New Testament passages describe the Church and believers using imagery from the Tabernacle and Temple (1 Cor 3:16-17, 6:19-20, 1 Pet 2:4-8). How has worship changed under the New Covenant? What has stayed the same? If Christ reconciled God and man, is there any ongoing significance to the "Holy of Holies" concept in Christian worship today?

There are probably more connections to be found, but those struck me as the obvious ones. By asking these questions, we gain access to a vast system of associated thoughts, frameworks, and theology. As we seek to interpret the Scriptures, we must not neglect their broader theological significance.

## (4) How does that truth apply to me?

Last but not least, we come to application. Our first two "primary" questions deal with the authorial intent of the text, the third seeks to understand its theological significance, and this fourth question asks how these truths relate to us today. What must we do? How should this text change us?

We don't want to approach the text with a "Moral/Allegorical" hermeneutic (see Chapter 3), an application-driven reading of the text. We want to reserve our interpretive judgment until we've done the hard work of exegesis, only then deriving an application that aligns with a literal historical grammatical hermeneutic. Therefore, this final set of questions aim to understand the differences between our context and the original author's context.

Let's look at a New Testament example, 1 Corinthians 8:4-13. (I encourage you to read it all, but due to its length, I've simplified it to core arguments here.) *"(4) Therefore concerning the eating of things sacrificed to idols, we know that there is no such thing as an idol in the world, and that there is no God but one... (7) However not all men have this knowledge; but some, being accustomed to the idol until now, eat food as if it were sacrificed to an idol; and their conscience being weak is defiled... (13) Therefore, if food causes my brother to stumble, I will never eat meat again, so that I will not cause my brother to stumble."*

This text addresses a situation that's pretty foreign to most of us. The issue of eating meat slaughtered in a pagan ritual is not one we often encounter in 21st Century America. But these questions will help us understand how to apply this text appropriately in our own lives.

### (A) What is my relationship to the human author?

Paul was a devout Jew who converted to Christ. By the time he wrote this letter, he had dedicated his whole life to reaching Gentiles with the gospel. Even though I'm living 2,000 years later, I'm a Gentile believer myself. In this way, I'm a beneficiary of Paul's evangelistic efforts. You could say that Paul is my great-grandfather in the faith.

### (B) What is my relationship to the original audience?

The church at Corinth was a diverse group of Christians of different genders, races, and socio-economic statuses, all struggling to live out their faith in a big city well-known for immorality. Corinth was the Las Vegas of its time. Even though I live 5,000 miles away from Corinth, I live in a relatively metropolitan environment myself. A contemporary American city really isn't too different from an ancient Greco-Roman city; both are centers of culture, politics, business, and entertainment. And both contexts share a common cultural idol: sexuality. The spiritual struggles I encounter today are comparatively similar to those faced by the original audience of 1 Corinthians.

### (C) What are the differences between my world and their world?

In 21st Century America, we can scarcely fathom just how religious everything was in ancient Corinth. Perhaps some of our Southern states feature the Ten Commandments in a prominent public display. But that infusion of religion and society pales in comparison to the ancient world. Shrines, temples, and idols lined the streets and filled people's homes; every element of society connected to the pantheon somehow.

For example, there were no grocery stores in ancient Corinth. If you wanted to buy meat, you shopped in marketplaces stocked with religious sacrifices. Local priests and priestesses would have slaughtered everything on display in pagan ceremonies for one god or another. This reality posed an interesting dilemma for Christians who lived in Corinth.

Some Christians saw it as a sin to eat meat sacrificed to pagan gods, while others thought it was okay. In this letter, Paul wasn't so much concerned with the rightness or wrongness of buying meat at the Corinthian market. Instead, he was addressing the conflict that arose between Christians over this issue. He urged Christians to take the high path: don't cause your brother to stumble, even if they're misinformed.

Now, this exact issue doesn't exist in most contemporary Christian circles. (At least, not in the West.) However, today, there are plenty of cultural practices that some Christians see as sinful while others don't. Is it okay for Christians to drink alcohol? What movies are appropriate for a Christian to watch? These sorts of topics begin to approach the realm of application for 1 Corinthians 8.

### (D) Is my relationship to God different than the original audience?

In some cases, you might well answer this "Yes!"

For example, think back to Jeremiah 29:11. In that case, the original audience lived under the law of Moses. They expressed faithfulness to God and trust in the Messiah through the sacrificial system. And as Old Testament believers, they were not indwelt by the Holy Spirit. When you think about it, their relationship with God was quite different from mine! Imagine how difficult it must have been to see the Temple demolished by Babylonian invaders. That would be a hopeless situation. The invaders had destroyed not only their home but also their only means of worshipping God. And how encouraging and hopeful it must have been to hear God say, *"I have plans to give you a future and a hope!"*

However, returning to 1 Corinthians 8, we must answer "No!" The Corinthians believers were not under the law of Moses. They were Gentiles who had received the gospel of Jesus Christ. Our relationship with God is no different than theirs. We enjoy the same spiritual status in Christ, and God desires the same expressions of worship from us both.

So, **how does this truth apply to me?** Using our answers from each of these four subordinate questions, we can answer the primary question confidently.

As a believer, I should be ready to lay down my rights and freedoms to protect the spiritual well-being of my brothers and sisters in Christ. I should care more about protecting my brother's conscience than expressing my own liberty unhindered. Depending on the cultural issue, that might mean abstaining from alcohol in certain groups, times, or locations. That might mean picking movies that everyone can enjoy together.

Now, that doesn't mean that I should accept legalism. Because Christ is my righteousness, my actions can't win any more of God's grace or incur any more of God's judgment. I'm not saved by my rightness or wrongness on cultural issues; I'm saved by faith in Jesus Christ. And that goes for my brothers and sisters, too. So, when it comes to hotly-debated cultural habits, *"each person must be fully convinced in his own mind"* because *"whatever is not from faith is sin"* (Romans 14:5, 23).

These are simple, straightforward applications of 1 Corinthians 8. We derive these applications from a commonsense reading of Scripture, and they're not difficult to discover! By asking thoughtful questions, we can confidently and accurately apply the Scriptures to our lives.

## Summary

What did this text mean to the human author? What does this text teach me about God? How should this text contribute to my theology? And how does that truth apply to me? Good questions lead to helpful answers.

I keep these questions in my Bible and use them often. And I encourage you to use them, too. You don't need to know Greek or Hebrew or have a degree in biblical studies to answer these questions. All you need is a little curiosity.

But you might be wondering: *How do I actually learn about the literary, historical, and grammatical context of the Bible? It's one thing for you to spout off facts about the Babylonian exile, but what if I don't know anything about that period? What if, like the Ethiopian eunuch, I'm asking a question that I can't answer without help?*

In these next two chapters, I'll teach you how to find those answers for yourself.

First, we'll learn one of the most crucial skills for Bible study: outlining. This practice will help us wrap our minds around the literary and grammatical context of a passage.

And second, we'll learn how to use extrabiblical resources. What's the difference between a lexicon and a Bible dictionary? How should we use commentaries? And how can we confidently research the Bible's historical context without getting a Ph.D.?
