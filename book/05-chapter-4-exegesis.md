# 4. Exegesis

As we advance to the next level of our pyramid, remember our definition of Exegesis: *The process used to determine the interpretation of a text.* We are now moving from the principles of Bible study into the process of Bible study. And as we dive in, we'll find that our view of hermeneutics dictates our exegetical procedures.

The word "exegesis" is an English transliteration of the Greek term *exegeomai*. It means "to lead out, to set forth in great detail, to expound," referring to the process of finding meaning in a text. Inspiration is how God put meaning into the text, and hermeneutics are our principles for pinpointing that meaning. Exegesis, then, is the actual act of extracting that meaning from a given text.

In this chapter, I'll take a slightly different approach. This chapter will be more "show" than "tell." I want to exegete a passage about exegesis. (Meta, right?) We'll not only learn from the text itself, but we'll also learn from our treatment of the text. And that text is Nehemiah 8.

> "(1) And all the people gathered as one man at the square which was in front of the Water Gate, and they asked Ezra the scribe to bring the book of the law of Moses which the Lord had given to Israel. Then Ezra the priest brought the law before all the assembly of men, women, and all who could listen with understanding, on the first day of the seventh month... (7) Also Jeshua, Bani, Sherebiah, Jamin, Akkub, Shabbethai, Hodiah, Maaseiah, Kelita, Azariah, Jozabad, Hanan, Pelaiah, the Levites, explained the law to the people while the people remained in their place. They read from the book, from the law of God, translating to give the sense so that they understood the reading."

## Context

This passage is laden with context. And without a reasonable understanding of that context, it can be challenging to find anything meaningful here. So let's discuss it.

The Israelites had just returned from seventy years of captivity. A lot can change in seventy years! Babylon had enslaved Israel, and then Persia had conquered Babylon. These waves of pagan influence had no small effect on the Israelite minority. Their captivity forced them to adapt to their new circumstances in several ways.

When the invaders destroyed Solomon's Temple, the core elements of Jewish worship came to a screeching halt, including the priestly and sacrificial systems. Instead of Hebrew, many Jews began to speak Aramaic, the common tongue in their new home. The religious leaders faced a dilemma. How could they remain faithful in a foreign land without their central place of worship? They began meeting in small community forums instead of a large assembly. In each location, elders would read the Scriptures and expound upon them. These local meetings eventually became known as synagogues.

Once the Jews returned to their land, they were reluctant to resettle the long-abandoned ruins of Jerusalem. The city was unsafe. Surrounding nations had already filled the power vacuum that arose in Israel's absence, and other people groups had seized their old towns and farmlands. Even with Persia's blessing, they became the object of constant scorn, suspicion, and espionage. Their struggle to maintain a Hebrew identity did not ease upon their return.

But God raised up two men who led Israel into a period of revival. The first was Nehemiah, an able statesman and organizer. Serving under king Cyrus of Persia, Nehemiah was appointed governor of the newly-formed vassal state of Israel. He organized the rebuilding of Jerusalem, introduced labor reforms, and guarded against political treachery from jealous neighbors. The second man was Ezra.

Ezra was a priest and a scribe. A priest was someone who served in the Temple, presiding over the sacrifices and maintaining the instruments of worship. But as I mentioned, there was no Temple at this time. So, in keeping with the synagogue model of the period, Ezra was also a scribe. A scribe was someone who studied the Scriptures for a living. A scribe was a scholar, a teacher, someone intimately familiar with the Hebrew language and every detail of the law. In a time when the Israelites had grown disconnected from their religious roots, God used Ezra's expert knowledge of the Scriptures to lead the people back to Him.

That's the context leading up to Nehemiah 8.

Does that seem like an unnecessary amount of information? It isn't! I've done this on purpose to demonstrate the importance of context. You see, it will be difficult to grasp the meaning of Nehemiah 8 without an understanding of Israel's captivity, return, and struggle for identity. With all this context in mind, let's read it once again. Notice how a little context brings the story to life.

## Explanation

> "(1) And all the people gathered as one man at the square which was in front of the Water Gate, and they asked Ezra the scribe to bring the book of the law of Moses which the Lord had given to Israel. Then Ezra the priest brought the law before the assembly of men, women and all who could listen with understanding, on the first day of the seventh month... (7) Also Jeshua, Bani, Sherebiah, Jamin, Akkub, Shabbethai, Hodiah, Maaseiah, Kelita, Azariah, Jozabad, Hanan, Pelaiah, the Levites, explained the law to the people while the people remained in their place. They read from the book, from the law of God, translating to give the sense so that they understood the reading."

It was not a fraction of the people who gathered. And they did not assemble reluctantly. Instead, we read that **"all the people gathered as one man."** This event was the beginning of a spiritual revival in Israel. The days of their splintered, fragmented, oppressed existence in Babylon and Persia were over. In a way that their foreign oppressors had never permitted, they assembled *en masse*, with one heart and mind, no longer a minority group but the majority group.

They gathered **"at the square which was in front of the Water Gate."** They couldn't have made such a statement six months earlier. Not long ago, the walls, houses, and gates that framed their assembly had laid in shambles. But under the leadership of Nehemiah, they had rebuilt Jerusalem. Despite constant political pressure to abandon the project, the entire nation could assemble in the restored ruins of their capital city.

And what was the purpose of their meeting? You might expect them to celebrate the end of construction, to rest on their laurels, or pat themselves on the back. Instead, **"they asked Ezra the scribe to bring the book of the Law of Moses."** They were coming from a place of humility. The Scriptures were not imposed upon them by the higher-ups; they were asking for it. They knew they needed to return to God and His Word. But what drove this conviction? Why ask for this book?

Because this was a book **"which the Lord had given to Israel."** They acknowledged the inspiration of the Scriptures. They wanted to understand and obey that which God had revealed to them. Their attitude embodied Deuteronomy 29:29, *"The secret things belong to the Lord our God, but the things revealed belong to us and to our sons forever, that we may observe all the words of this law."*

This revival revolved around the Scriptures, so **"Ezra the priest brought the law."** In the previous sentence, Ezra was called a scribe. But here he is called a priest. Ezra's religious leadership involved these two crucial roles. As a scribe, he led Israel in an accurate understanding of the Scriptures. And as a priest, he led them in an appropriate response to the Scriptures. His leadership encompassed both teaching and worship.

The goal of this gathering was for God's people to understand God's Word. It was not a strategy session, limited to influential men and military leaders. It was not an all-hands meeting for the construction effort, limited to able-bodied men. This gathering was an **"assembly of men, women, and all who could listen with understanding."** Comprehension was the only qualification that mattered.

This event took place **"on the first day of the seventh month."** Back in Leviticus 23:24-25, God ordained a restful, worshipful holiday on this exact date. *"In the seventh month on the first of the month you shall have a rest, a reminder by blowing of trumpets, a holy convocation. You shall not do any laborious work, but you shall present an offering by fire to the Lord."* Nine days later, they were to celebrate Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement. And four days after that, they were to celebrate Sukkot, the Feast of Booths. After seventy years in a foreign land, the freedom to once again observe these festivals was a reminder of God's deliverance.

Skipping down to verse 7, we read a long list of hard-to-pronounce names. **"Jeshua, Bani, Sherebiah, Jamin, Akkub, Shabbethai, Hodiah, Maaseiah, Kelita, Azariah, Jozabad, Hanan, Pelaiah, the Levites."** The Levites were a clan of priests who traditionally worked in the Temple and maintained the sacrificial system. But since the Temple's destruction, the nature of their work had morphed. As the synagogue model developed, the Levites became responsible for explaining God's Word to the people -- and that's just what they did next.

First, the people stood for the reading of God's Word. Verse 3 says Ezra read the Scriptures *"from early morning until midday,"* around five hours of reading. (And we start getting antsy when a sermon lasts longer than an hour!) But that wasn't the whole event. **"The people remained in their place"** while the thirteen Levites **"explained the law to the people."** Simply reading wasn't enough; they needed someone to explain it. They had to dig deeper to understand what they had heard.

That's the nature of exegesis. The people spent seventy years in exile, growing apart from the language and culture of their ancestors. So when Ezra read the Scriptures in the original Hebrew, they needed to perform exegesis. They needed someone "to lead out, to set forth in great detail, to expound" upon what Ezra read. Verse 8 tells us that these Levites were **"translating to give the sense so that they understood the reading."**

Their goal was not to simply preach sermons because they liked to. Their goal was not to show off their Hebrew language skills as if Aramaic speakers weren't spiritual enough. And their goal was not to keep the people entertained. Their goal was to help the people understand their Bibles!

That's exegesis.

The people couldn't expect to understand the text immediately. So much had changed. They had to reacquaint themselves with Mosaic literature, recall its historical and religious context, and translate the text into their contemporary Aramaic vernacular. In other words, they had to draw meaning out of the Scriptures according to a literal, historical, grammatical hermeneutic.

## Clarity

In this chapter, I've tried to model a simple interpretive process.

First, we tried reading our passage with no context. Then we took a step back. We immersed ourselves in the context of Nehemiah 8 before rereading it. And the second time, it (hopefully) leaped off the page in new ways! As we examined each detail, we were able to extract more precise meaning.

In Nehemiah 8, the Jews had to bridge the gap created by seventy years' distance from the text. In the same way, we must also bridge the gap between the Scriptures and our own cultural context. The difference is: we're dealing with a lot more than seventy years! Therefore, we have to work even harder to understand the literary, historical, and grammatical context of the Bible. This fact leads to an important principle I want you to understand.

> **Getting to authorial intent takes hard work!**

Now, a word of clarification is in order. I don't mean that Scripture is unclear. On the contrary: Scripture is indeed clear and understandable. You can read your Bible and be confident that it means what it says. However, there are two different kinds of "clear."

Consider the equation **2 + 2 = 4**. That is a clear statement. It's also very simple, easily graspable; you can demonstrate it to a toddler with Cheerios.

Now consider the equation **e = mc²**. That is also a clear statement. Its meaning is not ambiguous; it communicates a specific concept in universal terms. But it's not very simple. It may take years of study to grasp it. And, to the best of my current knowledge, you can't demonstrate Einstein's Theory of Relativity with Cheerios.

The Bible is indeed clear, but it's not always simple. Even the Apostle Peter admitted that some Scriptures were *"hard to understand"* (2 Peter 3:16). Getting to authorial intent takes hard work. But the hard work of exegesis is well worth the effort!

## Significance

Let's look at one more example, this time from the New Testament. In Acts 8:29-36, Philip has a fascinating interaction with an Ethiopian eunuch. I won't spend much time on this one. I simply want to demonstrate that, 500 years after the events of Nehemiah 8, the process of extracting meaning from the text had remained largely the same.

> "Then the Spirit said to Philip, 'Go up and join this chariot.' Philip ran up and heard him reading Isaiah the prophet, and said, 'Do you understand what you are reading?' And he said, 'Well, how could I, unless someone guides me?' And he invited Philip to come up and sit with him. Now the passage of Scripture which he was reading was this: 'He was led as a sheep to slaughter; and as a lamb before its shearer is silent, so He does not open His mouth. In humiliation His judgment was taken away; who will relate to His generation? For His life is removed from the earth.' The eunuch answered Philip and said, 'Please tell me, of whom does the prophet say this? Of himself or someone else?' Then Philip opened his mouth, and beginning from this Scripture he preached Jesus to him."

The Ethiopian eunuch was struggling to understand the Scriptures. Because he was missing the full context of the 700-year-old prophecy in Isaiah 53, he couldn't answer some crucial questions about the text. And notice how Philip responded. He explained the text's meaning (authorial intent), but he didn't stop there. He also explained its full theological significance and then applied it, encouraging the Ethiopian to be baptized.

These distinctions are essential. The context, meaning, significance, and application of a text are all different things. When we confuse them, we tend to misunderstand, misappropriate, and misapply the Scriptures. But sound exegesis seeks to align these elements according to a literal historical grammatical hermeneutic. I like to diagram it like this:

**Interpretation** (A Thing)
- *Meaning* (Authorial)
  - *Context*
    - Literary
    - Historical
    - Grammatical
- *Significance* (Theological)
  - Biblical
  - Systematic

**Application** (An Action)
- Personal
- Corporate

We'll talk about application later, but I want you to see how this all fits together.

We avoid an "Overly Theological" hermeneutic (see chapter 3) by anchoring ourselves in the original author's literary, historical, and grammatical context. This is necessary for sound exegesis. But once we understand the authorial intent of the text, we must not neglect its broader theological significance. You see, biblical and systematic theology are still crucial for interpreting the Bible; they're just not the right starting place.

Philip models this in Acts 8:28-36. He explained the meaning of Isaiah's prophecy and then took it all the way home. The answer to the Ethiopian's question -- *"Of whom does the prophet speak?"* -- was inherently connected to the broader theological significance of Isaiah 53.

*"Surely our griefs He Himself bore, and our sorrows He carried; yet we ourselves esteemed Him stricken, smitten of God, and afflicted. But He was pierced through for our transgressions, He was crushed for our iniquities; the chastening for our well-being fell upon Him, and by His scourging we are healed. All of us like sheep have gone astray, each of us has turned to his own way; but the Lord has caused the iniquity of us all to fall on Him."*

And then, the section the Ethiopian was struggling with, *"He was oppressed and He was afflicted, yet He did not open His mouth; like a lamb that is led to slaughter, and like a sheep that is silent before its shearers, so He did not open His mouth. By oppression and judgment He was taken away; and as for His generation, who considered that He was cut off out of the land of the living for the transgression of my people, to whom the stroke was due?"*

Isaiah wrote about the substitutionary atonement necessary to deliver God's people from judgment, and yet this suffering servant's identity had been a mystery until Christ. But Philip had the whole picture, the broader story, the big idea. So when the Ethiopian asked him who the verse was referring to, he didn't hesitate to interpret the text fully. *"Beginning from this Scripture He preached Jesus to him."* Philip explained not only the authorial intent but also the broader theological significance of the text.

That's exegesis.

## Summary

When we study our Bibles, we engage in work that God's people have done for thousands of years. We holistically discover the context, meaning, and significance of the Scriptures. Both Old Testament and New Testament saints upheld this process. The chapters so far have been mostly theoretical; the remaining chapters will get very practical.
