# 2. The Doctrine of Inspiration

At each level of our pyramid, I've chosen a passage of Scripture that embodies, exemplifies, or explains that concept well. Let's begin with 2 Timothy 3:16-17.

> *"All Scripture is inspired by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, for training in righteousness; so that the man of God may be adequate, equipped for every good work."*

In the original Greek text, the phrase "inspired by God" is a single word: *theopneustos*. It means "breathed out by God." By using this word, Paul is saying that God is the source of all Scripture; that is, it has proceeded directly from God. This truth distinguishes Scripture from other writings. We have many outstanding Christian books available to us, but only the Bible was breathed out by God.

Paul also says that Scripture is "profitable" precisely because it is God-breathed. Due to its uniquely divine origin, Scripture is helpful in a way that no other writings are. That doesn't mean that we should reject other sources of truth as useless, but we should recognize that the Bible is uniquely useful to Christians.

This idea (that Scripture is God-breathed) is what we mean by the term "inspiration." Now, there are multiple ways to think about inspiration. We'll discuss some competing views in a moment. But first, I want to look at the most logical, biblical perspective and the one that Christians have affirmed throughout the ages.

> **Verbal Plenary Inspiration** -- *The precise wording of Scripture has been breathed out by God, and therefore bears God's authority.*

It's a complicated term for a relatively simple concept.

By **"verbal,"** we mean that God delivered His revelation in the form of words, gathered into phrases that convey meaning. Now, this doesn't mean that God never communicates non-verbally. In Romans 1:20, Paul says that *"since the creation of the world His invisible attributes, His eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly seen, being understood through what has been made."*

But the Bible is different. Hebrews 1:1-2 says, *"God, after He spoke long ago to the fathers in the prophets in many portions and in many ways, in these last days has spoken to us in His Son."* As New Testament believers, we have received the complete revelation of God in verbal form. That's why we refer to it as "the Word of God."

By **"plenary,"** we mean that it possesses unqualified and absolute authority. If you've ever been to a large Christian conference, the event schedule may have distinguished between "breakout" sessions and "plenary" sessions. A plenary session is the main event, a large gathering where everyone sits under the preaching of the Word together. And in the context of inspiration, that term conveys the universal authority of God's Word.

*Verbal Plenary Inspiration*, then, is the view that God sovereignly directed the authors of Scripture to communicate His exact message. Each word, sentence, paragraph, book, corpus, testament, and indeed the whole canon is composed precisely according to God's design. Nothing is missing; nothing is out of place.

Furthermore, because this message is God-breathed, it possesses His authority. If you subscribe to this view, you essentially read these ancient documents as the very words of God Himself. From this understanding, we derive an important principle about the doctrine of inspiration:

> *Scripture is the Word of God, and the words of God.*

What do I mean by this? People will often say something like, "While I agree that the Bible is God's Word, I don't believe that every single part of the Bible is the actual words of God." They like the big ideas of the Bible, but they're not confident that every story is factual or every command is binding.

But with a Verbal Plenary view of inspiration, we would reject this kind of statement. We believe that Scripture is not only the Word of God (an accurate representation of His overall message); it's also the words of God (stories, statements, and commands that have come directly from God). Psalm 119:60 says, *"The sum of Your word is truth, and every one of Your righteous ordinances is everlasting."* It's not either-or, it's both-and!

In another passage, David provides a fascinating description of how Verbal Plenary Inspiration worked. He says in 2 Samuel 23:2, *"The Spirit of the Lord spoke by me, and His word was on my tongue."* He is describing a kind of dual authorship. When we read a Davidic psalm, was David the author? Yes. Was God the author? Also yes. The Holy Spirit's message came to us through the mouth of David. God didn't override David's free will. Through David's own skill, language, life experience, and artistic impulse, God sovereignly produced the exact message He desired to impart to His people. That's how Verbal Plenary Inspiration works.

## Competing Views

There are several competing theories of inspiration, other beliefs about how God put meaning into the biblical text. As we examine them, it's important to remember our definition of **Authorial Intent** (*the original meaning intended by the author of a text*). We'll notice how each of these views thinks about authorial intent.

> **Dictation** -- *God bypassed the intentions and circumstances of the human authors, "dictating" the entire content of the Bible.*

Imagine you need to respond to a text message while driving. You might hand your phone to a passenger and tell them precisely what to write. Or you might use voice recognition to transcribe your message automatically. To the person on the receiving end, it doesn't necessarily matter what method you've used, as long as your message was transcribed accurately. This is very similar to the Dictation Theory of inspiration.

There's a nugget of truth here; God did indeed dictate some parts of the Bible. For example, the Sinaitic Law is essentially Moses' record of what God told him to write. And in Revelation 1-3, we have John's transcript of Christ's message to several first-century churches. However, Dictation Theory proposes that God dictated the entire Bible in this way. In this view, authorial intent is unimportant. The intentions and circumstances of the human author have little to do with interpreting their writings because the human authors were simply stenographers.

> **Partial/Conceptual** -- *God impressed concepts into the minds of the human authors, and they wrote about them in detailed but potentially flawed ways.*

Have you ever played a game of telephone? You whisper something to the person on your right, and they repeat it to the person on their right until the phrase goes around the entire table. By the time it makes its way back into your left ear, it may be a completely different concept than you originally intended. Each individual's understanding corrupts and contorts the original phrase. That's similar to how the Partial/Conceptual view of inspiration works.

In this view, passages that speak against homosexuality may be considered a product of their time. You could reduce the Creation account to a simple rehash of Ancient Near-Eastern mythology. These passages may not represent God's opinion as much as the human author's culture. In this view, authorial intent is still fundamentally important. But it's important because it's obscuring the truth. The intentions and circumstances of the human authors interfere with the message that God's trying to convey.

> **Natural** -- *There is no divinity in the inspiration of the Bible. The intentions and circumstances of the human authors are all there is, and therefore, they have no real authority.*

In this view, there is no supernatural distinction between the Bible and any other historical document. The Scriptures are simply the writings of wise men. They are no more inherently authoritative or profitable than the writings of Oprah or the Bhagavad Ghita. A proponent of the Natural view might say, "The authors of the Bible were extraordinarily gifted writers, poets, and perhaps even gifted fortune-tellers, but not vessels of divine truth."

In this case, there is only authorial intent. There is no dual authorship, no "God-breathed" nature to the Scriptures. There is nothing more than the intentions and circumstances of the human authors. And therefore, there is no absolute authority behind the Scriptures. You can accept or reject the truth claims of the Bible as you would the opinions of any human writer.

## Summary

None of these competing views are sufficient to explain the Inspiration of Scripture. Verbal Plenary Inspiration is the most logical, biblical view available to us. As we continue to the next level of our pyramid, we'll continue to flesh out the implications of this view for the study of God's Word.
