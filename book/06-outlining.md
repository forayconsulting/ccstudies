# 6. Outlining

When it comes to interpreting the Bible, outlining is a crucial skill. This chapter will demonstrate why outlining is essential, how to do it, and how to use outlines in our Bible study. But before we go much further, I need to define what I mean by "outlining."

> **Outlining** -- *The process of notating the structure, themes, and argument flow of a book of the Bible.*

Outlining helps us discover the literary and grammatical context of an individual passage. Writing a good outline is like tracing an object's contours to depict its silhouette on paper. But writing a bad outline is like trying to force a square peg into a round hole. Good outlines reflect what's in the text; they don't try to impose a structure that isn't there.

It's also worth defining what I *don't* mean. I don't mean sermon notes (often referred to as "homiletical outlines"). And I'm not talking about a commentary or exposition, either. I mean a streamlined series of points, identifying the sections and subsections of a book.

Let me show you an example.

> **A Syntactical Outline of 2 Thessalonians**
>
>     (1:1-4) Greetings & Thanksgiving
>
>     (1:5-3:5) Instruction Regarding the Return of Christ
>
>             (1:5-12) The Hope & Threat of Christ's Return
>
>             (2:1-17) The Timing & Implications of Christ's Return
>
>             (3:1-5) Praying for the Triumph of God's Kingdom
>
>     (3:6-15) Exhortation Regarding the Undisciplined
>
>             (3:6-10) Principle Exhortations
>
>             (3:11-15) Practical Exhortations
>
>     (3:16-18) Conclusion

I created this outline while studying 2 Thessalonians. You can see how I've parsed Paul's letter into an indented outline that highlights its structure, themes, and argument flow. This structure is called a "syntactical outline" -- an analysis based on syntax.

Now, it's important to note that outlines are subjective. Mine will probably look different from yours, and they'll both probably look different from the outline in your study Bible. We might end up seeing the same major divisions across an entire book, but we may title them a bit differently.

The important thing is: if we've produced an outline, we've engaged with the literature and developed a sense of the author's message. It's essential to do this on our own. It's hard work, but it pays dividends when answering complex questions about the context of a passage.

## Why Outline?

At the risk of quoting *Toward an Exegetical Theology* too often here, I want to highlight another statement by Walt Kaiser. I think this quote speaks to the importance of outlining in the interpretive process.

> *"Good exegetical procedure dictates that the details be viewed in light of the total context. Unless the exegete knows where the thought of the text begins and how that pattern develops, all the intricate details may be of little or no worth. This ability -- the ability to state what each section of a book is about and how the paragraphs in that section contribute to that argument -- is one of the most critical steps. If the exegete falters here, much of what follows will be wasted time and effort."*

A single blue puzzle piece could be anything: flowers, water, the sky, a bluebird. To understand what it is and where it fits, you need to understand the whole picture. And Scripture passages are like individual puzzle pieces. We must read, study, and explain those pieces as they relate to the whole.

So, why outline? I would propose three reasons why.

First, because **outlining develops authorial intent.** Remember our definition of **Authorial Intent:** *The original meaning intended by the author of a text.* Outlining helps us interpret and understand the text as God intended it to be interpreted and understood.

Second, because **outlining discovers the structure of the book.** The ancient authors didn't divide their work into digestible sections using chapter and verse markings (commentators developed these divisions several centuries after the New Testament's completion). Outlining helps us grasp the high-level themes and arguments the author used to make their point.

And third, because **outlining determines sections for concentrated study.** Do you ever sit down to study the Bible but find it difficult to identify what exactly to study? Outlining helps us figure out where to start by breaking a big book into smaller sections.

## Two Critical Skills

There are two invaluable skills involved in outlining: summary and synthesis.

> **Summary** -- *Condensing a lengthy, difficult, or complicated passage into a simple statement.*

> **Synthesis** -- *Combining multiple sources, ideas, or passages to form a single argument.*

Outlining involves both of these skills working in tandem. Let me demonstrate what I mean using the example of Matthew 1-2. The skill of **Summary** takes those two chapters and condenses them down to a series of simple points:

>     (1:1-17) The Genealogy of Christ
>
>     (1:18-25) The Birth of Christ
>
>     (2:1-12) The Visit of the Wise Men
>
>     (2:13-15) The Flight to Egypt
>
>     (2:16-18) Herod Kills the Children
>
>     (2:19-23) Jesus Returns to Nazareth

And the skill of **Synthesis** combines these points into a single header line that captures the overall topic of the entire passage:

>     (Matthew 1:1-2:23) The Advent of the King

Do you see how these skills are helpful?

By thoughtfully summarizing the Scriptures, we create a list of simplified statements reflecting the content of the text. And by repetitively synthesizing, we add structure to that list, reflecting the overall movement of the text. These two skills are invaluable as we seek to understand the literary and grammatical context of any passage.

I call them "skills" because they take practice to get good at! The more you outline the Bible, the more accurate and readable your outlines will become. So don't be discouraged if you find it challenging at first; repetition is the key to mastery!

## Finding the Unit of the Text

Our first task is to divide a passage into appropriately sized pieces for outlining. Thus, we need to identify the primary unit of thought in that particular text. How does this passage naturally break down? What are the literary building blocks of the author's argument?

However, this can be challenging. That measure -- the author's unit of thought -- often changes from one genre to another. And the Bible contains several different genres. You might outline Matthew's gospel one way and Paul's letter to the Corinthians in an entirely different way. In the section below, we'll identify the unit of thought most commonly identified with each genre.

### Epistolary Literature

An epistle is a personal letter. Romans, Jude, and Hebrews are all epistles. And in a personal letter, the quintessential unit of thought is a sentence. I don't mean that you can't find meaning in other units (clauses or paragraphs, for example). I just mean that in epistles, sentences are usually the building blocks of the author's argument. A sentence typically conveys a single, complete thought.

For example, let's say we're outlining Paul's epistle to the Ephesians. Our first unit would be Ephesians 1:1-2 because that's the first complete sentence in the book.

> *"Paul, an apostle of Christ Jesus by the will of God, to the saints who are in Ephesus and who are faithful in Christ Jesus: Grace to you and peace from our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ."*

And our final unit would be Ephesians 6:24 because that's the last complete sentence in the book.

> *"Grace be with all those who love our Lord Jesus Christ with incorruptible love."*

### Narrative Literature

Narrative literature tells a story. Judges, Matthew, and Acts are examples of narrative literature. And in a narrative, the quintessential unit of thought is a scene. I don't mean that you can't find meaning in other units. An author still constructs a narrative using sentences and paragraphs, but they're not the primary unit of thought like in a personal letter. In narratives, scenes are usually the building blocks of the author's argument.

When you're outlining narrative literature, think like a movie director. If you filmed this story, when would you cut away to a different time or location? Where does the plot "jump ahead" chronologically or geographically?

For example, let's say we're outlining the book of Acts. The author depicts the story of Pentecost in a single scene. The scene begins by establishing a new setting in Acts 2:1.

> *"When the day of Pentecost had come, they were all together in one place."*

And the "cut" is found in Acts 3:1, as the author introduces the next scene.

> *"Now Peter and John were going up to the temple at the ninth hour, the hour of prayer."*

Therefore, the entirety of chapter 2 is a single unit of thought. Of course, if we're studying Pentecost, we'd still want to dig deeper. But if we're outlining the whole book of Acts, Pentecost is a single literary unit.

### Poetic Literature

Poetry can be a bit more complicated. Poets typically structure their work around some kind of literary device. For example, most Western literature is designed to rhyme. However, biblical poetry is different; it rarely rhymes, even in Hebrew. This is because most Ancient Near Eastern poetry was structured around another literary device: parallelism.

Parallelism is when an author arranges statements that compare or contrast with each other to produce meaningful, symbolic, or even ironic results. These statement-pairings are called strophes. And strophes are the quintessential unit of biblical poetry.

For example, Psalm 42:1 is a single strophe comprised of two parallel clauses. (I'll separate them with a forward slash to illustrate what I mean.)

> *"As the deer pants for water / so my soul pants for you, O God."*

Strophes don't necessarily involve word pictures or comparisons. Sometimes they're just two parallel thoughts paired together in a meaningful way. For example, consider Psalm 23:1.

> *"The Lord is my Shepherd / I shall not want."*

When outlining biblical poetry, a single strophe is a unit of thought.

### Prophetic Literature

The writings of Isaiah, Ezekiel, and Jeremiah are examples of this genre. The prophets were essentially preachers, and their careers sometimes spanned many decades and multiple eras of Jewish history. We can often divide their written works into individual sermons. Now, we can (and should) outline each individual sermon on its own. But if we're outlining an entire prophetic book, each sermon is a quintessential unit of thought.

Sermons often begin by identifying their topic. For example, Isaiah 13 begins with the phrase, *"The oracle concerning Babylon which Isaiah the son of Amoz saw"* (13:1). This statement tells us what we're about to read.

Two chapters later, another sermon starts with *"The oracle concerning Moab..."* (15:1). And another two chapters down the line, a third sermon begins, *"The oracle concerning Damascus..."* (17:1).

As I mentioned, we can (and should) dissect each of these sermons further. But most prophetic books are best outlined in terms of the sermons they contain. Due to the nature of prophetic ministry, sermons are the primary divisions inherent to that genre of literature.

### Apocalyptic Literature

Have you ever listened to an old classic remixed by a cutting-edge DJ? It's surprising and intriguing because it could go anywhere! What was once an orchestral swell could become a pulsing bassline, a crooner's voice could become a mind-boggling beatbox, and so on. Apocalyptic literature does something similar.

The apocalyptic format is an interesting (and often confusing) mashup of other genres. And the key to grasping this format is understanding its references and allusions. You might enjoy the experience, but you won't truly "get" it unless you appreciate the other authors, genres, and theology that it's remixing.

For example, the Book of Revelation begins like an epistle.

> *"John to the seven churches that are in Asia: Grace to you and peace, from Him who is and who was and who is to come, and from the seven spirits which are before His throne, and from Jesus Christ, the faithful witness, the first-born of the dead, and the ruler of the kings of the earth"* (1:4).

However, within a few chapters, it shifts into a narrative.

> *"After these things I looked, and behold, a door standing open in heaven, and the first voice which I had heard, like the sound of a trumpet speaking with me, said, 'Come up here, and I will show you what must take place after these things.'"* (4:1)

Throughout the rest of the book, we also find several poetic sections like this one.

> *"And they sang a new song, saying, 'Worthy are You to take the book and break its seals; for You were slain, and purchased for God with your blood men from every tribe and tongue and people and nation"* (5:9).

And the entire book borrows style, imagery, and authority from the prophetic genre. John's conclusion especially highlights this aspect of the text.

> *"And he said to me, 'These words are faithful and true;' and the Lord, the God of the spirits of the prophets, sent His angel to show His bond-servants the things which must soon take place. 'And behold, I am coming quickly. Blessed is he who heeds the words of the prophecy of this book.'"* (22:6-7)

Scripture contains plenty of other genres and sub-genres. However, I've found that these five genres are the most recognizable. And when I start a new outline, I begin by identifying the genre and unit of that text. Now, let's look at the next important element of outlining.

## Identifying Logical Movement

A conjunction is an essential part of speech. Conjunctions connect clauses, sentences, ideas, or thoughts by expressing the relationship between them. And when we're outlining a passage of Scripture, they allow us to identify logical movement. They serve as keywords, telegraphing the author's flow of thought, moving their argument forward clause by clause.

I want to call your attention to six types of conjunctions. The categories themselves aren't terribly important, but they serve to illustrate the purpose of conjunctions in the Bible. When you're outlining a passage of Scripture, be on the lookout for these keywords.

**Coordinating Conjunctions** show equality between two thoughts or clauses.

> Look for terms like *"and," "or," "nor," "for,"* and *"but."* Also, keep an eye out for conjunction pairings like *"neither... nor," "either... or,"* and *"both... and."*
>
> For example, Romans 2:2 begins with a coordinating conjunction. *"**And** we know that the judgment of God rightly falls upon those who practice such things."*

**Adversative Conjunctions** show contrast.

> Look for terms like *"but," "yet," "except," "however,"* and *"nevertheless."*
>
> For example, Romans 3:21 begins with an adversative conjunction. *"**But** now apart from the law the righteousness of God has been manifested."*

**Emphatic Conjunctions** demonstrate force.

> Look for terms like *"yea," "certainly," "verily," "truly,"* and *"in fact."*
>
> For example, Romans 6:5 uses an emphatic conjunction to join two thoughts. *"For if we have become united with Him in the likeness of His death, **certainly** we shall also be in the likeness of his resurrection."*

**Inferential Conjunctions** show a conclusion or relation between ideas.

> Look for terms like *"therefore," "so," "when," "wherefore,"* and *"for."*
>
> For example, Romans 8:1 begins with an inferential conjunction. *"**Therefore** there is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."*

**Transitional Conjunctions** demonstrate a link or continuation of thought.

> Look for terms like *"and," "moreover,"* and *"then."*
>
> For example, Romans 9:23 uses a transitional conjunction to continue an earlier thought. *"**And** He did so to make known the riches of His glory upon vessels of mercy."*

And **Subordinating Conjunctions** show a dependent clause.

> Look for terms like *"when," "because," "if," "since,"* and *"although."*
>
> For example, Romans 4:25 uses two subordinating conjunctions. *"He who was delivered over **because** of our transgressions, and was raised **because** of our justification."*

These categories are somewhat subjective. But I want you to start thinking about logical movement. Where is the author going? How do they get there? How are individual ideas used to form a comprehensive argument? As we seek to answer these questions, it's helpful to understand the author's use of conjunctions.

We've addressed several foundational concepts. We've talked about the twin skills of summary and synthesis. We discussed the importance of genre in finding the unit of the text. And we've seen how conjunctions help us identify logical movement.

## A Practical Technique for Outlining

To outline an entire book of the Bible, we follow the same process detailed above. But instead of focusing on a single chapter, we widen our scope to the whole book. Now that you're familiar with this process, I want to propose a practical technique for outlining books of the Bible. I'll list the steps and explain them below.

1. Read the book repetitively.
2. Identify major divisions.
3. Break the text down into units.
4. Summarize each unit.
5. Group the units by commonality.
6. Repeat 4 and 5 until no longer possible.

I want to stress the importance of Step 1. Repetitive reading is an integral part of engaging the text. I usually read a book two or three times before outlining it. And as I read, I note potential movements, themes, and sub-topics. These notes accumulate across multiple readings so that I already have a pretty good idea of what the author's getting at by the time I start outlining.

Step 2 is especially essential when outlining longer books. It can be challenging to analyze an entire body of work as a single entity, so I've found myself identifying major divisions first. Then I can tackle each in order. To identify these high-level sections, I look for big moments. These might be major plot points like David's sin with Bathsheba (2 Samuel 11). It could be a shift in genre, like the unexpected and unrepeated use of narrative in Amos 7:10. Maybe there's a shift in perspective, like the section of Daniel written by Nebuchadnezzar (Daniel 4). Or perhaps there are theological crescendos like Paul's climactic "therefore" in Romans 8:1. Once I've identified these big moments, I can divide the book into high-level movements and outline each individually. This approach makes it much easier to outline an entire book.

Steps 3 through 6 follow the same process as our earlier exercises. Using this technique, we can study whole books with an organized approach. Outlining empowers us to apply a literal, historical, grammatical hermeneutic at the macro-level, strengthening our exegetical efforts at the micro-level.

In this sense, outlining should be considered an essential part of exegesis. When we take the time to digest the high-level argument of the text, we will inevitably reap richer results from our study of any one passage within that text. I strongly encourage you to practice these skills often. You'll get better at outlining over time, and as you do, I believe you'll find the exercise more and more valuable.
