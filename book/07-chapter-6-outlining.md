# 6. Outlining

Outlining is a crucial skill for interpreting the Bible. As we continue to develop the tools necessary for faithful exegesis, we must learn to see the big picture of a biblical book before we zoom in on individual passages.

> **Outlining** – *The process of notating the structure, themes, and argument flow of a book of the Bible.*

Good outlines reflect what's in the text; they don't impose structure on it. An outline is not a set of sermon notes or a commentary. It is a streamlined series of points identifying the sections and subsections of a biblical book. Think of it as a road map that traces the author's argument from beginning to end.

As an example, consider a syntactical outline of 2 Thessalonians. An effective outline would use indentation and verse references to show how the book's argument flows from one section to the next, identifying major divisions and their subordinate points.

## Why Outline?

In *Toward an Exegetical Theology*, Walt Kaiser emphasizes the importance of tracing the flow of an author's argument as a mark of good exegetical procedure. Without this discipline, we risk misinterpreting individual passages by divorcing them from their context.

Think of it like a puzzle. If you dump a thousand pieces onto a table, each piece is meaningless on its own. You need to see the picture on the box to understand where each piece fits. Outlining gives us the picture on the box. It helps us understand the whole so that we can properly interpret the parts.

There are three reasons why outlining is essential:

1. **Outlining develops authorial intent.** By tracing the argument of a book from start to finish, we discover what the author was trying to communicate. Individual passages take on clearer meaning when we understand the larger argument they serve.

2. **Outlining discovers the structure of the book.** Ancient authors didn't use chapter and verse markings. Those were added centuries later for reference purposes. The original structure of a biblical book must be discovered through careful observation, and outlining is the tool that helps us do that.

3. **Outlining determines sections for concentrated study.** Once we understand the structure of a book, we can identify appropriate sections for deeper study. Without an outline, we might arbitrarily select a passage that begins or ends in the wrong place, cutting across the author's intended units of thought.

## Two Critical Skills

Two skills are essential to the outlining process: summary and synthesis.

> **Summary** – *Condensing a lengthy, difficult, or complicated passage into a simple statement.*

> **Synthesis** – *Combining multiple sources, ideas, or passages to form a single argument.*

To illustrate, consider Matthew 1-2. Using the skill of summary, we can condense each section into a simple statement:

- (1:1-17) The Genealogy of Christ
- (1:18-25) The Birth of Christ
- (2:1-6) The Search of the Magi
- (2:7-12) The Worship of the Magi
- (2:13-18) The Flight to Egypt
- (2:19-23) The Return to Nazareth

Then, using the skill of synthesis, we can combine these summaries into a single heading that captures the overarching theme:

- (Matthew 1:1-2:23) The Advent of the King

Summary breaks the text down into manageable pieces. Synthesis builds those pieces back up into a coherent whole.

## Finding the Unit of the Text

The first step in outlining is to divide the passage into appropriately sized pieces. The key question is: what constitutes a "unit of thought" in this text? The answer depends on the genre.

**Epistolary Literature** — In letters, the unit of thought is a sentence. Each sentence advances the author's argument. For example, the first sentence in Ephesians is 1:1-2, and the last sentence is 6:24. Each sentence between them represents a unit to be summarized.

**Narrative Literature** — In narratives, the unit of thought is a scene. Just as a movie is composed of individual scenes, a biblical narrative moves from one event to the next. In Acts, for example, 2:1 begins the scene of Pentecost, and 3:1 begins the next scene at the temple gate.

**Poetic Literature** — In poetry, the unit of thought is a strophe, which consists of parallel statement-pairings. Hebrew poetry is built on parallelism, where two lines express a related idea. Psalm 42:1 provides an example: "As the deer pants for water / so my soul pants for you, O God." And Psalm 23:1: "The Lord is my Shepherd / I shall not want."

**Prophetic Literature** — In prophetic books, the unit of thought is a sermon. The prophets delivered a series of distinct oracles, each addressing a different audience or subject. Isaiah provides clear markers: 13:1 introduces an "oracle concerning Babylon," 15:1 an "oracle concerning Moab," and 17:1 an "oracle concerning Damascus."

**Apocalyptic Literature** — Apocalyptic writing is a mashup of other genres, which makes it uniquely challenging to outline. Revelation, for instance, begins as an epistle (1:4), shifts to narrative (4:1), includes poetic sections (5:9), and borrows heavily from the prophetic genre (22:6-7). Outlining apocalyptic literature requires sensitivity to these genre shifts and an awareness of the conventions at work in each section.

## Identifying Logical Movement

As we trace the flow of a biblical author's argument, conjunctions serve as important signposts. Conjunctions connect clauses and express logical relationships between ideas. Understanding these relationships is essential for accurate outlining.

**Coordinating Conjunctions** — These express equality or addition: "and," "or," "nor," "for," "but." They connect ideas of equal weight. Example: Romans 2:2.

**Adversative Conjunctions** — These express contrast: "but," "yet," "except," "however," "nevertheless." They signal a shift or counterpoint in the argument. Example: Romans 3:21.

**Emphatic Conjunctions** — These express force or emphasis: "yea," "certainly," "verily," "truly," "in fact." They intensify the point being made. Example: Romans 6:5.

**Inferential Conjunctions** — These express conclusion or logical relation: "therefore," "so," "when," "wherefore," "for." They indicate that the author is drawing a conclusion from what has been said. Example: Romans 8:1.

**Transitional Conjunctions** — These express a link or continuation: "and," "moreover," "then." They signal that the author is moving to a new but related point. Example: Romans 9:23.

**Subordinating Conjunctions** — These introduce a dependent clause: "when," "because," "if," "since," "although." They indicate that one idea is dependent on another. Example: Romans 4:25.

## Synthesis Exercise

Now let's take our summaries and synthesize them into an outline.

**Text:** Philippians 3

**Instructions:**

1. Group the summaries into high-level movements.
2. Create headers for each group.
3. Identify groups of groups.

Step 1 — Looking at our thirteen summaries, we can identify three keywords that capture the major movements of the chapter: "rejoice," "confidence," and "perseverance."

Step 2 — We create three headers based on these movements:

- (3:1) Joy in the Lord
- (3:2-11) Confidence in the Lord
- (3:12-21) Perseverance in the Lord

Step 3 — The third group can be further divided into sub-points:

- (3:12-14) Example
- (3:15-17) Instruction
- (3:18-21) Reasoning

## A Practical Technique for Outlining

Here is a six-step process for outlining any book of the Bible:

1. **Read the book repetitively.** There is no substitute for reading the text over and over again. Familiarity with the content is the foundation of a good outline.
2. **Identify major divisions.** Look for the big moments in the text — the turning points, genre shifts, or structural markers that divide the book into its largest sections. For example, David's sin in 2 Samuel 11 is a pivotal turning point in the narrative. The genre shift in Amos 7:10 marks a major division. Daniel 4 introduces a new speaker. Romans 8:1 signals a major inferential conclusion.
3. **Break the text down into units.** Using the genre-appropriate unit of thought (sentence, scene, strophe, sermon), divide the text into its smallest meaningful pieces.
4. **Summarize each unit.** Condense each unit into a simple, concise statement.
5. **Group units by commonality.** Look for shared themes, keywords, or logical connections that allow you to combine individual units into larger sections.
6. **Repeat steps 4 and 5 until it is no longer possible.** Continue summarizing and synthesizing at higher levels until you arrive at the broadest divisions of the book.

Steps 1 and 2 deserve special emphasis. Repetitive reading is indispensable. You cannot outline what you do not know. And identifying major divisions early in the process provides a framework that guides the rest of your work.

Outlining is an essential part of exegesis. It strengthens our macro-level understanding of a biblical book and prepares us to study individual passages with accuracy and confidence.
