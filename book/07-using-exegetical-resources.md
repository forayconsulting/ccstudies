# 7. Using Exegetical Tools

When you're asking questions about a passage, outlining doesn't give you all the answers. Maybe you need to research a book's historical context. Perhaps you're looking for biographical information about the author or a particular character. In such cases, you'll almost always need to turn to external resources.

As Americans in the 21st Century, we're incredibly blessed. Books are more accessible and less expensive than they've ever been. The internet has made millions of texts available for little or no cost. Compared to many of our brethren overseas and in past ages, we have access to an unprecedented breadth of Christian literature. We must not fail to take advantage of this! However, this immense blessing can create issues of its own.

The sheer volume of literature available to us can quickly become overwhelming. There are thousands of potential resources vying to answer any question you might have. Understanding the various categories and uses of these tools can sometimes feel like insider information, reserved for the "professionals." How do you know which one to use? What's the right tool for the job? When do you turn to a commentary? And how do you decide whether to accept or reject an author's interpretation?

In this chapter, I hope to clarify these issues for you.

We'll start by reviewing some common categories of exegetical tools. I'll recommend some choice resources to get you started, many of which are free or widely accessible. Then, we'll talk about how to use these resources appropriately and independently. And finally, we'll walk through some examples out of Philippians 3.

## Types of Tools

The first thing to know is that there are different types of biblical resources. The universe of biblical studies is immense and ever-expanding. New, cutting-edge reference works release with enough frequency that it can be tough to keep up. However, in this book, I'll focus on tried-and-true resources, and there are two reasons why.

First, I like to keep the focus off the resources and on the Scriptures. Our ability to study the Bible is not dependent on owning the latest, greatest lexicon or commentary set. If you're a study nerd like me, I don't mean to discourage you from checking out the latest releases. But let's remember that Christians have been faithfully studying God's Word for thousands of years. And many of our forefathers had comparatively limited access to reference and commentary literature. For this reason, I'll be recommending established and time-tested tools.

Second, many of these widely accepted tools are cheaper and more accessible. Most of us do not have access to seminary libraries or subscribe to academic journals. We won't be dropping $200 for a lexicon or $1,000 for a commentary set. As valuable and enriching as many of those resources may be, they are not accessible for most Christians. For this reason, I like to recommend accessible resources that are free or reasonably priced.

With those caveats out of the way, let's look at some high-level categories of exegetical tools.

### Study Bibles

Study Bibles are simply Bibles that contain a limited amount of commentary to aid the reader in understanding. A Study Bible usually includes detailed introductory material for each book. I recommend the *ESV Study Bible* and *The Open Bible*.

If you're trying to answer a question about a whole book, a study Bible is a great place to get a quick, concise answer. Who was the author? When did they live? What were they addressing? Study Bibles will address these topics in their introductory material. These brief articles are typically easy to consume and designed for daily study.

### Commentaries

A commentary is an author's interpretation of a biblical text, following the consecutive order of the text. There is a great deal of variation among commentaries. Some are comprehensive, covering the entire canon of Scripture (like *Matthew Henry's Commentary on the Whole Bible*). Others focus on a specific facet of the Scriptures (like Carson and Beale's *Commentary on the New Testament Use of the Old Testament*). Some sets are written by multiple authors, usually a collection of specialists in various books or genres. Others reflect a single author's view on an entire corpus, testament, or the whole Bible.

Not all commentaries are written for the same audience or used in the same ways. I generally group commentaries into three broad sub-categories.

#### Devotional Commentaries

Devotional commentaries aim at straightforward exposition and application. They are not unlike long-form sermons and typically engage a more general audience. The author(s) should still address major interpretive issues and engage with linguistic aspects of the text. But they may not delve into the full reasoning behind their exegetical decisions. I recommend *Matthew Henry's Commentary on the Whole Bible* and *The NIV Application Commentary* series.

Are you struggling to conceptualize how a passage could apply to us today? Are you looking for a pithy quote that perfectly encapsulates the meaning of a passage? A devotional commentary is a great place to go for these sorts of needs.

#### Exegetical Commentaries

Exegetical commentaries aim at explanation and exploration. They usually assume a certain level of Greek and/or Hebrew proficiency and are therefore written for a more academic audience. Exegetical commentaries should discuss major interpretive issues and address multiple viewpoints where applicable. I recommend *The New International Commentary* series and *The Word Biblical Commentary* series.

Are you trying to understand the merits and flaws of various interpretations of a text? Do you require some detailed context on a particular Greek or Hebrew word? An exegetical commentary will help you answer these sorts of questions.

#### Hybrid Commentaries

Hybrid commentaries are a mix of the two categories mentioned above. They're more academic and expositional than a devotional commentary but more straightforward and applicational than an exegetical commentary. I recommend *The Expositor's Bible Commentary* and *The Tyndale Old and New Testament Commentaries*.

Looking for a quick "health check" on your interpretation of a passage? Want to make sure you're not completely off the deep end? While you'll never find a single commentary that addresses every possible area, hybrid commentaries are great go-to resources. Use them to make sure you're covering your bases, not forgetting some crucial element of the text you may have overlooked.

These three types of commentaries all play their own role in a well-balanced approach to the text. While it can be expensive and time-consuming to own these resources, many are free or cheap online. In Appendix B, I've listed the current links to several free commentaries.

### Bible Dictionaries

A Bible dictionary is a collection of articles elaborating on names, places, texts, and topics found in the Bible. It's usually sorted alphabetically, like an encyclopedia. Good Bible dictionaries include comprehensive bibliographies for each entry, making them an excellent entry point for any topical study. Bible dictionaries usually aren't the work of a single author. I recommend *The Holman Illustrated Bible Dictionary* and *The Anchor Yale Bible Dictionary*.

Are you studying a specific person, place, or thing in Scripture? Do you need to quickly collect a wide swath of biographical material about a Bible character? Are you looking for a list of other books to read on a particular subject? A Bible dictionary is the perfect place to start your search.

### Concordances

A concordance is a reference work that lists the usages of a specific word throughout the whole Bible. These are useful when studying a term or concept as it develops throughout Scripture. Concordances are organized alphabetically. I recommend *Strong's Exhaustive Concordance of the Bible* and *Young's Analytical Concordance to the Bible*.

Are you studying a specific concept, phrase, or term in Scripture? Do you need to collect a list of passages that use a particular word? A Concordance will help you get those answers quickly.

### Lexicons

A lexicon is a dictionary of terms in a biblical language like Greek, Hebrew, or Aramaic. Lexicons are organized alphabetically -- but in the alphabet of their target language. (For example, a Greek lexicon will begin with *Alpha* and end with *Omega*.) A good lexicon should list multiple potential definitions for a given word based on context, usage, or even time period. "Abridged" or "pocket" lexicons usually provide a few simple "glosses" of Greek or Hebrew words.

For Greek, I recommend *Abbott-Smith's Manual Greek Lexicon of the New Testament*, and for Hebrew, I recommend *The Brown-Driver-Briggs Hebrew-English Lexicon of the Old Testament*. Some expensive but highly recommended alternatives are the lengthily titled *A Greek-English Lexicon of the New Testament and Other Early Christian Literature* (commonly referred to as BDAG) and *A Hebrew and Aramaic Lexicon of the Old Testament* (commonly referred to as *HALOT*).

A word about lexicons: use them with caution. If you don't already know the ins and outs of Greek or Hebrew grammar, never build an interpretation upon the information you find in a lexicon. (In fact, you probably shouldn't be doing that even if you do know Greek or Hebrew!) This might like a strange thing to say, so let me illustrate this point with a brief story.

Several years ago, an acquaintance of mine came to a startling realization about the Greek word *baptizo* (from which we derive the English word "baptize"). You see, his lexicon listed several different definitions for *baptizo*. The first was pretty straightforward: *"to immerse in liquid."* But the fourth or fifth definition was more puzzling: *"to pickle."*

(Now, it should be obvious that the New Testament isn't referring to pickling people. *Baptizo* simply carried the connotation of immersing something in liquid. And at some point in the ancient world, someone used this term to describe the process of preserving food in brine.)

Based on this word study, he excitedly proclaimed that a believer's baptism serves as a kind of spiritual preservative. Baptism, he claimed, guards us against decay, extends our spiritual shelf life, and makes us "salty." Funny, but wrong! That's an example of how *not* to use a lexicon. Any Greek professor worth their salt (pun intended) will repeat this simple mantra time and again: "context, context, context." Context is king. Just because a word *can* mean something doesn't mean it *does* mean that thing every time it appears.

### Cross Referencing Tools

This category is an important one. You may find these resources referred to as "harmonies," "synopses," or "cross-references." Cross-referencing tools connect passages of Scripture to other passages of Scripture. They are typically arranged in the same order as your Bible. When you flip to a particular book, chapter, and verse, you will find a list of other verses that complement, explain, or clarify the one you're looking up. I recommend *The Treasury of Scripture Knowledge* and *The Thompson Chain Reference Bible* (a Bible that integrates extensive cross-references).

Does an author allude to previously written Scripture? Are you trying to answer the question, "What other passages of Scripture clarify and/or illustrate this text?" Turn to a cross-referencing tool to get started.

### Other Types of Tools

There are many other categories of resources, too. From *Systematic Theologies* to *Historical Surveys*, *Grammars* to *Bible Atlases*, we could spend many chapters unpacking all the types of literature in the world of biblical studies. However, for our purposes, the categories I've highlighted above are the most important.

I usually access these tools in three ways: *hard copy, digital, or internet archive*. The first two formats typically require a purchase. The third option is increasingly viable with popular sites like **Archive.org** and **Google Books**. Learn how to build a well-structured query in Google, and you can find almost anything.

## Using Tools

Now, understanding the categories of tools is only the first step. How do we actually use these tools? To use extrabiblical study resources effectively, you must be intentional with them. There are three areas where intentionality is crucial.

First, you must possess **normal reading comprehension**. It might seem like this should go without saying, but trust me -- it doesn't. When I began studying the Bible in earnest, I had to refresh my knowledge of basic English grammar. What's the difference between the object and subject of a sentence? What's a preposition? I had to work on basic reading skills before I could accurately engage the Bible and exegetical tools.

Second, you must practice **organized note-taking**. Keep a list of questions you're trying to resolve, including (but not limited to) our "Ask the Text" questions. And keep a list of your answers -- even answers that may disagree with each other. Keep a list of observations you make while reading or outlining. I'd also encourage you to keep a list of instinctual, intuitive interpretations of a passage, even if they might prove wrong. Sometimes writing them down exposes cognitive biases you may not have otherwise recognized. And getting them down on paper keeps them out of your head until you've finished the work of exegesis.

There are many different methods of note-taking. Maybe you write in the margins of your Bible. Maybe you use Sticky Notes or notepads. You might go the digital route with Microsoft Word, Kindle Notes, or Logos Bible Software. I highly recommend the ESV Scripture Journals, which provide plenty of writing space for outlines and observations. Writing things down helps keep you objective. It doesn't matter where you store your notes -- anywhere but your head!

And third, to use exegetical tools rightly, you must adopt **interpretive freedom**. Your goal should never be to "fall in line" with someone else's interpretation. When you read commentaries, you should use their conclusions to help you form your own.

Now, I do not mean that you should remove the doctrinal boundaries of orthodoxy when you're studying a text. You don't need to wipe off the theological slate of your mind every time you approach a text. But at the end of the day, if we believe that the Bible is God's truth, then it should hold up under scrutiny. Don't ever adopt an interpretation purely because the professionals say so. The Scriptures not only *can* make sense to you, they *must* make sense to you.

When extrabiblical tools enter the picture, it's crucial to practice intentionality in these three areas. We use these resources appropriately when we approach them with solid reading comprehension, organized note-taking, and interpretive freedom.

## Exegetical Examples

Let's look at a few examples from the passage we outlined last chapter, Philippians 3. Say we're asking two initial questions about the author:

- When did they write this?
- Where did they live?

Where would we go to find this information? One place might be a trustworthy Study Bible. Like most Study Bibles, *The Open Bible* includes a wealth of useful introductory material before each book. And sure enough, the introduction to Philippians speaks to the questions of "when?" and "where?" quite openly.

> *"Silas, Timothy, Luke, and Paul first came to Philippi in **A.D. 51, eleven years before Paul wrote this letter**. Philippians 1:13 and 4:22 suggest that **it was written from Rome**, although some commentators argue for Caesarea or Ephesus. Paul's life was at stake, and he was evidently awaiting the verdict of the Imperial Court (2:20-26)."* (emphasis mine)

So *The Open Bible* suggests that Paul wrote his letter to the Philippians from Rome in 62 A.D. Let's look at another reliable resource to compare. In the *ESV Study Bible*'s introduction to Philippians, we find this helpful section:

> *"Scholars have debated Paul's location when he wrote Philippians. Caesarea, Ephesus, and Rome have been the three most commonly proposed locations. Paul was in fact imprisoned in the 'praetorium' of Herod the Great in Caesarea (Acts 23:35; cf. ESV footnote on Phil 1:13) around A.D. 60. But his statements to the Philippians about his possibly imminent death (e.g., 1:20) would be puzzling if coming from Caesarea, since he would have been able to request a trial in Rome, as in fact he did. Ephesus was close enough to Philippi for Paul to receive regular news from there, but there is no mention of his ever being imprisoned in Ephesus. On balance, **it seems most likely that the letter was written from Rome, c. A.D. 62.** This also fits most naturally with the mention of the praetorium and 'Caesar's household' (1:13 and 4:22)."* (emphasis mine)

These two different resources are closely aligned. If we desired, we could compare several other resources to validate. But even with two, we have a reasonable ability to answer our initial questions.

- **When did they write this?** *Paul wrote his letter to the Philippians in roughly 62 A.D., around eleven years after visiting Philippi and planting the Christian church there.*
- **Where did they live?** *Although Paul was from Damascus, he most likely wrote this letter from Rome while in prison awaiting trial.*

So why is this information relevant?

Well, it's interesting to note that Paul has already known his audience for a long time. And it's helpful to remember that he's writing from prison. These facts contextualize his statements in Philippians 3:12-21 (the section we entitled "Perseverance in the Lord" in our outline). In 3:17, when Paul urges his audience to *"join in following my example"* of perseverance, we know what example he means. He has spent more than a decade modeling patient, joyful sacrifice. And he does so now from prison. That kind of longsuffering for the sake of the gospel is what Paul means by "my example." Knowing the time and location of writing clues us into Paul's authorial intent. And that, in turn, helps us more accurately apply this truth to ourselves.

Let's consider another example.

When studying the Bible, I find that *comparing different translations* helps me identify interesting, important, or challenging words. There's not always a noticeable difference between translations, but when there is one, it's probably worth conducting a brief word study. Let's look at the difference between two reliable translations of Philippians 3:15a.

| New American Standard Version | English Standard Version |
|---|---|
| *"Let us therefore, as many as are **perfect**, have this attitude..."* | *"Let those of us who are **mature** think this way..."* |

The words "perfect" and "mature" are pretty different from each other! So why have two relatively similar versions of the Bible used them both to translate the same idea? And what does that fact tell us about the meaning of this passage? To answer such questions, we can turn to a few types of resources. First, let's look at a commentary. *A Commentary Critical and Explanatory on the Whole Bible* by Jamieson, Fausset, and Brown is a well-known resource available for free online. Here's what it says for Philippians 3:15.

> *"'As many of us then, as are perfect,' that is, full grown (no longer 'babes') in the Christian life (Php 3:3, 'worshipping God in the Spirit, and having no confidence in the flesh'), 1 Co 2:6, fully established in the things of God. Here, by 'perfect' he means one fully fit for running [BENGEL]; knowing and complying with the laws of the course (2 Ti 2:5). Though 'perfect' in this sense, he was not yet 'made perfect' (Greek) in the sense intended in Php 3:12, namely, 'crowned with complete victory,' and having attained absolute perfection."*

Although written in a halting, referential style, this commentary is helpful. It gives us a few cross-references and helps us understand the idea in this passage. When Paul says "perfect," he's not referring to morally flawless people. He's using the term subjectively. He's referring to people who are grown up in their faith, who are fit to run the race. This idea sounds very close to the ESV's way of translating the word as "mature," doesn't it?

Another essential point of reference here is the often-overlooked footnotes in the New American Standard Bible. In cases where an alternate wording is reasonable, the footnotes will usually make a note of it. And so it is here. When you read Philippians 3:15, you'll notice a tiny footnote next to the word "perfect," like this.

> *"Let us therefore, as many as are <sup>1</sup>perfect..."*

When you follow the footnote into the margins, there is a brief, one-word explanation:

> <sup>1</sup> *Mature*

In other words, the NASB recognizes the difficulty of translating this word. While they've chosen "perfect" for the main text, "mature" is a reasonable alternative translation. We don't need to be thrown off by this term. By comparing a commentary and a footnote, we can quickly grasp what Paul intended with this verse.

So why is this information relevant?

By understanding Paul's intention, we avoid the potential misinterpretation that Christians can ever achieve moral perfection. Although some have cited this verse when making such claims, that's not the main idea in this passage. On the contrary, this phrase highlights the Apostle Paul's humility and transparency. After more than a decade of discipleship, he's still open with his friends: "I'm mature, but I'm not there yet. I have no reason to boast in anything except in Jesus Christ, my Lord."

And let's consider one more example.

Let's say we're studying Philippians 3:20, which says, *"For our citizenship is in heaven, from which also we eagerly wait for a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ."* Where do we go to answer the question "what other passages of Scripture clarify and/or illustrate this text?"

If we turn to *The Treasury of Scripture Knowledge*, we'll find this entry for Philippians 3:20:

> (20) **our.** ch. 1:18-21. Ps. 16:11, 17:15; 73:24-26. Pr. 15:24. Mat. 6:19-21; 19:21. Lu. 12:21, 32-34; 14:14. 2 Co. 4:18; 5:1, 8. Ep. 2:6, 19. Col. 1:5; 3:1-3. He. 10:34, 35. 1 Pe. 1:3, 4. **citizenship.** Gr. Is. 26:1, 2. Ga. 4:26. Ep. 2:19. He. 12:22. Re. 21:10-27. **from.** Ac. 1:11. 1 Th. 4:16. 2 Th. 1:7, 8. Re. 1:7. **we wait.** ch. 1:10. 1 Co. 1:7. 1 Th. 1:10. 2 Ti. 4:8. Tit. 2:13. He. 9:28. 2 Pe. 3:12-14.

This is a pretty dense wall of references, isn't it? But it gives us a lot to go on.

Notice how the verse is divided into sections by keyword. As we're studying, we can explore all these cross-references and see how they relate to the main passage. Let's say we're trying to understand Paul's reference to "citizenship in heaven." What does that mean? What other passages of Scripture clarify this for us?

The cross-reference to Ephesians 2 is especially helpful. Verses 18-20 say this,

> *"For through Him we both have access in one Spirit to the Father. So then you are no longer strangers and aliens, but you are fellow citizens with the saints, and are of God's household, having been built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Christ Jesus Himself being the cornerstone."*

Our citizenship in heaven is "through" Jesus Christ. Our inclusion into God's kingdom is because we are in Him; our relationship to Jesus is our passport, the documentation of our spiritual citizenship. And that citizenship binds us together as Christians. In this sense, earthly citizenship is different than the heavenly citizenship Paul's talking about here. We are united not by social or ethnic similarity but by our mutual relationship to Jesus Christ. Paul refers to this whole collective group as "God's household." As members of this household, the foundation of our fellowship is that which God revealed to "the apostles and prophets" -- the Bible. And the cornerstone that brings this all into alignment is Christ Jesus Himself.

So, back in Philippians, when Paul wrote that *"our citizenship is in heaven, from which we also eagerly wait for a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ,"* he's trying to direct our attention to two things: where we truly belong (heaven), and why we truly belong there (Jesus).

Using cross-reference tools to explain Scripture with other Scripture is one of the most powerful ways to dig into a concept or phrase. I encourage you to make this a regular part of your Bible study. You'll begin to see the development of theological themes across the entire canon of Scripture.

## Summary

As we take a curious, intellectually honest approach to the Scriptures, we can utilize interpretive tools to help us answer critical questions. We ought not to lean upon them too much, essentially outsourcing the hard work of exegesis. We want to interpret the Scripture confidently for ourselves. But we don't want to study in a vacuum, either. We ought to recognize the efforts of those who have gone before us and use their conclusions to help us form our own.

We want to be like the Christians in ancient Berea, of whom Paul said in Acts 17:11, *"Now these were more noble-minded than those in Thessalonica, for they received the word with great eagerness, examining the Scriptures daily to see whether these things were so."* In other words, we want to accept teachings that will genuinely benefit us. But we strive to be discerning. We strive to accept these teachings not because someone impressive has delivered them or because others accept them, but because they genuinely make sense.

I hope this chapter has encouraged you to find and utilize time-tested resources in your daily Bible study. In Appendix B, I will provide a brief bibliography of reliable tools. Wherever possible, I will provide links to free versions available online.
