# Bible Study Methodology

This project uses the Bible study methodology from *Hermeneutics and Exegesis for Laypeople* by Clayton Chancey. When conducting a Bible study, follow the framework below faithfully. Reference the `book/` directory for deeper reading on any topic.

## The Pyramid of Interpretation

Every study must build from the ground up through these four layers. Never skip a layer.

1. **The Doctrine of Inspiration** — Scripture is God-breathed (2 Tim 3:16-17). Affirm **Verbal Plenary Inspiration**: the precise wording of Scripture has been breathed out by God and bears God's authority. Scripture is both the Word of God and the words of God. Dual authorship (God + human author) means both are relevant. → `book/02-the-doctrine-of-inspiration.md`

2. **Hermeneutics** — The principles used to determine an interpretation. Use the **Literal Historical Grammatical Hermeneutic**: a commonsense reading, taking into account the historical setting and linguistic choices of the author to determine his originally intended meaning. → `book/03-hermeneutics.md`

3. **Exegesis** — The process used to determine an interpretation. Getting to authorial intent takes hard work. Extract meaning through context (literary, historical, grammatical) before considering theological significance. → `book/04-exegesis.md`

4. **Interpretation** — A judgment as to the meaning of a text. The culmination of sound doctrine, balanced hermeneutics, and rigorous exegesis. → `book/05-asking-the-text-questions.md`

## Key Principle

> How we get meaning out of the text depends on how God put meaning into the text.

God determined the meaning of Scripture when He inspired it. We extract that meaning in alignment with the circumstances and intentions of the human authors that God used to communicate His message.

## Step 1: Ask the Text Questions

Use these four primary questions with their subordinate questions as a checklist for every passage studied. The full reference list is in `book/09-appendix-a-ask-the-text-questions.md`.

### (1) What did this text mean to the human author?

Discover authorial intent by identifying relevant context about the human author.

- **(1A) Who were they?** — Who was the human author? What biographical information do we know?
- **(1B) When did they write this?** — What point in history? What was going on historically, politically, culturally?
- **(1C) Where did they live?** — What geographical context informed this passage?
- **(1D) What were they addressing?** — What topics, events, or circumstances led them to write?
- **(1E) What was in their Bible?** — Where does this text fall in progressive revelation? Which other books existed at that point?

### (2) What does this text teach me about God?

Observe how God reveals Himself through His Word. Embrace dual authorship.

- **(A) What is God saying or doing in this text?** — Identify God's action or communication.
- **(B) Why is God saying or doing this?** — Identify God's purpose and motivation.
- **(C) How is God saying or doing this?** — Providential or miraculous means? Direct speech, dream, angel, or prophet?
- **(D) Which of God's attributes are on display?** — What does this text reveal about who and what God is?

### (3) How should this text contribute to my theology?

Shift from meaning (authorial intent) to significance (broader theological implications).

- **(A) How does this text advance the metanarrative of Scripture?** — The overarching story of redemption. How does this text develop that story?
- **(B) How does this text point to Christ?** — Identify Christological connections (types, prophecies, fulfillments).
- **(C) What other passages clarify and/or illustrate this text?** — Use cross-references. Let Scripture interpret Scripture.
- **(D) Which areas of systematic theology relate?** — Bibliology, Theology Proper, Christology, Pneumatology, Angelology, Anthropology, Hamartiology, Soteriology, Ecclesiology, Eschatology.

### (4) How does this truth apply to me?

Reserve application until after exegesis. Derive application aligned with LHG hermeneutic.

- **(A) What is my relationship to the human author?**
- **(B) What is my relationship to the original audience?**
- **(C) What are the differences between my world and their world?**
- **(D) Is my relationship to God different than theirs?**
- **(E) Which of our modern sensibilities are at odds with this text? Which of them resonate with it? And why?**

Detailed examples for each question: `book/05-asking-the-text-questions.md`

## Step 2: Outline the Text

Outlining discovers the literary and grammatical context of a passage. → `book/06-outlining.md`

### Why Outline

- Outlining develops authorial intent
- Outlining discovers the structure of the book
- Outlining determines sections for concentrated study

### Two Critical Skills

- **Summary** — Condensing a lengthy, difficult, or complicated passage into a simple statement.
- **Synthesis** — Combining multiple sources, ideas, or passages to form a single argument.

### Find the Unit of the Text (by genre)

| Genre | Unit of Thought | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Epistolary** (Romans, Hebrews, Jude) | Sentence | Each complete sentence is a unit |
| **Narrative** (Judges, Matthew, Acts) | Scene | When the setting/time/location changes |
| **Poetic** (Psalms, Proverbs) | Strophe | Parallel statement-pairings |
| **Prophetic** (Isaiah, Ezekiel, Jeremiah) | Sermon/Oracle | Individual sermons, often marked by introductory formulas |
| **Apocalyptic** (Revelation) | Mixed — follows the genre it's drawing from | Epistolary → narrative → poetic → prophetic elements |

### Identify Logical Movement via Conjunctions

| Type | Function | Keywords |
|---|---|---|
| Coordinating | Equality between thoughts | and, or, nor, for, but, neither...nor, either...or |
| Adversative | Contrast | but, yet, except, however, nevertheless |
| Emphatic | Force | certainly, verily, truly, in fact |
| Inferential | Conclusion/relation | therefore, so, when, wherefore, for |
| Transitional | Link/continuation | and, moreover, then |
| Subordinating | Dependent clause | when, because, if, since, although |

### The 6-Step Outlining Technique

1. Read the book repetitively
2. Identify major divisions
3. Break the text down into units (using genre-appropriate unit)
4. Summarize each unit
5. Group the units by commonality (synthesis)
6. Repeat steps 4 and 5 until no longer possible

## Step 3: Use Exegetical Resources

Use external resources with intentionality. → `book/07-using-exegetical-resources.md`

### Three Requirements for Using Tools Well

1. **Normal reading comprehension** — Understand basic grammar and sentence structure
2. **Organized note-taking** — Keep lists of questions, answers, observations, and intuitions
3. **Interpretive freedom** — Form your own conclusions; don't outsource exegesis

### Categories of Tools

| Tool Type | Use When You Need... | Recommended |
|---|---|---|
| **Study Bible** | Quick intro info about a book (author, date, themes) | ESV Study Bible, The Open Bible |
| **Devotional Commentary** | Straightforward exposition and application | Matthew Henry's Commentary, NIV Application Commentary |
| **Exegetical Commentary** | Detailed analysis, multiple viewpoints, Greek/Hebrew | New International Commentary, Word Biblical Commentary |
| **Hybrid Commentary** | Quick "health check" on your interpretation | Expositor's Bible Commentary, Tyndale Commentaries |
| **Bible Dictionary** | Articles on names, places, texts, topics | Holman Illustrated Bible Dictionary, Anchor Yale Bible Dictionary |
| **Concordance** | Find every usage of a word across Scripture | Strong's Exhaustive Concordance, Young's Analytical Concordance |
| **Lexicon** | Greek/Hebrew word definitions | Abbott-Smith (Greek), BDB (Hebrew), BDAG, HALOT |
| **Cross-Reference** | Connect passages to other passages | Treasury of Scripture Knowledge, Thompson Chain Reference Bible |

Full resource list with links: `book/10-appendix-b-recommended-resources.md`

### Lexicon Warning

Never build an interpretation on lexicon definitions alone. **Context is king.** Just because a word *can* mean something doesn't mean it *does* mean that in every occurrence. Always let context determine the meaning of a word.

## Meaning vs. Significance vs. Application

These are distinct and must not be confused. → `book/04-exegesis.md`

**Interpretation** (A Thing):
- **Meaning** (Authorial) — determined by literary, historical, and grammatical context
- **Significance** (Theological) — biblical theology and systematic theology

**Application** (An Action):
- Personal
- Corporate

Anchor in authorial intent *first*, then identify theological significance, *then* derive application.

## Guardrails

### Avoid These Imbalanced Hermeneutics

1. **Moral/Allegorical** — An application-driven reading that prioritizes personal relevance over actual meaning. Don't rush to "what does this mean to me" before understanding what it meant to the author. → `book/03-hermeneutics.md`

2. **Anagogical** — A mystical/codical reading seeking secret patterns. Diction over context. Don't treat the Bible like a code to crack. → `book/03-hermeneutics.md`

3. **Theological** — A theology-driven reading imposing presuppositions onto the text. Derive theology *from* the text; don't impose it *on* the text. Avoid excessive typology and extreme eschatological speculation. → `book/03-hermeneutics.md`

*None of these are never the right way to read Scripture, and none are always the right way.* They each revolve around an important truth that is simply over-emphasized.

### Competing Views of Inspiration to Reject

- **Dictation** — God bypassed human authors entirely (ignores dual authorship)
- **Partial/Conceptual** — Human authors corrupted God's message (undermines authority)
- **Natural** — No divine element at all (removes authority entirely)

→ `book/02-the-doctrine-of-inspiration.md`

### General Principles

- Context is king — literary, historical, grammatical
- One passage can't mean two different things based on who's reading it
- We don't determine the meaning of Scripture; we discover it
- Getting to authorial intent takes hard work — but Scripture is clear and understandable
- Scripture is more trustworthy than personal experience (2 Peter 1:19-21)
- Let Scripture interpret Scripture before turning to external resources
- Use extrabiblical tools to help form your own conclusions, not to outsource interpretation
- Distinguish between what a text *meant* (to the original audience) and how it *applies* (to us today)
